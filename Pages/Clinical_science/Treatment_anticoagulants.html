Introduction to Anticoagulants

Historical Perspective

The discovery of anticoagulants revolutionized medicine in the 20th century. Heparin, the first clinically used anticoagulant, was isolated from dog liver in 1916 by Jay McLean and William Henry Howell. Warfarin, derived from spoiled sweet clover causing cattle bleeding in the 1920s, was introduced for human use in the 1950s after its mechanism was understood. The development of low-molecular-weight heparins (LMWHs) in the 1980s and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) in the 2000s marked major therapeutic advances.

Hemostasis Physiology

Normal hemostasis involves a delicate balance between procoagulant and anticoagulant pathways. The vascular endothelium maintains this balance by producing anticoagulants (thrombomodulin, heparan sulfate) and inhibitors (tissue factor pathway inhibitor, TFPI). When injury occurs, platelet adhesion (via vWF-GPIb interaction) and coagulation cascade activation lead to fibrin clot formation. The coagulation cascade consists of the intrinsic (contact activation) and extrinsic (tissue factor) pathways converging at factor Xa.

Pathophysiology of Thrombosis

Virchow's triad describes three factors predisposing to thrombosis: endothelial injury (e.g., atherosclerosis), stasis (e.g., atrial fibrillation, immobility), and hypercoagulability (e.g., factor V Leiden). Arterial thrombi are platelet-rich ("white clots") while venous thrombi are fibrin/red cell-rich ("red clots"). This distinction guides anticoagulant choice - antiplatelets for arterial thrombosis, anticoagulants for venous thrombosis.

Vitamin K Antagonists (VKAs)

Mechanism of Action

Warfarin, the prototypical VKA, inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), preventing recycling of oxidized vitamin K to its reduced active form. This impairs Î³-carboxylation of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) and anticoagulant proteins (C, S). The effect is delayed (2-3 days) due to existing factor stores. Warfarin's effect is monitored via INR (International Normalized Ratio), which primarily reflects factor VII activity early on (short half-life) and factors II, IX, X later.

Indications

VKAs remain first-line for:

  1. Mechanical heart valves (DOACs contraindicated due to risk of valve thrombosis)
  2. Antiphospholipid syndrome (especially triple-positive with arterial thrombosis)
  3. Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH)
  4. Patients with severe renal impairment (CrCl <15-30 mL/min) where DOACs are contraindicated

Pharmacokinetics

Warfarin is 99% protein-bound (mainly albumin), metabolized by CYP2C9 (major) and CYP3A4/CYP1A2 (minor). Genetic polymorphisms in CYP2C9 (poor metabolizers) and VKORC1 (target sensitivity) affect dosing. The half-life is 20-60 hours, allowing once-daily dosing. Onset is delayed, requiring bridging with heparin/LMWH when treating acute thrombosis.

Side Effects & Interactions

Major risks include bleeding (1-3%/year major bleeding) and skin necrosis (rare, due to transient hypercoagulability from protein C/S inhibition). Numerous interactions occur via:

  1. Enhanced effects: CYP2C9 inhibitors (amiodarone, fluconazole), vitamin K deficiency (antibiotics killing gut flora), malnutrition
  2. Reduced effects: CYP2C9 inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine), high vitamin K intake (leafy greens)

Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs/NOACs)

Classification & Mechanism

DOACs directly inhibit specific coagulation factors:

  1. Direct thrombin (IIa) inhibitors: Dabigatran
  2. Direct factor Xa inhibitors: Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban, Betrixaban

Unlike warfarin, they have predictable pharmacokinetics (fixed dosing), rapid onset (1-4 hours), and fewer interactions (no CYP450 metabolism for dabigatran). They don't require routine monitoring but have limited reversal options (idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors).

Indications

DOACs are approved for:

  1. Non-valvular atrial fibrillation (superior to warfarin for intracranial bleed risk)
  2. VTE treatment/prophylaxis (non-inferior to warfarin with less bleeding)
  3. Post-orthopedic thromboprophylaxis (rivaroxaban, apixaban)

Pharmacokinetics

DOACs have varying renal excretion: dabigatran (80%) > edoxaban (50%) > rivaroxaban (35%) > apixaban (25%). Apixaban has dual excretion (feces 55%, urine 25%). Most are P-gp substrates (avoid with strong P-gp inducers/inhibitors in renal impairment). Half-lives range from 5-17 hours (shorter than warfarin), requiring BID dosing for some.

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages over warfarin include no dietary restrictions, fewer interactions, and predictable effects. Limitations include:

  1. Contraindicated in severe renal impairment (except apixaban to CrCl 15 mL/min)
  2. No proven efficacy in mechanical valves/antiphospholipid syndrome
  3. Higher cost and lack of reliable monitoring assays

Heparins & Low-Molecular-Weight Heparins (LMWHs)

Mechanism of Action

Unfractionated heparin (UFH) binds antithrombin (AT), accelerating its inhibition of thrombin (IIa) and Xa (1:1 ratio). LMWHs (enoxaparin, dalteparin) preferentially inhibit Xa over IIa (2-4:1 ratio) due to shorter polysaccharide chains. UFH requires monitoring via aPTT, while LMWHs have predictable dosing (weight-based).

Indications

UFH is used when rapid reversal may be needed (e.g., cardiac surgery, PCI) or in renal failure (UFH renally cleared). LMWHs are preferred for:

  1. VTE treatment/prophylaxis (superior to UFH for DVT prevention)
  2. Cancer-associated thrombosis (better than warfarin)
  3. Pregnancy (doesn't cross placenta)

Pharmacokinetics

UFH has zero-order kinetics (saturable cellular binding), short half-life (1-2h), and is reversed by protamine. LMWHs have first-order kinetics, longer half-life (4-7h), and are only partially reversed by protamine. Both are contraindicated in HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia).

Side Effects

Major risks include:

  1. HIT (Type II: immune-mediated, 5% with UFH, <1% LMWH)
  2. Bleeding (less with LMWH than UFH)
  3. Osteoporosis (long-term use)
  4. Hyperkalemia (via aldosterone suppression)
Mnemonic: "HEPARIN" for UFH vs LMWH
H - Half-life longer (LMWH)
E - Efficacy similar
P - Protamine reverses UFH fully
A - aPTT monitors UFH
R - Renal clearance (both)
I - Injections (both SC/IV)
N - No monitoring usually for LMWH

Other Antithrombotic Agents

Antiplatelet Agents

Used primarily for arterial thrombosis (platelet-rich):

  1. Aspirin: Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 â reduced TXA2 (platelet aggregation)
  2. P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel): Block ADP receptors
  3. GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors (abciximab): Final common pathway blockade (IV use in PCI)
  4. PAR-1 antagonists (vorapaxar): Thrombin receptor inhibition

Statins

Beyond lipid-lowering, statins have pleiotropic antithrombotic effects:

  1. Upregulate endothelial NO synthase (vasodilation)
  2. Reduce tissue factor expression
  3. Stabilize atherosclerotic plaques
  4. Modulate platelet reactivity

These effects contribute to reduced cardiovascular events independent of LDL lowering.

Novel Agents

Emerging anticoagulants include:

  1. FXI inhibitors (abelacimab): Target contact activation pathway
  2. RNA interference (fitusiran): Reduces antithrombin production
  3. Factor XII inhibitors: May provide thrombosis prevention without bleeding risk
