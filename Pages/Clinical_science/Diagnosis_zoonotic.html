Introduction to Zoonotic Infections

Zoonotic infections are diseases transmitted from animals to humans, either directly (e.g., bites, scratches) or indirectly (e.g., vectors like ticks or fleas). These pathogens exploit interspecies interactions, often causing unique clinical syndromes due to their evolutionary adaptations.

Key Terminology

Vector: An organism (e.g., tick, mosquito) that transmits pathogens. Reservoir: An animal species that harbors the pathogen without severe illness. Definitive host: Where the pathogen reaches maturity (e.g., malaria in mosquitoes).

Relevant Anatomy & Physiology

Transmission depends on:

  1. Skin barrier breaches (bites, scratches)
  2. Mucosal exposure (inhalation of aerosols, e.g., hantavirus from rodent droppings)
  3. Vector feeding mechanisms (e.g., tick saliva inhibits host immune responses)

Historical Context

The Black Death (Yersinia pestis, transmitted by fleas) highlighted zoonotic potential. Modern examples include Lyme disease (1975) and COVID-19 (likely zoonotic origin).

Pathophysiology

Animal-borne pathogens often evade human immune defenses by:

  1. Antigenic variation (e.g., Borrelia burgdorferi)
  2. Intracellular survival (e.g., Bartonella in erythrocytes)
  3. Toxin production (e.g., Pasteurella multocida from cat bites)

Epidemiology

~60% of human infectious diseases are zoonotic. Tick-borne diseases dominate in temperate regions, while rabies remains endemic in low-resource areas.

1. Lyme Disease (Borrelia burgdorferi)

Aetiology

Borrelia burgdorferi, a spirochete transmitted by Ixodes ticks. Reservoirs include white-footed mice and deer.

Pathophysiology

Spirochetes disseminate via bloodstream after tick attachment (>36 hours). They evade immunity through surface protein variation (VlsE) and trigger inflammatory responses in joints/nerves.

Presentation

  1. Stage 1 (Early localized): Erythema migrans (70-80% of cases), flu-like symptoms.
  2. Stage 2 (Early disseminated): Multiple erythema migrans, facial nerve palsy, carditis (AV block).
  3. Stage 3 (Late): Lyme arthritis (knee swelling), encephalopathy.

Diagnosis

Two-tier testing: ELISA followed by Western blot. PCR for synovial fluid in arthritis.

Therapy

Doxycycline (first-line for adults), amoxicillin for children/pregnant women. IV ceftriaxone for neurologic/cardiac involvement.

Mnemonic: BORRELIA - Bull's-eye rash, Over 36-hour attachment, Reservoir is rodents, Relapsing fever possible, Erythema migrans, Lyme arthritis late, Ixodes vector, AV block in carditis

2. Cat-Scratch Disease (Bartonella henselae)

Aetiology

Bartonella henselae, a gram-negative bacterium transmitted via cat scratches/bites or flea feces. Kittens are common reservoirs.

Pathophysiology

Bacteria infect erythrocytes and endothelial cells, causing vascular proliferation and granuloma formation via angiogenic factors (e.g., VEGF stimulation).

Presentation

Classic triad:

  1. Regional lymphadenopathy (tender, suppurative)
  2. Fever
  3. History of cat exposure

Atypical forms: Parinaud oculoglandular syndrome, hepatosplenic granulomas, encephalitis.

Diagnosis

Serology (IgG titers ≥1:256) or PCR. Warthin-Starry stain shows bacilli in tissue.

Therapy

Typically self-limited. Azithromycin for severe cases. Doxycycline + rifampin for hepatosplenic/neurologic disease.

Mnemonic: CAT SCRATCH FEVER - Cervical nodes, Azithromycin option, Tender lymph nodes, Silver stain positive, Cat exposure, Rodents transmit fleas, Angiogenic lesions, Three-week incubation, Children commonly affected, Histology shows granulomas, Fever common, Erythrocyte infection, VEGF stimulation, Enlarged spleen possible, Rare encephalitis

3. Rabies (Lyssavirus)

Aetiology

Rabies virus (Rhabdoviridae family), transmitted via animal bites (dogs, bats, raccoons). Nearly 100% fatal once symptomatic.

Pathophysiology

Virus enters peripheral nerves, travels retrograde to CNS via axoplasmic transport. Causes neuronal apoptosis and Negri bodies (cytoplasmic inclusions).

Presentation

Prodrome: Fever, paresthesia at bite site.

Acute neurologic phase:

  1. Furious rabies (80%): Hydrophobia, aerophobia, hyperactivity
  2. Paralytic rabies (20%): Flaccid paralysis mimicking Guillain-Barré

Diagnosis

Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) test on skin biopsy or brain tissue. PCR of saliva/CSF.

Therapy

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): Wound cleaning + rabies immunoglobulin + vaccine series. No effective treatment once symptomatic.

Mnemonic: RABIES - Retrograde axonal transport, Animal bite, Bite site paresthesia, Inclusion bodies (Negri), Encephalitis fatal, Saliva transmission

4. Leptospirosis (Leptospira interrogans)

Aetiology

Leptospira interrogans, a spirochete transmitted via contact with rodent urine-contaminated water. Common in tropical areas.

Pathophysiology

Spirochetes penetrate mucous membranes/abraded skin → hematogenous spread → endothelial damage → vasculitis + multi-organ failure.

Presentation

Biphasic illness:

  1. Acute septicemic phase: High fever, conjunctival suffusion, myalgia (calf pain), headache
  2. Immune phase: Weil's syndrome (jaundice, AKI, hemorrhage)

Diagnosis

Microscopic agglutination test (MAT) or PCR. Dark-field microscopy of blood (early phase) shows motile spirochetes.

Therapy

Doxycycline (mild) or IV penicillin/ceftriaxone (severe). Supportive care for renal/hepatic failure.

Mnemonic: LEAPTO - Liver/kidney failure, Endothelial damage, Acute febrile illness, Penetrates skin/mucosa, Transmitted via rodent urine, Ocular suffusion

5. Capnocytophaga Infection (Dog/Cat Bites)

Aetiology

Capnocytophaga canimorsus, a gram-negative rod in dog/cat oral flora. Asplenic patients are at highest risk for sepsis.

Pathophysiology

Bacteria evade phagocytosis via polysaccharide capsule. In asplenic hosts, rapid progression to DIC and purpura fulminans occurs due to impaired IgM production.

Presentation

  1. Early: Cellulitis, lymphangitis at bite site.

  2. Late: Fever, septic shock, ecchymotic rash, meningitis (mimicking meningococcemia).

Diagnosis

Blood culture (requires prolonged incubation). Gram stain shows fusiform gram-negative rods.

Therapy

IV beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor (e.g., ampicillin-sulbactam). Add clindamycin for toxin suppression in shock.

Mnemonic: DOG BITE - DIC risk, Oral flora pathogen, Gram-negative rods, Beta-lactamase production, Immunocompromised vulnerable, Toxin-mediated shock, Ecchymosis in sepsis

6. Rodent-Borne Infections

Overview

Rodents are reservoirs for numerous pathogens transmitted via direct contact (urine/feces), aerosols, or arthropod vectors (fleas, ticks). Key syndromes range from hemorrhagic fevers to neuroinvasive disease.

Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS)

Aetiology: Sin Nombre virus (Americas), transmitted via inhalation of deer mouse excreta.

Pathophysiology: Virus targets pulmonary endothelium → capillary leak → non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema. CD8+ T-cell response exacerbates injury.

Presentation:

  1. Prodrome: Fever, myalgia, headache (3-5 days)
  2. Cardiopulmonary phase: Rapid-onset ARDS, hypoxia, hypotension

Diagnosis: IgM ELISA, PCR (blood/tissue). Thrombocytopenia + left shift are early clues.

Therapy: Supportive care (mechanical ventilation often required). Ribavirin efficacy unclear.

Mnemonic: HANTAVIRUS - Hemoconcentration, Acute respiratory failure, New World mice, Thrombocytopenia, ARDS, Vascular leak, IgM ELISA diagnostic, Rodent exposure, Urban cases rare, Supportive care only

Lymphocytic Choriomeningitis Virus (LCMV)

Aetiology: Arenavirus transmitted via house mice (urine, droppings).

Presentation:

  1. Febrile phase: Fever, malaise, photophobia
  2. Neurologic phase: Aseptic meningitis (CSF shows lymphocytosis + low glucose)

Diagnosis: CSF PCR or IgM serology.

Therapy: Supportive; ribavirin may be used in immunocompromised.

Mnemonic: LCMV - Low CSF glucose, Chorio-meningitis, Mice reservoir, Viral PCR in CSF

Plague (Yersinia pestis)

Rodent link: Rats (reservoir) → fleas (vector) → humans. Endemic in SW USA.

Forms:

  1. Bubonic: Painful lymphadenopathy (buboes), fever
  2. Pneumonic: Hemoptysis, rapid progression (person-to-person transmission)
  3. Septicemic: DIC, acral necrosis ("black death")

Diagnosis: Gram stain (bipolar "safety pin" rods), culture, PCR.

Therapy: Gentamicin or doxycycline. Post-exposure prophylaxis for contacts.

Mnemonic: PLAGUE - Painful buboes, Lymphadenopathy, Acral necrosis, Gentamicin treatment, Urban rats, Endemic in SW USA

Prevention of Animal-Borne Infections

Primary Prevention

Primary prevention consists of measures aimed at a susceptible population or individual. The purpose of primary prevention is to prevent a disease from ever occurring. Thus, its target population is healthy individuals. It commonly institutes activities that limit risk exposure or increase the immunity of individuals at risk to prevent a disease from progressing in a susceptible individual to subclinical disease. For example, immunizations are a form of primary prevention.

Avoid exposure:

  1. Tick prevention: DEET, permethrin-treated clothing, tick checks
  2. Animal bites: Avoid provoking animals, supervise children around pets
  3. Rodent control: Seal food, eliminate standing water (leptospirosis)

Avoid pathogenesis

  1. Vaccination: Pre-exposure rabies vaccine for veterinarians
  2. Prophylaxis: Doxycycline for leptospirosis in endemic areas
  3. Education: Teach high-risk populations about early symptoms

Secondary Prevention

Secondary prevention emphasizes early disease detection, and its target is healthy-appearing individuals with subclinical forms of the disease. The subclinical disease consists of pathologic changes but no overt symptoms that are diagnosable in a doctor's visit. Secondary prevention often occurs in the form of screenings. For example, a Papanicolaou (Pap) smear is a form of secondary prevention aimed to diagnose cervical cancer in its subclinical state before progression.

Post-exposure measures:

  1. Rabies PEP: Immunoglobulin + vaccine series
  2. Lyme disease: Single-dose doxycycline for high-risk tick bites
  3. Wound care: Irrigation, debridement for animal bites

Tertiary Prevention

Tertiary prevention targets both the clinical and outcome stages of a disease. It is implemented in symptomatic patients and aims to reduce the severity of the disease as well as any associated sequelae. While secondary prevention seeks to prevent the onset of illness, tertiary prevention aims to reduce the effects of the disease once established in an individual. Forms of tertiary prevention are commonly rehabilitation efforts.

Quaternary Prevention

According to the Wonca International Dictionary for General/Family Practice, quaternary prevention is "action taken to identify patients at risk of overmedicalization, to protect him from new medical invasion, and to suggest to him interventions, which are ethically acceptable." Marc Jamoulle initially proposed this concept, and the targets were mainly patients with illness but without the disease. The definition has undergone recent modification as "an action taken to protect individuals (persons/patients) from medical interventions that are likely to cause more harm than good."
