Introduction to the Thyroid Gland

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped organ located in the anterior neck, inferior to the larynx and overlying the trachea. It plays a crucial role in metabolic regulation through the production of thyroid hormones: thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3).

These hormones influence nearly every tissue in the body, regulating basal metabolic rate, heat production, and affecting cardiovascular, neuromuscular, and gastrointestinal systems. The thyroid is under control of the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis.

The history of thyroid disease treatment spans from crude surgical approaches in antiquity to the fine-tuned biochemical and radiological diagnostics and treatments we use today.

Understanding Hypothyroidism

Hypothyroidism refers to the clinical state resulting from insufficient thyroid hormone production. Symptoms include fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation, and bradycardia. In severe cases, it leads to myxedema, a life-threatening form of hypothyroidism.

Thyroid hormone deficiency reduces cellular metabolism, leading to systemic slowing down of physiological processes. Understanding this helps interpret lab tests (e.g., elevated TSH with low free T4) and guides replacement therapy.

Common Causes of Hypothyroidism

1. Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

An autoimmune disease where anti-thyroid peroxidase (TPO) and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies attack thyroid tissue, leading to fibrosis and atrophy. It presents with painless goiter and typical hypothyroid symptoms. Diagnosed with positive antibodies and ultrasound, treated with levothyroxine.

2. Post-ablative Hypothyroidism

Occurs after radioactive iodine therapy or thyroidectomy, typically in the context of Graves' disease or thyroid cancer. Requires lifelong hormone replacement.

3. Iodine Deficiency

Still prevalent in certain regions, leading to goiter formation and hypothyroidism. Diagnosed by history and urinary iodine levels. Managed with dietary iodine or supplementation.

4. Congenital Hypothyroidism

Due to thyroid dysgenesis or dyshormonogenesis. Critical to diagnose early via newborn screening to prevent cretinism.

5. Secondary Hypothyroidism

Due to pituitary or hypothalamic failure (low TSH with low T4). Often accompanied by other pituitary hormone deficiencies.

Introduction to Hyperthyroidism

Hyperthyroidism refers to the clinical state resulting from elevated levels of circulating thyroid hormones. It leads to a hypermetabolic state characterized by symptoms such as weight loss, heat intolerance, tremors, palpitations, and anxiety. Reflexes are brisk, and there may be proximal muscle weakness.

The physiological basis lies in the overactivation of cellular metabolism via increased mitochondrial activity, Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase activity, and enhanced catecholamine sensitivity.

1. Graves' Disease

This is an autoimmune disorder where thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSIs) bind to the TSH receptor, causing autonomous thyroid hormone production. It's the most common cause of hyperthyroidism in young adults and is associated with ophthalmopathy and pretibial myxedema.

Diagnosis is confirmed by low TSH, high T4/T3, and positive TSI. Radioactive iodine uptake is diffusely elevated. Treatment includes antithyroid drugs, radioactive iodine ablation, or surgery.

Mnemonic: Think "GRAVES = Glaring eyes, Raised metabolism, Autoimmune, Very high uptake, Exophthalmos, Stimulatory antibodies"

2. Toxic Multinodular Goiter

This condition involves multiple autonomously functioning thyroid nodules producing thyroid hormones independent of TSH regulation. It generally affects older adults and lacks ophthalmopathy.

Diagnosis includes heterogeneous uptake on radioactive scan. Treatment includes antithyroid medication and, if needed, surgery or ablation.

3. Toxic Adenoma

A single autonomously functioning nodule that secretes thyroid hormone, often seen in younger patients. Diagnosed by a "hot nodule" on scan, with suppressed uptake in surrounding tissue.

Treated by radioactive iodine or surgical excision.

4. Subacute (De Quervain) Thyroiditis

This post-viral thyroiditis causes transient hyperthyroidism due to inflammatory destruction of follicular cells. It presents with painful thyroid and raised ESR. Thyroid function typically normalizes after a brief hypothyroid phase.

Question 8

5. Factitious Thyrotoxicosis

This results from intentional or accidental ingestion of thyroid hormone. There is low TSH, high T4, but low thyroglobulin. There is no uptake on scan, distinguishing it from endogenous causes.

Parathyroid Dysfunction

The parathyroid glands regulate calcium and phosphate homeostasis via parathyroid hormone (PTH). PTH increases serum calcium through bone resorption, renal reabsorption, and increased vitamin D activation.

Hyperparathyroidism typically results in hypercalcemia, "bones, stones, groans, and psychiatric overtones." Causes include adenoma, hyperplasia, or carcinoma.

Hypoparathyroidism leads to hypocalcemia with tetany, Chvostek and Trousseau signs. It can follow thyroidectomy or be autoimmune.

Mnemonic: Hyper = "High Cal = Crazy Bones", Hypo = "Low Cal = Spasm Hell"
