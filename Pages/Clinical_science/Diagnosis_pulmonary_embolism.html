Introduction

Essentially, a pulmonary embolism is an arterial blockage in the lungs that has moved from somewhere else in the body. Usually, it originates from the legs.

Pathophysiology

Roughly 90% of pulmonary emboli are resultant from a proximal deep vein thrombosis (i.e., located from the knee and up). In young men without any other health conditions, however, one must suspect a venous thoracic outlet syndrome which is rare.

DVT, and thus VTE, is much more common in immunocompromised, those undergoing orthopaedic surgery at or below the hip (because of immobility and/or venous damage), pancreatic and colon cancer patients (increased release of pro-coagulants), patients with high-grade tumours, pregnant women (hypercoagulable state to decrease risk of hemorrhage during childbirth), those on estrogen medication. Other risk factors are varicose veins, pulmonary hypertension, diabetes mellitus, traumatic hip fractures that immobilise the patient and joint fixation. In general, however, a search for secondary causes is only briefly conducted. Only when a second event happens, under anticoagulants, is a thorough search conducted.

Pathophysiologically, the clot originates in the venous system, which then travels through the vena cava inferior, after which it enters the right atrium, passes the tricuspid valve, then passes the right ventricle, which then passes the pulmonary valve entering the pulmonary artery where it ends up getting stuck.

Symptomatology

DVT

A DVT may present with a leg that is red, warm, swollen and painful, which is then followed by a PE.

PE

The onset typically, is sudden, and my include the following: dyspnea, tachypnea, chest pain of pleuritic nature (i.e., worse on breathing), coughing and hemoptysis. When severity increases, signs such as cyanosis, collapse and circulatory instability. In the end, sudden death may follow. Roughly 15% of all sudden death cases are attributable to PE. Syncope, however, is rare, as less than 1% of syncope cases are attributable to PE.

On physical examination then, one might find a pleural frction rub over the affected area, especially when infarcted. The affected area then also shows decreased percussion note, audible breath sounds and vocal resonance. Strain on the right ventricle may be detected as a left parasternal heave, loud pulmonary component of the second heart sound or raised jugular veinous pressure. If it is a large hemorrhage or infarction, a low-grade fever may be present.

To some extent, it is possible to determine the size of the embolus and thus the region of infarction based on the symptomatology.

Smaller emboli

They tend to lodge in the more peripheral areas without collateral circulation, which then carries a higher risk for infarction. This then causes significant pain, but not necessarily hypoxia, dyspnea and/or haemodynamic instability.

Larger emboli

They tend to lodge centrally which does not cause infarction as there is collateral circulation. Because of this, they're often painless. However, they typically do cause dyspnea, hypoxia, low blood pressure, tachycardia, and fainting.

Diagnosis

The preferred method of diagnosis is a CT pulmonary angiogram because it's easy and accurate. One could also use probability testing, which is usally done by the Wells score:

  1. Clinically suspect DVT -> 3 points
  2. Alternative diagnosis less likely -> 3 points
  3. Tachycardia -> 1.5 points
  4. Immobilisation or surgery in last month -> 1.5 points
  5. History of DVT or PE -> 1.5 points
  6. Hemoptysis -> 1 point
  7. Malignancy -> 1 point

This is then interpreted using the cutoff of 4: above 4 PE is likely and one should use diagnostic imaging while below it, one can rely on the D-dimer rule.

D-dimer then, a fibrin degredation product, is highly sensitive but not specific (~50%) which means that it may be used to exclude PE when negative but not diagnose PE when positive. Troponin also is increased up to 50% in PE.

Alternative imagine includes a ventilation/perfusion scan or lung scintigraphy, which is used in those allergic to iodinated contrasted or when pregnant. Chest X-rays are often done on those with dyspnea and are useful for CHF or rib fractures but often don't show sufficient signs to diagnose PE. Ultrasonography may be useful to indicated anticoagulants, especially in the pregnant.

An ECG is useful to exclude other origins, and may show right heart straight or acute cor pulmonale in case of large PE (large S wave in lead I, large Q in III, and inverted T in lead III). Any case then, with an inverted T in V1-3 is suspected of PE or inferior MI.

Treatment

The basis of treatment of PE is anticoagulant therapy with supportive treatments such as oxygen or analgesia. The main anticoagulant used to be vitamin K antagonists (Warfarin etc.), however as they act very slowly, current treatment is started by injecting heparin or LMWH. Even DOACs are now used as replacement of Vitamin K antagonists as those don't require titration to the INR. Therapy is usally continued for 3-6 months or lifelong if there is previous DVTs or PEs or if there is non-transient risk factors.

If the PE causes haemodynamic instability, i.e., systolic pressure under 90 or a drop of 40 mmHG for more than 15 minutes, then thrombolysis should be employed. This entails the enzymatic destruction of the clot with medication. One could, however, also use a catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT) performed by an interventional radiologist.

If anticoagulant therapy is contraindicated or ineffective, an inferior vena cava filter could be amployed.

Prognosis

Fatality rate is low: less than 5-10% percent of symptomatic PE lead to death within the hour.
