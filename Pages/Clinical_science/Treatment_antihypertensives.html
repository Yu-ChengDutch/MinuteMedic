Introduction to Antihypertensives and Diuretics

Hypertension, defined as a sustained blood pressure ≥140/90 mmHg, is one of the most prevalent modifiable risk factors for cardiovascular disease worldwide. The regulation of blood pressure is a complex interplay between cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance, governed by the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), sympathetic nervous system, and renal sodium handling.

Historical Perspective

The treatment of hypertension has evolved remarkably since the 1950s when the first effective antihypertensives (ganglionic blockers) were introduced. The development of thiazide diuretics in the late 1950s marked a turning point, proving that blood pressure control could prevent cardiovascular complications. The subsequent introduction of beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, and RAAS inhibitors has transformed hypertension management.

Renal Physiology and Blood Pressure Regulation

The kidneys play a pivotal role in long-term blood pressure regulation through:

  1. Pressure natriuresis: Increased arterial pressure leads to enhanced sodium and water excretion
  2. RAAS activation: Decreased renal perfusion pressure stimulates renin release from juxtaglomerular cells
  3. Tubuloglomerular feedback: Macula densa cells sense NaCl delivery to the distal tubule

Vascular Physiology

Vascular smooth muscle tone is primarily regulated by:

  1. Calcium-dependent contraction: Via L-type calcium channels
  2. Sympathetic input: α1-adrenergic receptors mediate vasoconstriction
  3. Endothelial factors: Nitric oxide (vasodilation) and endothelin (vasoconstriction)

RAAS Inhibitors: ACE Inhibitors and ARBs

Mechanism of Action

ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril) block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II by inhibiting angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), while ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan) competitively block the AT1 receptor. This results in:

  1. Reduced vasoconstriction
  2. Decreased aldosterone secretion (reducing sodium retention)
  3. Increased bradykinin levels (ACE inhibitors only)

Indications

RAAS inhibitors are first-line for:

  1. Hypertension (especially in diabetes or proteinuria)
  2. Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
  3. Post-myocardial infarction
  4. Diabetic nephropathy

Side Effects

ACE inhibitors may cause:

  1. Dry cough (bradykinin-mediated, in 5-20% of patients)
  2. Angioedema (rare but potentially life-threatening)
  3. Hyperkalemia (due to reduced aldosterone)
  4. Teratogenicity (contraindicated in pregnancy)

ARBs generally have better tolerability with lower cough risk.

Pharmacokinetics

Most ACE inhibitors (except lisinopril and captopril) are prodrugs requiring hepatic activation. ARBs are primarily metabolized by CYP2C9. Dosage adjustment is needed in renal impairment.

Calcium Channel Blockers

Classification

Calcium channel blockers (CCBs) are divided into:

  1. Dihydropyridines (nifedipine, amlodipine): Predominantly vascular selective
  2. Non-dihydropyridines (verapamil, diltiazem): Cardiac and vascular effects

Mechanism of Action

CCBs block L-type calcium channels, leading to:

  1. Vascular smooth muscle relaxation (reduced systemic vascular resistance)
  2. Cardiac effects (non-DHPs only): Negative inotropy and chronotropy

Clinical Uses

Dihydropyridines are preferred for hypertension, while non-DHPs are used for:

  1. Rate control in atrial fibrillation
  2. Vasospastic angina
  3. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (verapamil)

Adverse Effects

Dihydropyridines may cause:

  1. Peripheral edema (dose-dependent)
  2. Reflex tachycardia (short-acting formulations)
  3. Gingival hyperplasia

Non-DHPs can cause:

  1. Bradycardia
  2. Constipation (especially verapamil)
  3. Heart block (contraindicated in 2nd/3rd degree AV block)
Mnemonic: "DHP vs Non-DHP" - Remember the key differences:
Dihydropyridines = Dilate vessels

Beta-Adrenergic Blockers

Classification

Beta-blockers are categorized by:

  1. Cardioselectivity (β1 preference): Metoprolol, atenolol
  2. Intrinsic sympathomimetic activity: Pindolol, acebutolol
  3. Additional α-blockade: Carvedilol, labetalol

Mechanisms

Beta-blockers reduce blood pressure through:

  1. Decreased cardiac output (reduced heart rate and contractility)
  2. Reduced renin release (β1 effect on juxtaglomerular cells)
  3. Central sympathetic outflow reduction

Clinical Uses

Indications include:

  1. Hypertension (though no longer first-line for uncomplicated cases)
  2. Coronary artery disease
  3. Heart failure (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol)
  4. Arrhythmias

Adverse Effects

Common issues include:

  1. Fatigue and exercise intolerance
  2. Bronchoconstriction (non-selective agents)
  3. Masking of hypoglycemia symptoms
  4. Erectile dysfunction

Diuretics: Thiazides and Thiazide-like Agents

Mechanism of Action

Thiazides (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide) and thiazide-like diuretics (e.g., chlorthalidone, indapamide) inhibit the Na+-Cl- symporter in the distal convoluted tubule, leading to:

  1. Increased sodium and water excretion
  2. Initial plasma volume reduction
  3. Long-term reduction in peripheral vascular resistance (mechanism unclear)

Clinical Uses

Thiazides are first-line for uncomplicated hypertension, especially in:

  1. Black patients (per JNC guidelines)
  2. Elderly patients
  3. Patients with osteoporosis (calcium-sparing effect)

Adverse Effects

Important side effects include:

  1. Hypokalemia (due to increased distal Na+ delivery)
  2. Hyperuricemia (may precipitate gout)
  3. Hypercalcemia (reduced calcium excretion)
  4. Hyperglycemia (insulin resistance)

Loop Diuretics

Mechanism of Action

Loop diuretics (e.g., furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) inhibit the Na+-K+-2Cl- symporter in the thick ascending limb of Henle, resulting in:

  1. Profound natriuresis and diuresis (15-25% of filtered Na+ excreted)
  2. Reduced medullary interstitial tonicity (impairs urinary concentrating ability)
  3. Increased calcium and magnesium excretion

Clinical Uses

Primary indications include:

  1. Edematous states (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome)
  2. Acute pulmonary edema
  3. Hypercalcemia
  4. Resistant hypertension (in combination with thiazides)

Adverse Effects

Important complications:

  1. Hypokalemia (more severe than with thiazides)
  2. Ototoxicity (especially with rapid IV administration)
  3. Hyperuricemia
  4. Hypovolemia and prerenal azotemia
Mnemonic: "LOOP" - Key features:
Lasts short (short half-life)
Ototoxicity risk
Overcomes thiazide resistance
Potassium wasting

Potassium-Sparing Diuretics

Classification

Potassium-sparing diuretics include:

  1. Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone)
  2. Direct ENaC inhibitors (amiloride, triamterene)

Mechanisms

These agents act on the collecting duct:

  1. Aldosterone antagonists block mineralocorticoid receptors
  2. ENaC inhibitors block the epithelial sodium channel
  3. Both reduce potassium excretion by decreasing lumen-negative potential

Clinical Uses

Important indications:

  1. Heart failure (especially spironolactone in reduced EF)
  2. Primary hyperaldosteronism
  3. Adjuvant in thiazide/loop diuretic therapy to prevent hypokalemia
  4. Resistant hypertension

Adverse Effects

Key concerns:

  1. Hyperkalemia (especially with renal impairment or ACEI/ARB use)
  2. Gynecomastia (spironolactone, due to antiandrogen effects)
  3. Metabolic acidosis (amiloride/triamterene)
