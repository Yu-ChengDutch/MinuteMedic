Introduction to Limb Fractures

The human musculoskeletal system is a marvel of biological engineering, with bones providing structural support and protection while enabling mobility through articulated joints. Limb fractures represent one of the most common traumatic injuries encountered in clinical practice, with their management forming a cornerstone of orthopaedic surgery.

Historical Perspective

The treatment of fractures dates back to ancient civilizations. The Edwin Smith Papyrus (circa 1600 BCE) contains the earliest documented descriptions of fracture management, while Hippocrates (460-370 BCE) established fundamental principles of traction and immobilization that remained largely unchanged until the 19th century. The development of radiographs by Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895 revolutionized fracture diagnosis, and the mid-20th century saw the advent of internal fixation techniques that transformed outcomes.

Anatomical Considerations

Understanding limb fractures requires thorough knowledge of osteology and regional anatomy. Long bones consist of:

  1. Diaphysis: The shaft containing cortical bone
  2. Metaphysis: The flared transitional region
  3. Epiphysis: The articular ends composed mainly of cancellous bone

The periosteum plays crucial roles in fracture healing, containing osteoprogenitor cells in its inner cambium layer. Blood supply varies by bone segment, with the metaphysis being highly vascular compared to watershed areas prone to avascular necrosis (e.g., femoral head).

Physiology of Bone Healing

Fracture healing occurs through three overlapping phases:

  1. Inflammatory phase (days 1-7): Hematoma formation and cytokine release
  2. Reparative phase (weeks 1-12): Callus formation through endochondral ossification
  3. Remodeling phase (months to years): Wolff's law-driven cortical reconstruction

Healing may be primary (direct cortical bridging with rigid fixation) or secondary (callus formation with relative stability). Numerous factors influence healing including age, comorbidities, fracture pattern, and mechanical environment.

Mnemonic: "Fracture Healing CRASH Course"
C - Cytokines initiate healing
R - Repair cells recruited
A - Angiogenesis occurs
S - Soft callus forms
H - Hard callus remodels

Upper Limb Fractures: Shoulder Region

Proximal Humerus Fractures

These commonly occur in osteoporotic elderly after falls onto an outstretched hand. The Neer classification (based on displacement of four segments: articular surface, greater tuberosity, lesser tuberosity, and shaft) guides management. The axillary nerve is particularly vulnerable as it courses around the surgical neck.

Clinical Features

Patients present with pain, swelling, and inability to abduct the arm. The "sail sign" on radiographs indicates displacement of the greater tuberosity. Neurovascular assessment must include evaluation of axillary nerve function (regimental badge area sensation) and radial nerve integrity.

Management

Non-displaced fractures (<1cm displacement, <45° angulation) are treated with sling immobilization and early pendulum exercises. Surgical fixation (ORIF or hemiarthroplasty) is indicated for displaced fractures, particularly in young patients or those with articular involvement. The Holstein-Lewis fracture (distal third spiral fracture) carries high risk of radial nerve palsy.

Mnemonic: "Shoulder Fracture Red Flags" (NEEDS MRI)
N - Neurovascular deficit
E - Extensive displacement (>1cm)
E - Elderly with articular involvement
D - Dislocation component
S - Surgical neck involvement
M - Multiple tuberosity fractures
R - Rotator cuff dysfunction
I - Instability on examination

Upper Limb Fractures: Upper Arm

Humeral Shaft Fractures

These typically result from direct trauma (e.g., motor vehicle accidents) or indirect torsional forces (e.g., arm wrestling). The radial nerve is particularly vulnerable as it spirals around the humerus in the radial groove (mid-distal third junction).

Clinical Assessment

Examination must document wrist extension (extensor carpi radialis longus/brevis) and thumb abduction (extensor pollicis longus) to assess radial nerve function. The Holstein-Lewis fracture (distal third spiral pattern) has up to 22% incidence of radial nerve palsy.

Treatment Principles

Most humeral shaft fractures heal well with functional bracing (Sarmiento brace), achieving union rates >90%. Surgical indications include open fractures, vascular injury, floating elbow, and polytrauma. Plate osteosynthesis remains the gold standard, while intramedullary nailing carries higher risk of shoulder impingement.

Mnemonic: "Radial Nerve Examination" (BEST)
B - Brachioradialis reflex
E - Extension of wrist
S - Sensation over dorsal first webspace
T - Thumb abduction (extensor pollicis longus)

Upper Limb Fractures: Forearm

Radius and Ulna Fractures

The forearm functions as a complex ring structure, where disruption at one site often implies injury elsewhere (the "ring of destruction" concept). The interosseous membrane maintains stability between radius and ulna, allowing pronation-supination. Fractures may involve:

Monteggia Fracture-Dislocation

This eponymous injury combines proximal ulna fracture with radial head dislocation. Bado's classification (Types I-IV) describes the direction of radial head displacement. The posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) is particularly vulnerable in Type III injuries.

Galeazzi Fracture-Dislocation

The "fracture of necessity" involves distal radius fracture with distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) disruption. The Essex-Lopresti variant adds interosseous membrane rupture and proximal radial migration.

Both-Bone Forearm Fractures

These high-energy injuries in adults require anatomic reduction to preserve rotation. The radial bow (average 13° apex lateral) must be restored during ORIF to maintain pronation-supination arc.

Mnemonic: "Forearm Fracture Essentials" (CRASH)
C - Check for associated dislocations (Monteggia/Galeazzi)
R - Restore radial bow during fixation
A - Assess DRUJ stability after radius fixation
S - Secure fixation allows early motion
H - High complication rate with non-operative treatment

Upper Limb Fractures: Hand and Wrist

Distal Radius Fractures

The most common fracture in adults under 75 years, typically from FOOSH (Fall On OutStretched Hand) mechanism. Key patterns include:

Colles' Fracture

Dorsally displaced extra-articular fracture with dinner fork deformity. The Frykman classification incorporates ulnar styloid involvement and articular extension.

Smith's Fracture

"Reverse Colles'" with volar displacement, often from direct volar impact. Higher risk of carpal tunnel syndrome due to volar displacement.

Barton's Fracture

Intra-articular marginal fracture-dislocation (volar or dorsal). Requires CT for surgical planning due to articular comminution.

Scaphoid Fractures

The most commonly fractured carpal bone (70%), with high risk of avascular necrosis in proximal pole fractures due to retrograde blood supply. The snuffbox tenderness is pathognomonic but lacks sensitivity.

Mnemonic: "Scaphoid Fracture Red Flags" (SCAPH)
S - Snuffbox tenderness
C - Capitate displacement (DISI deformity)
A - AVN risk (proximal pole)
P - Pain with axial loading
H - High miss rate on initial radiographs

Lower Limb Fractures: Hip Region

Femoral Neck Fractures

These life-altering injuries in the elderly have 1-year mortality up to 30%. The medial femoral circumflex artery supplies the femoral head, making intracapsular fractures prone to AVN.

Garden Classification

Stages I-IV describe increasing displacement. Garden I (valgus impacted) may be treated with screws, while Garden III/IV typically require arthroplasty in elderly patients.

Pauwels Classification

Based on fracture angle relative to horizontal:

  1. Type I (<30°): Low shear forces
  2. Type II (30-50°): Moderate shear
  3. Type III (>50°): High shear, worst prognosis

Intertrochanteric Fractures

Extracapsular fractures between greater and lesser trochanters. The Evans classification distinguishes stable (reducible) from unstable (irreducible or reverse obliquity) patterns.

Mnemonic: "Hip Fracture Emergency" (SURGERY)
S - Surgery within 48 hours reduces mortality
U - Unstable patterns need cephalomedullary nails
R - Risk assessment for medical optimization
G - Garden classification guides treatment
E - Elderly require DVT prophylaxis
R - Rehabilitation starts day 1 post-op
Y - Yearly follow-up for contralateral hip

Lower Limb Fractures: Femoral Shaft

Femoral Diaphyseal Fractures

These high-energy injuries often result from motor vehicle collisions or falls from height. The Winquist-Hansen classification grades comminution from I (minimal) to IV (segmental).

Emergency Management

Femoral shaft fractures can lose 1-1.5L blood into the thigh. The ATLS protocol mandates immediate splinting (traction splint if available) and hemodynamic monitoring.

Definitive Treatment

Intramedullary nailing is the gold standard, with antegrade nailing (piriformis or trochanteric entry) preferred for most fractures. The safe zone for distal locking screws is 2cm above the physeal scar in adults.

Complications

Include malrotation (assess by comparing internal rotation of hips), leg length discrepancy, and fat embolism syndrome (tachypnea, hypoxia, petechiae).

Mnemonic: "Femoral Shaft Fracture Checklist" (FEMUR)
F - Fat embolism risk assessment
E - Evaluate for ipsilateral injuries (hip/knee)
M - Measure rotation intraoperatively
U - Use appropriate nail diameter/length
R - Ream for optimal endosteal contact

Lower Limb Fractures: Tibial Shaft

Tibial Diaphyseal Fractures

As the most commonly fractured long bone, tibial shaft fractures present unique challenges due to the subcutaneous anteromedial border and tenuous blood supply. The mechanism of injury determines fracture pattern:

Classification Systems

AO/OTA classification categorizes fractures by location, pattern, and comminution. The Tscherne classification grades soft tissue injury from 0 (closed, minimal damage) to III (compartment syndrome with arterial injury).

High-Risk Features

Open fractures require emergent irrigation/debridement (Gustilo-Anderson classification guides antibiotic duration). The distal third "watershed area" has poor vascularity, increasing nonunion risk. Compartment syndrome must be ruled out with serial examinations.

Treatment Principles

Intramedullary nailing is first-line for most fractures, with careful attention to:

  1. Maintaining 1cm of cortical overlap to prevent malunion
  2. Avoiding anterior cortical perforation during reaming
  3. Ensuring proper start point (just medial to lateral tibial spine on AP view)
Mnemonic: "Tibial Fracture Red Flags" (SPINE)
S - Soft tissue compromise (open fracture)
P - Pain out of proportion (compartment syndrome)
I - Intra-articular extension (plateau/plafond)
N - Neurovascular deficit (peroneal nerve, anterior tibial artery)
E - Elevated compartment pressures (>30mmHg or ΔP<30mmHg)

Lower Limb Fractures: Ankle

Ankle Fracture Classifications

Ankle fractures involve a complex interplay of bony and ligamentous structures. Two key systems guide management:

Lauge-Hansen Classification

Based on injury mechanism (position + force):

  1. Supination-Adduction (SA): Transverse fibula fracture + vertical medial malleolus
  2. Supination-External Rotation (SER): Spiral fibula fracture + posterior malleolus/deltoid injury
  3. Pronation-Abduction (PA): Short oblique fibula fracture + syndesmosis rupture
  4. Pronation-External Rotation (PER): High fibula fracture (Maisonneuve) + medial injury

Weber Classification

Simpler system based on fibula fracture level relative to syndesmosis:

  1. Weber A: Below syndesmosis (stable if isolated)
  2. Weber B: At syndesmosis (stability depends on medial injury)
  3. Weber C: Above syndesmosis (unstable, requires fixation)

Syndesmotic Injuries

The syndesmosis complex (anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament, interosseous membrane, posterior inferior tibiofibular ligament) requires fixation when:

  • Medial clear space >4mm on stress views
  • Fibular fracture >3cm above plafond
  • Positive intraoperative Cotton test
Mnemonic: "Ankle Fracture Stability" (MEDS)
M - Medial malleolus intact (or fixed)
E - Equal tibiofibular overlap on AP view
D - Deltoid ligament competent
S - Syndesmosis not widened (>5mm tibiofibular clear space)

Lower Limb Fractures: Foot

Calcaneal Fractures

These high-energy injuries often result from axial loading (falls from height). The Sanders classification (based on coronal CT) divides fractures by posterior facet involvement:

  1. Type I: Non-displaced
  2. Type II: Two-part posterior facet fracture
  3. Type III: Three-part with central depression
  4. Type IV: Comminuted (>3 parts)

Key Complications

Subtalar arthritis develops in 60% of operatively treated cases. Bohler's angle (normal 20-40°) predicts outcomes - values <20° indicate significant displacement.

Lisfranc Injuries

These tarsometatarsal joint disruptions are often missed. The "fleck sign" (avulsion fragment between 1st/2nd metatarsals) is pathognomonic. Myerson classification describes directional displacement (A=total, B1/B2=partial, C=divergent).

Surgical Considerations

Anatomical reduction is critical. The second TMT joint is the "keystone" of the arch. Fixation typically combines dorsal plating and transarticular screws.

Mnemonic: "Calcaneal Fracture Complications" (STEPS)
S - Subtalar arthritis
T - Tibial nerve entrapment (tarsal tunnel)
E - Exostosis (lateral wall blowout)
P - Peroneal tendon impingement
S - Skin necrosis (from displaced tuberosity)

Special Considerations: Pediatric Fractures

Physeal Injuries

The Salter-Harris classification guides management of growth plate fractures:

  1. Type I: Pure physeal separation (prognosis excellent)
  2. Type II: Metaphyseal fragment (most common, good prognosis)
  3. Type III: Intra-articular epiphyseal fracture (requires anatomic reduction)
  4. Type IV: Metaphyseal + epiphyseal fracture (high growth arrest risk)
  5. Type V: Physeal compression (worst prognosis)

Management Principles

Remodeling potential depends on:

  • Age (younger > older)
  • Location (physeal proximity)
  • Plane of deformity (sagittal > coronal)

Greenstick fractures (incomplete cortical breaks) require complete reduction to prevent recurrence.

Pediatric Femur Fractures

Treatment varies by age:

  • <2 years: Pavlik harness/spica cast
  • 2-5 years: Early spica casting (acceptable shortening <2cm)
  • 6-10 years: Flexible intramedullary nailing
  • >11 years: Rigid trochanteric-entry nailing
Mnemonic: "Salter-Harris Complications" (GROWTH)
G - Growth arrest (Types IV-V)
R - Residual deformity (Types III-V)
O - Open reduction often needed (Types III-IV)
W - Weight-bearing restrictions (articular involvement)
T - Timing matters (reduce within 7 days)
H - Hardware removal (may bridge physis temporarily)
