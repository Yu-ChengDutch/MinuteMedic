Introduction to Ventricular Arrhythmias & Conduction Blocks

The heart's electrical system is a marvel of biological engineering, coordinating the synchronized contraction of atrial and ventricular myocardium to maintain efficient cardiac output. Ventricular arrhythmias and conduction blocks represent disruptions in this delicate system, often with life-threatening consequences.

Anatomical Foundations

The ventricular conduction system originates at the atrioventricular (AV) node, continues as the bundle of His which bifurcates into right and left bundle branches, and terminates in the extensive Purkinje fiber network. This specialized tissue conducts impulses 2-3 times faster than working myocardium, ensuring near-simultaneous ventricular activation.

Historical Perspective

The study of cardiac arrhythmias began with Augustus Waller's first human electrocardiogram (1887). Willem Einthoven's string galvanometer (1903) allowed clinical ECGs, while Mines and Garrey established reentry theory (1910s). The His bundle electrogram (1969) revolutionized conduction system analysis.

Electrophysiological Principles

Cardiac myocytes maintain a resting membrane potential (-90mV) via Na+/K+ ATPase. Action potentials propagate via gap junctions, with phase 0 depolarization (Na+ influx), phase 1-3 repolarization (K+ efflux), and phase 4 resting potential. Abnormal automaticity, triggered activity, or reentry circuits underlie arrhythmias.

Risk Factor Spectrum

Major risk factors include coronary artery disease (50% of sudden cardiac deaths), cardiomyopathies, electrolyte disturbances (K+, Mg2+, Ca2+), drug toxicity (digoxin, antiarrhythmics), and genetic channelopathies (Long QT, Brugada syndromes).

Ventricular Premature Complexes (VPCs)

Ventricular premature complexes (VPCs, also called PVCs) are early depolarizations originating from ectopic ventricular foci, appearing as wide QRS complexes (>120ms) without preceding P waves. They occur in >60% of healthy individuals but increase in frequency with age and cardiac disease.

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Three primary mechanisms underlie VPCs: enhanced automaticity (ectopic pacemaker cells fire faster than sinus node), triggered activity (early afterdepolarizations [EADs] or delayed afterdepolarizations [DADs]), and reentry (circus movement around areas of scar or ischemia).

Clinical Significance

While often benign, VPCs may indicate underlying pathology when frequent (>10/hr), multifocal, or occurring in patterns (bigeminy, trigeminy). "R-on-T phenomenon" (VPC landing on T wave) can precipitate ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation.

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

Ventricular tachycardia is defined as ≥3 consecutive ventricular beats at rate >100 bpm, classified by duration (non-sustained <30 sec, sustained ≥30 sec) and morphology (monomorphic vs polymorphic). It accounts for 70-80% of wide-complex tachycardias and carries significant mortality risk.

Etiological Spectrum

Ischemic cardiomyopathy (post-MI scar reentry) causes 70% of VT cases. Non-ischemic causes include dilated cardiomyopathy, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, arrhythmogenic RV dysplasia, and infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, amyloidosis). Genetic channelopathies (e.g., Brugada, CPVT) cause polymorphic VT.

Electrocardiographic Features

Monomorphic VT shows regular wide QRS (>140ms) with AV dissociation (independent P waves), fusion beats, and capture beats. Polymorphic VT has changing QRS morphology, often degenerating into VF. Torsades de pointes ("twisting of points") is a polymorphic VT with QRS axis rotation, occurring in long QT syndromes.

Management Strategies

Acute treatment depends on stability: unstable VT requires immediate synchronized cardioversion (100-200J), while stable VT may respond to IV amiodarone (150mg bolus) or procainamide. Long-term prevention involves ICD implantation (primary/secondary prevention) and catheter ablation of reentrant circuits.

Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)

Ventricular fibrillation represents chaotic, disorganized ventricular depolarizations without effective contraction, appearing as irregular waveforms without discernible QRS complexes. It is the initial rhythm in 70% of sudden cardiac deaths, requiring immediate defibrillation.

Pathophysiological Cascade

VF begins when multiple wavelets of depolarization propagate randomly through the ventricles due to dispersion of refractoriness and fractionation of wavefronts. This creates >300 uncoordinated contractions/minute, resulting in pulseless electrical activity and circulatory collapse within seconds.

Reversible Causes (Hs and Ts)

Management follows ACLS algorithms while addressing reversible causes: Hypoxia, Hypovolemia, Hydrogen ions (acidosis), Hyper/hypokalemia, Hypothermia, Toxins, Tamponade, Tension pneumothorax, Thrombosis (coronary/pulmonary).

Treatment Protocol

Immediate defibrillation (200-360J biphasic) is paramount, followed by CPR during rhythm checks. Epinephrine (1mg q3-5min) or amiodarone (300mg bolus) may be administered. Post-resuscitation care involves therapeutic hypothermia (32-36°C for 24h) and coronary angiography if STEMI is suspected.

First-Degree AV Block

First-degree AV block manifests as PR interval prolongation (>200ms) with maintained 1:1 AV conduction. While generally benign, marked PR prolongation (>300ms) may cause hemodynamic compromise due to reduced ventricular filling time.

Pathological Substrates

Common causes include increased vagal tone (athletes), AV nodal disease, medications (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin), and inflammatory conditions (Lyme disease, rheumatic fever). It represents delayed conduction without actual block, typically at the AV node.

Clinical Implications

Asymptomatic patients require no treatment. Symptomatic cases (e.g., pacemaker syndrome from excessive PR prolongation) may need pacemaker implantation. Progression to higher-grade block occurs in <5% annually.

Second-Degree AV Block: Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach)

Mobitz I (Wenckebach) block shows progressive PR prolongation until a P wave fails to conduct, typically at the AV node. The ECG reveals "grouped beating" with RR interval shortening before the dropped beat.

Characteristic Features

The block ratio (e.g., 3:2, 4:3) describes conducted vs non-conducted P waves. The pause containing the dropped beat is less than twice the preceding PP interval. This distinguishes it from Mobitz II where PR remains constant before sudden block.

Second-Degree AV Block: Mobitz Type II

Mobitz II block involves sudden failure of conduction without preceding PR prolongation, typically below the AV node (His-Purkinje system). It carries higher risk of progression to complete heart block (35-50% annually).

Electrocardiographic Diagnosis

The hallmark is constant PR intervals before sudden non-conducted P waves, usually with a fixed conduction ratio (e.g., 2:1, 3:1). QRS is often wide due to associated bundle branch disease. Unlike Wenckebach, the pause equals exactly two PP intervals.

Clinical Management

Asymptomatic Mobitz II requires permanent pacing due to unpredictable progression to complete heart block. Symptomatic patients receive transvenous pacing emergently. Reversible causes (e.g., Lyme carditis) may respond to antibiotics.

Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block

Complete heart block features total dissociation of atrial and ventricular activity, with atrial rate exceeding ventricular escape rate. The escape rhythm determines QRS width: narrow (junctional, 40-60 bpm) vs wide (ventricular, 20-40 bpm).

Etiological Considerations

Congenital complete block occurs with maternal lupus (anti-Ro/SSA antibodies). Acquired causes include degenerative fibrosis (Lenègre-Lev disease), ischemia (right coronary occlusion), infiltrative diseases (amyloidosis), and valvular surgery (aortic valve replacement).

Treatment Imperatives

Hemodynamically unstable patients require immediate transcutaneous/transvenous pacing. Permanent dual-chamber pacemaker is indicated for symptomatic bradycardia or escape rate <40 bpm. Reversible causes (e.g., drug toxicity) should be addressed.
