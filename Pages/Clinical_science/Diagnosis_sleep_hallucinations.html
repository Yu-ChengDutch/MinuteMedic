Sleep-Related Hallucinations

Sleep-Related Hallucinations: A Clinical Overview

Introduction: The Sleeping Brain and the Boundary of Consciousness

Sleep is not a passive state of neural quiescence. It is an active, structured, cyclically organised process during which the brain engages in memory consolidation, synaptic pruning, metabolic restoration, and — of particular relevance here — the generation of rich perceptual experiences entirely disconnected from the external world. The study of hallucinations that arise in the context of sleep occupies a peculiar position in neuropsychiatry: it sits at the intersection of normal neurobiology and overt psychopathology, demanding that the clinician understand what constitutes a variant of healthy sleep before recognising what departs from it.

Historical Context

Interest in the relationship between sleep and hallucinatory experience dates back to classical antiquity, with Aristotle writing on the nature of dreams as early as the 4th century BCE. The modern scientific era began with the application of the electroencephalogram (EEG) to sleep research in the mid-twentieth century. The American psychiatrist William Charles Dement (1928–2020), a foundational figure in sleep medicine, went so far as to suggest that dreams themselves qualify as hallucinations — a provocative claim that continues to motivate inquiry into the continuum between normal dreaming and waking psychopathology.

The first formal diagnostic classification of sleep-related hallucinations appeared in the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD), first edition, 1990, under the narrow category of Terrifying Hypnagogic Hallucinations — defined then as rare, intensely frightening experiences occurring exclusively at sleep onset. Subsequent editions (ICSD-2, 2005; ICSD-3, 2014; ICSD-3-TR, 2023) progressively broadened this category into what is now termed Sleep-Related Hallucinations (SRH), which constitute a formal diagnostic group within the broader cluster of Other Parasomnias.

The French neurologist and psychiatrist Jules-Gabriel François Baillarger (1809–1890) produced the first published clinical descriptions of hypnagogic hallucinations in the general population, nearly 180 years ago. Alfred Maury (1817–1892), in 1865, proposed a continuum between sleep-onset hallucinations and daytime hallucinatory experiences — a conceptual framework that remains contested but intellectually productive.

Neurophysiology of Sleep States: The Essential Foundation

Human consciousness naturally cycles among three neurophysiological states: wakefulness, rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, and non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep. These are not discrete categorical switches but continuous, quantifiable states, distinguished by the complexity and pattern of neuronal interactions across the central nervous system.

NREM sleep is itself stratified into three stages. N1 (light sleep) is the transitional stage at sleep onset, characterised by theta-range EEG activity, slow eye movements, and reduced muscle tone; this is the stage most associated with hypnagogic hallucinations. N2 is defined by the appearance of sleep spindles and K-complexes, representing active inhibitory thalamocortical processing. N3 (slow-wave sleep, SWS) is characterised by high-amplitude, low-frequency delta waves and represents the deepest stage of NREM; it is the stage from which disorders of arousal (sleepwalking, sleep terrors) typically emerge.

REM sleep is characterised by a desynchronised, low-amplitude EEG that closely resembles wakefulness, rapid conjugate eye movements, and near-complete skeletal muscle atonia mediated by active inhibition of spinal motor neurons. It is the predominant substrate for vivid dreaming and is closely linked to memory consolidation, particularly of procedural and emotional material. The pontine brainstem — specifically the pedunculopontine nucleus (PPN) and the locus coeruleus — plays a central role in REM generation and, as will become relevant, in the pathophysiology of complex nocturnal visual hallucinations.

A key anatomical concept is the relative disconnection of the prefrontal cortex during both NREM and REM sleep. Executive functions — reality monitoring, source attribution, voluntary attention — depend on prefrontal engagement. Their suspension during sleep is why internally generated perceptual experiences are accepted as real. This is the foundation upon which all sleep-related hallucinations rest.

In some individuals, the ordinarily clean separation between these states breaks down. Elements of REM and NREM may dissociate or recombine, creating undesirable or intrusive experiences during sleep: the parasomnias. Sleep-related hallucinations represent one class of phenomena arising from precisely this state-boundary instability.

Methodological Considerations

SRH are assessed primarily through subjective retrospective self-report — surveys, interviews, and sleep laboratory awakenings. This is a significant constraint. The sleeping brain exists in an altered state of consciousness, and the transition to wakefulness interrupts memory transfer, producing anterograde amnesia on awakening. REM sleep, whose neurophysiology most closely approximates wakefulness, tends to produce better-recalled experiences than NREM. Recall is further degraded by time elapsed, memory interference, and — in the clinical populations most affected by complex nocturnal visual hallucinations — cognitive impairment. Interpretation of prevalence and phenomenology data must therefore be tempered by these methodological limitations.

Objective sleep laboratory methods (polysomnography, real-time EEG, video-PSG) allow identification of the sleep stage from which hallucinations arise by awakening participants at predetermined intervals. The combination of subjective report with video-polysomnography is the most informative approach, particularly for distinguishing complex nocturnal visual hallucinations from REM sleep behaviour disorder, NREM parasomnia, and nocturnal seizures.

Classification of Sleep-Related Hallucinations

ICSD-3 Diagnostic Criteria and Taxonomy

Under the ICSD-3 and its 2023 text revision, Sleep-Related Hallucinations (SRH) are defined as hallucinatory experiences that occur at sleep onset or on awakening from sleep. The formal diagnostic criteria require: (1) a complaint of recurrent hallucinations experienced just before sleep onset or on awakening; (2) that the hallucinations are prominently visual in modality; and (3) that the disturbance is not better explained by another sleep, mental, or medical disorder, medication, or substance use. They are classified within the broader group of Other Parasomnias, a residual ICSD-3 category encompassing conditions with diverse presentations and incompletely characterised pathophysiological bases.

SRH comprise exactly two distinct phenomena:

  1. Hypnagogic and Hypnopompic Hallucinations (HHH) — occurring in the drowsy state at sleep onset (hypnagogic) or during awakening from sleep (hypnopompic). These are collectively referred to as hypnagogia.
  2. Complex Nocturnal Visual Hallucinations (CNVH) — occurring during the night following a sudden awakening, predominantly in patients with visual or neurologic disorders.

These two subtypes are related by their shared classification but differ substantially in their epidemiology, clinical associations, electrophysiology, and likely mechanisms, as detailed in the sections that follow. The ICSD-3 criteria specify that infrequent hallucinations of this type may fall within the limits of normal sleep-wake transition; recurrence is required to meet the diagnostic threshold.

Mnemonic — "HHH vs CNVH: Boundary vs Breach"
HHH occur at the boundary of sleep — at the threshold of falling asleep or waking up. They are common, mostly benign, and represent a normal variant of sleep-wake transition.
CNVH represent a breach — they erupt from the middle of the night, interrupt sleep abruptly, persist into wakefulness, and signal possible neuropathology.
Think: HHH = at the threshold; CNVH = through the night.

Hypnagogic Hallucinations

Epidemiology and Phenomenology

Hypnagogic hallucinations are fleeting perceptual phenomena that occur at the moment of falling asleep. They are among the most common hallucinatory experiences in the human population, with lifetime prevalence rates ranging from 2% to 75% in the general population — a variation that reflects significant methodological heterogeneity across studies rather than genuine epidemiological uncertainty. The prevalence distribution follows a U-shaped curve, occurring with greater frequency in both younger individuals (adolescents and young adults) and older people, with a relative trough in middle age. Gender effects are inconsistently reported across studies.

In people with psychiatric disorders — including anxiety disorders, PTSD, psychotic disorders, and mood disorders — hypnagogic hallucinations are reported at substantially elevated rates, approximately 50–80%, compared to the general population. However, the nature of this association is not well understood. Poor sleep quality and frequent nocturnal awakenings in these populations may simply provide more opportunities for recall of sleep-onset experiences rather than reflecting a genuinely elevated biological rate of occurrence.

Hypnagogic hallucinations are defined as arising at sleep onset, conventionally associated with N1, though definitions vary across studies, with some including N2 or any NREM stage immediately preceding REM. As sleep progressively deepens from N1 toward REM, there is a well-documented evolution in the form and content of sleep mentation. In N1, reports are typically vague and fragmentary — predominantly visual, lacking narrative structure. As sleep deepens, thought-like imagery progressively acquires perceptual qualities: auditory and somatosensory components emerge, the content becomes unusual or bizarre, and voluntary control and reality monitoring are progressively lost — features that begin to resemble clinical hallucinations.

Sensory Modalities and Content

The visual modality predominates. Visual imagery is present in 55–86% of hypnagogic reports. It spans a wide range from simple to complex: geometric patterns, shapes, kaleidoscopically changing colours, and flashes of light represent the simpler end; human faces, figures, animals, scenes involving landscapes and people represent the more complex. Some of the simpler imagery may be entoptic in origin — arising from the visual system itself, possibly related to spontaneous activity in the occipital cortex during the transition to sleep, which has been documented using H₂¹⁵O-PET and EEG-fMRI studies.

Auditory components are present in 8–35% of hypnagogic events and include everyday sounds (radio, phone, doorbell, music) as well as voices. Bodily sensations are reported in 25–50% of events — sensations of touch and bodily distortion, or feelings of weightlessness, flying, or falling into an abyss. The latter (hypnic jerk or hypnagogic jerk) is among the most universally reported experiences at sleep onset. Affective content is present in fewer than 5% of events — examples include being caught in a fire or involved in an attack. A sense of presence in the room may also occur, though this is more characteristically associated with sleep paralysis than with isolated hypnagogic hallucinations.

Critically, hypnagogic hallucinations are generally benign and non-pathological. They do not require investigation or treatment unless they are frequent, distressing, or occur in the context of other sleep disorder symptoms. Their upgrading across successive ICSD editions from rare, clinically significant phenomena to a formal diagnostic category has been criticised as potentially unwarranted pathologisation of a normal biological experience.

Hypnopompic Hallucinations

Phenomenology and Distinguishing Features

Hypnopompic hallucinations accompany the departure from sleep, typically persisting for seconds to a few minutes. The term derives from the Greek hypnos (sleep) and pompē (a sending away). They most commonly occur in the morning, though this is not invariable. They are reported less frequently than hypnagogic hallucinations, with prevalence rates of approximately 7–13% in the general population, rising to approximately 15–36% in individuals with psychiatric disorders.

A defining characteristic of hypnopompic hallucinations is that they tend to arise from a period of REM sleep rather than from the shallow NREM onset that characterises hypnagogic hallucinations. As a result, their phenomenology more closely resembles the continuation of a dream sequence: they are described as vividly real, intense, and sometimes narratively structured. A typical example is a figure from a dream conversation who remains visible and audible even after the eyes open, appearing as genuinely present in the bedroom — then fading as full wakefulness is established. The hallucinated figures rarely interact after wakefulness begins and disappear with the completion of arousal.

Despite their often striking character, hypnopompic hallucinations are rarely frightening — only approximately 30% carry a threatening or distressing content. The individual is frequently uncertain whether the experience was a continuation of dreaming or a genuine waking perception. Compared to hypnagogic hallucinations, forgetting appears less rapid: the experience tends to linger in awareness after awakening, which may explain why hypnopompic hallucinations are sometimes confused with more pathological perceptual disturbances.

Clinically, the key distinction to make is between isolated, infrequent hypnopompic hallucinations (benign) and those occurring in the context of narcolepsy, where they are associated with sleep-onset REM periods, cataplexy, sleep paralysis, and excessive daytime somnolence — the tetrad of narcolepsy. The presence of hypnopompic hallucinations should always prompt enquiry about daytime sleepiness and cataplexy.

Mnemonic — Hypnagogic vs Hypnopompic: "Going vs Gone"
HypnaGOgic = you are GOing to sleep (sleep onset).
HypnoPOMPic = pomp is a grand exit — you are making your exit from sleep (awakening).
Alternatively: "Gogic = Going; Pompic = Parting."

Complex Nocturnal Visual Hallucinations (CNVH)

Definition, Phenomenology, and Clinical Profile

Complex Nocturnal Visual Hallucinations represent the second and clinically weightier subtype of SRH. Unlike HHH, which occur at the margins of sleep, CNVH arise from within consolidated sleep, terminating it abruptly. They are characterised by sudden awakening during the night (rather than at sleep onset or morning arousal), followed by — or concurrent with — vivid, complex visual hallucinations that persist into the waking state. The individual may be partially or fully aroused and may engage in directed behaviours in response to the hallucinated content.

Phenomenologically, CNVH involve complex visual imagery: small animals (insects, rodents), human figures, distorted forms, or multicoloured, intricate images that may be superimposed on the actual environment — a feature described as scene superimposition. The images can be grotesque, distorted in size or proportion, and relatively immobile. The eyes may be open and visually tracking the hallucinated object across space. Affective content is frequently present: the experience may be frightening or alarming, and emotional responses — facial expressions of fear or even amusement — have been documented on video-polysomnography. Memory for the event is well preserved, which distinguishes CNVH from many other parasomnia events.

CNVH have been documented to occur with a mean frequency of approximately four times per week in affected individuals. The evidence base is comparatively thin — CNVH have been described largely in small case series, small cohorts, and patients with documented sleep disorders, rather than at the population level. This limits generalisability.

Electrophysiology

CNVH are associated with a distinctive EEG pattern on video-polysomnography: low-voltage, mixed-frequency signals during NREM sleep, representing a dissociated state. Gnoni and colleagues (2022) proposed that CNVH involve the simultaneous decoupling and reassembly of wake, NREM, and REM elements — a form of multi-state coexistence. Supporting this, EEG microstates of alpha-band activity recorded during slow-wave sleep and REM, localised to midbrain regions including the pedunculopontine nucleus (PPN), are indistinguishable from waking conscious states. The PPN is a particularly relevant structure: it receives projections from virtually every sensory system, and lesions in this region produce visual hallucinations that closely resemble CNVH.

Clinical Associations

Unlike HHH, CNVH are not a normal biological variant. They are predominantly associated with neurologic and visual disorders. Conditions most commonly linked include:

  1. Alpha-synucleinopathies: Lewy body disease and Parkinson's disease — where oculo-visual disturbances are common and hallucinations are a recognised disease feature, often worsening with darkness.
  2. Charles Bonnet Syndrome: arising from deafferentation of the afferent visual pathway (e.g., glaucoma, macular degeneration), with the visual cortex generating aberrant, release-type imagery.
  3. Peduncular hallucinosis: caused by midbrain or diencephalic lesions, producing vivid, colourful, complex visual hallucinations with retained insight.
  4. Other neurologic conditions: brainstem or thalamic lesions, pontine demyelination.

CNVH have also been described — though less commonly — in individuals without any identified neuropathology, and in cases responsive to melatonin, suggesting heterogeneity in the underlying mechanisms.

Mnemonic — CNVH Red Flags: "SNAP Awake"
S — Sudden arousal from mid-night sleep
N — Neurologic or visual disorder association
A — Acts out (behavioural response: sitting up, pointing, tracking)
P — Persists into wakefulness with clear recall
Any hallucination that makes you SNAP awake in the night should raise suspicion for CNVH and underlying neuropathology.

The Differential Diagnosis: A Systematic Approach

Key Distinguishing Features Across Sleep Disorders and Related Conditions

The clinical differentiation of SRH from other sleep disorders and neuropsychiatric conditions is non-trivial and requires attention to several axes simultaneously: timing within the night, content and complexity, affective valence, type of arousal, quality of memory recall, persistence into wakefulness, and co-occurrence with other parasomnias. The table below — discussed condition by condition — represents the core diagnostic framework drawn from the paper's primary comparative table.

Hallucinations in the "Other Parasomnias" Category (ICSD-3)

Hypnagogic Hallucinations

Occurring at sleep onset (usually in the first third of the night, from NREM or sleep-onset REM). Content progresses gradually from simple to complex, multimodal, with an auditory component as sleep approaches REM. Affective content is rare. Memory recall is immediate if the person is awoken but is rapidly forgotten. The arousal pattern is a brief arousal from sleep, which may be followed by a return to sleep or progression to wakefulness. The sleep-wake state boundary is sometimes difficult to differentiate from sleep-onset dreams. These hallucinations co-occur with other parasomnias.

Hypnopompic Hallucinations

Occurring on waking, usually in the second half of the night, out of a period of REM sleep. Content is sometimes complex, especially when arising out of REM. Affective content is rarely threatening. Recall is immediate but rapidly forgotten. The arousal pattern mirrors hypnagogic: brief arousal from sleep, possibly followed by return to sleep. The boundary between sleep and waking is sometimes indistinct — content can be difficult to differentiate from dreams. These also co-occur with other parasomnias.

Terrifying Hypnagogic Hallucinations (THH)

Occurring at sleep onset, with sudden awakening. Content is intensely frightening — similar to nightmares. Strong affective content (fear, dread, threatening presences). Immediate and prominent recall. Sudden, abrupt arousal with autonomic hyperactivity. A critical distinguishing feature: no clear separation of sleep and wake states — the experience persists into wakefulness. "Double consciousness" — simultaneous recall of the dream content and awareness of the external environment — has been described. Co-occurs with other parasomnias.

Complex Nocturnal Visual Hallucinations (CNVH)

As detailed in the preceding section: sudden arousal during the night. Complex visual imagery, sometimes superimposed on the environment. Strong affective content. Immediate and prominent recall. Sudden arousal with autonomic hyperactivity. No clear sleep-wake separation — persists into wakefulness. Identified by the patient as distinct from dreaming. Co-occurs with other parasomnias.

Exploding Head Syndrome

Occurring at sleep onset or on waking. Content is simple — a sudden loud auditory hallucination (abrupt loud bang), sometimes with a simple visual component (flash of light). Affective content is present (often distressing). Recall is immediate. Arousal is abrupt. The experience is recognised as distinct from dreaming. It is distinguished from SRH by its brevity (a fraction of a second) and by the absence of complex visual imagery. Lower prevalence than hypnagogic states, though reportedly common in college students.

REM-Related Parasomnias and NREM Disorders of Arousal

Nightmare Disorder (REM-related parasomnia)

Nightmares occur at any time during the night but are most frequent in the second half, predominating in REM sleep. Content is vivid and emotionally intense — distressing, threatening, and fear-inducing — though typically less intense than sleep terrors. A key distinguishing feature is that nightmares are recognised as dreams: the individual, on full awakening, knows the experience was a dream. Memory recall is vivid and preserved. There is full awakening with autonomic hyperactivity (tachycardia, sweating), but the experience does not persist into wakefulness — the individual is orientated and aware of their surroundings immediately on awakening. Nightmares share emotive and frightening content with hypnagogic hallucinations, but are distinguished by their origin in REM, their full dream recognition, and their complete cessation on arousal.

Recurrent Sleep Paralysis (REM-related parasomnia)

Sleep paralysis involves the disturbing temporary inability to move voluntary muscles at sleep-wake transitions, arising from inappropriate persistence of REM atonia into wakefulness. It can occur at any time but is frequently associated with early-night, sleep-onset REM periods. Hallucinations accompany sleep paralysis in 25–75% of cases and are multisensory — auditory, visual, and kinesthetic. They include the classical incubus phenomenon: a hallucinated figure perceived as pressing down on the chest, producing intense dread and a sensation of suffocation. The individual feels awake and conscious but paralysed. The hallucinatory content is surreal, ominous, and perceived as real. Sleep paralysis hallucinations arise from a mixed REM and waking EEG — another example of state-boundary dissociation. Sleep paralysis commonly co-occurs with SRH, particularly in the context of hypersomnia or insufficient sleep, with co-occurrence rates of 4–40%.

REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder (REM-RBD)

In REM-RBD, loss of normal REM atonia allows patients to physically act out their dreams. This can result in vocalisation, punching, kicking, and complex motor behaviours during REM sleep. The experience ceases when the person is awoken — a critical distinction from CNVH, which tends to persist into wakefulness. REM-RBD is a strong prodromal marker for alpha-synucleinopathies (Parkinson's disease, Lewy body dementia, multiple system atrophy), and its presence should prompt longitudinal neurological follow-up.

Sleep Terrors (NREM Disorder of Arousal)

Sleep terrors occur from slow-wave sleep (N2 or N3), often in the early hours of the night when SWS predominates. They are characterised by sudden arousal with intense fear or panic, piercing screams, autonomic hyperactivity, and semi-purposeful behaviours (sitting up, attempting to escape). The individual is unresponsive to external stimuli. A critical distinguishing feature: unlike CNVH and THH, the hallucinatory content of sleep terrors, if recalled at all, is recognised as dream-like and does not persist into wakefulness. Amnesia for the episode is frequent; only fragments may be recalled. Sleep terrors co-occur with CNVH, and NREM origin has been proposed for both.

Mnemonic — "Does it PERSIST into wakefulness?"
Use this single question as a first-cut diagnostic filter:
YES, persists: CNVH, Terrifying Hypnagogic Hallucinations, Sleep Paralysis hallucinations
NO, ceases on awakening: Nightmares, Sleep Terrors, REM-RBD, ordinary HHH
Persistence into wakefulness = pathological state-boundary failure = investigate further.

The Differential Diagnosis (Continued)

Narcolepsy, Neurologic Disorders, and Other Conditions

Narcolepsy

Narcolepsy is a disorder of REM sleep dysregulation, underpinned in most type 1 cases by loss of hypothalamic hypocretin (orexin)-producing neurons. SRH occur in approximately 33–80% of individuals with narcolepsy. The hallucinations in narcolepsy are intense, vivid, brief, and dream-like, often multisensory (visual, auditory, and tactile), occurring at any time during the night. Crucially, in narcolepsy, it is not sleep that intrudes on night-time (as in CNVH), but rather sleep episodes that intrude on daytime function. Narcolepsy typically begins in youth, between ages 10–20 years — in contrast to CNVH, which is associated with older age of onset (mean ~40 years). Narcolepsy is associated with cataplexy (sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by strong emotion) and skeletal muscle paralysis, whereas CNVH is associated with sudden movements and sitting up. The presence of hypnagogic or hypnopompic hallucinations, particularly alongside excessive daytime sleepiness and cataplexy, should prompt evaluation for narcolepsy with MSLT and CSF hypocretin measurement.

Visual Hallucinations in Alpha-Synucleinopathies (Parkinson's Disease and Lewy Body Disease)

Formed visual hallucinations are a recognised, diagnostically relevant feature of Lewy body disease (one of the core clinical features in DLB criteria) and occur in approximately 20–40% of patients with Parkinson's disease. They are primarily visual and unimodal, typically consisting of formed images of people or animals, and are notable for their tendency to occur in the evenings or at night, with darkness or reduced vigilance. They may change with illumination — a feature also described in CNVH. The hallucinations are associated with oculo-visual problems (reduced contrast sensitivity, impaired colour discrimination, visuospatial dysfunction) that are well-characterised in these conditions. The strong phenomenological overlap between these hallucinations and CNVH has led to proposals of shared neurophysiological substrates, with separate neural system involvement. Clinically, new-onset CNVH in an older patient should prompt screening for early Parkinsonism.

Charles Bonnet Syndrome (CBS)

CBS arises from deafferentation of the afferent visual pathway — in effect, a release phenomenon. With loss of normal visual input (due to macular degeneration, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, or other causes of vision loss), the visual cortex is disinhibited and generates complex, spontaneous visual imagery. The hallucinations are typically vivid, detailed, colourful, and can include complex images of people, animals, and objects, sometimes distorted or bizarre — closely resembling the content of CNVH. A critical distinguishing feature is that affective content is usually absent in CBS (the experience is typically neutral or even pleasant), whereas CNVH commonly carries fear or alarm. Insight is retained in CBS — patients recognise the images as unreal — which is also typical of CNVH. CBS hallucinations are not sleep-specific.

Hallucinations Due to Psychotic Disorder

Psychotic hallucinations are primarily auditory-verbal (command, commentary, conversational voices), though visual, tactile, and olfactory hallucinations occur in approximately 30% of cases. They are not sleep-specific and are associated with abnormal belief formation (delusions), disorganised thought, and impaired reality testing — features not characteristic of SRH. The presence of auditory-verbal hallucinations with persecutory or referential content in wakefulness argues strongly against an isolated SRH diagnosis and requires formal psychiatric assessment.

Peduncular Hallucinosis

Peduncular hallucinosis arises from brainstem or diencephalic pathology — vascular lesions, demyelination, tumours — affecting the midbrain and upper pons. Hallucinations are described as complex, vivid, and colourful, sometimes with auditory content, and are associated with retained insight. They may occur at night or in association with drowsiness. The resemblance to CNVH is strong — consistent with the proposed role of the pedunculopontine nucleus in CNVH pathogenesis. Neuroimaging is indicated if peduncular hallucinosis is suspected.

Delirium

Delirium produces visual hallucinations that are typical, though multimodal hallucinations are also common. They are characterised by prolonged duration and complex formed images, but are accompanied by reduced insight, confusion, and altered consciousness — a globally impaired attentional state — which clearly distinguishes delirium from SRH, in which baseline cognitive function and insight are typically intact.

Sleep-Related Epilepsy

Epileptic activity occurring during sleep can produce hallucinatory experiences at any point in the night, often during N2. Content is multisensory — somaesthetic, auditory, or visual depending on the localisation of the epileptic focus. Experiences are usually brief and simple, though they may be complex if parietal and temporal association areas are involved. The stereotyped, ictal nature of the experience, possible post-ictal confusion, and EEG findings on sleep study help differentiate this from SRH.

Medications and Substances

Dopaminergic medications (e.g., levodopa, dopamine agonists in Parkinson's disease), anticholinergic agents, psychostimulants, dissociative anaesthetics, and psychedelics can all produce or exacerbate abnormal dream experiences and hallucinations. Content is typically complex and varied, dependent on the psychoactive substance and context. Insight may be reduced and confusion may be present. A thorough medication and substance history is essential in any patient presenting with nocturnal hallucinatory experiences.

Lucid Dreams

Lucid dreams are a normal variant in which the dreamer becomes aware that they are dreaming and may exert voluntary control over dream content. Content is often visual, tactile, and proprioceptive — sometimes complex, and may be superimposed on the environment. Unlike pathological SRH, the individual can dynamically switch between conscious awareness and the dream state. No affective distress is typical. Strategies to distinguish lucid dreaming from wakefulness — reality testing, attempting to change the environment — are sometimes spontaneously employed by experienced lucid dreamers.

Pathophysiology: State-Boundary Instability and Dissociation

Unifying Mechanisms Across Sleep-Related Hallucinations

The fundamental neurobiological substrate of SRH is failure of state-boundary integrity — the incomplete separation of wakefulness, NREM sleep, and REM sleep, allowing elements of one state to intrude upon another. This concept is central to understanding the entire spectrum of parasomnia phenomena, and SRH represent its perceptual expression.

In HHH, the mechanism is relatively straightforward: at the boundary of sleep onset or awakening, the orderly transition between states is momentarily unstable. Spontaneous activity in the occipital cortex — documented by PET and EEG studies — generates visual imagery in the absence of external input, while the prefrontal executive system has already partially disengaged, preventing reality monitoring. The imagery is accepted as real. As sleep deepens, REM-like elements (multimodal content, narrative structure) intrude on the transitional state, producing the richer, more complex hypnagogic experiences associated with late N1 and N2.

In CNVH, the dissociation is more profound and clinically significant. Gnoni and colleagues proposed that CNVH represents the simultaneous co-existence of wake, NREM, and REM elements — decoupled from one another and reassembled in an abnormal configuration. The evidence includes: (1) low-voltage, mixed-frequency EEG during episodes, inconsistent with either clean NREM or clean REM; (2) EEG microstates of alpha-band activity during SWS and REM that are indistinguishable from waking conscious states; and (3) localisation of these "micro-wake fragments" to the pedunculopontine nucleus and surrounding midbrain reticular formation. The PPN is the central node: it regulates REM sleep via cholinergic projections, receives input from virtually every sensory system, and its lesions produce visual hallucinations resembling CNVH.

Earlier models attributed complex hallucinations to REM intrusions into wakefulness — a model supported by the overlap with narcolepsy (where sleep-onset REM is pathological) and with the visual hallucinations of Parkinson's disease (where REM dysregulation is prominent). More recent evidence implicates NREM as the primary substrate for CNVH, given that most recorded episodes arise from NREM sleep on polysomnography. The reconciliation proposed by Gnoni et al. — that CNVH involves all three states simultaneously — accommodates both lines of evidence and is currently the most supported hypothesis.

A broader theoretical framework, proposed by Hobson and Friston, suggests that the dreaming brain operates as an "offline" virtual reality simulation of the "online" waking world — a predictive generative model running in the absence of sensory grounding. SRH may represent moments when this offline simulation bleeds inappropriately into the grounded waking state, generating percepts with all the phenomenological hallmarks of reality.

Visual Release as a Mechanism for CNVH and CBS

Across CNVH, Charles Bonnet syndrome, and peduncular hallucinosis, a common thread is visual cortical release from afferent suppression. In CBS, this is straightforwardly due to loss of retinal input. In peduncular hallucinosis and CNVH, the deafferentation is functional rather than structural — arising from disruption of ascending sensory modulation from the brainstem. In all three, the visual cortex, freed from its normal input, generates spontaneous, complex, vivid imagery. This "release hallucination" model is well-supported and provides a mechanistic basis for the phenomenological similarities between these conditions.

Association with Other Sleep Disorders and Clinical Assessment

SRH as a Non-Specific Symptom: Diagnostic Caution

A recurring theme in the clinical evaluation of SRH is their diagnostic non-specificity. SRH occur across a wide range of conditions — narcolepsy, REM-related parasomnias, NREM disorders of arousal, psychiatric disorders, neurologic disorders, and in otherwise healthy individuals — and do not reliably point to any single underlying diagnosis. A categorical approach to assessment based solely on the presence of SRH is therefore insufficient and potentially misleading.

The clinical framework endorsed by the authors emphasises the importance of the combination of features accompanying SRH rather than any single hallmark. Key questions for the clinical evaluation include:

  1. What is the timing of the hallucinations within the night (sleep onset, mid-night, morning awakening)?
  2. What is the content — simple or complex, visual or multimodal?
  3. Is there affective content — and if so, of what valence?
  4. What is the type of arousal — gradual, brief, or sudden and abrupt?
  5. Is there clear separation of sleep and wake states, or does the experience persist into wakefulness?
  6. What is the quality of memory recall?
  7. Do the hallucinations co-occur with other parasomnia symptoms — sleepwalking, sleep paralysis, cataplexy, acting out of dreams?
  8. Are there daytime symptoms — excessive daytime somnolence, cognitive decline, tremor, visual impairment?

In terms of specific associations: SRH occur in approximately 33–80% of patients with narcolepsy, making them a clinically useful prompt for considering this diagnosis, particularly when accompanied by excessive daytime somnolence. The co-occurrence of SRH with sleep paralysis is common (4–40%), especially in the context of hypersomnia or insufficient sleep. CNVH are commonly seen in disorders of the visual pathway and in alpha-synucleinopathies; their presence in an older patient — particularly one with cognitive symptoms, REM-RBD, or Parkinsonian features — warrants neurological investigation.

Not all hallucinations require intervention. Infrequent hallucinations may fall within the limits of normal sleep-wake transition. The clinical threshold for investigation and treatment is set by recurrence, distress, functional impairment, or the presence of features suggesting an underlying neurological or psychiatric condition. The authors explicitly caution against over-pathologising what is, in the case of HHH, a common and predominantly benign experience.

Summary: Conceptual Synthesis and Unresolved Questions

What We Know, What Remains Uncertain, and Where Research Is Heading

Sleep-related hallucinations represent a diagnostically recognised but incompletely understood category of perceptual phenomena. Their classification under ICSD-3 as a unified group of "Other Parasomnias" containing both HHH and CNVH has been productive in drawing clinical attention to these experiences, but raises unresolved conceptual questions that the authors articulate as follows:

First, the relationship between CNVH and Terrifying Hypnagogic Hallucinations is unclear. They share: nightmare-like content, strong affective charge, body movements (vocalisation/screaming for THH; sitting up and eye tracking for CNVH), intact recall, persistence into wakefulness, and "double consciousness." The primary distinguishing feature is timing — THH occur at sleep onset, CNVH during established sleep. Whether these represent the same phenomenon at different sleep stages, or genuinely distinct entities, is unresolved.

Second, the question of whether HHH and CNVH should be grouped under the same diagnostic category remains open. HHH are extremely common, mostly benign, non-specific, and possibly the result of normal biological variation. CNVH are uncommon, neurologically significant, strongly associated with identifiable pathology, and carry a distinct clinical profile. Their shared EEG link to NREM sleep provides a commonality, but evidence also connects both to all states of consciousness — waking, NREM, REM, and sleep onset. A continuum model — HHH as the normal variant end, CNVH as the pathological end — is plausible, but a continuum of presentation does not necessitate a continuum of mechanism. Their differing demographic profiles and clinical associations suggest that the underlying neurobiological processes may be distinct.

Third, the pathologisation of HHH across successive ICSD editions has been questioned. Upgrading an extremely common, predominantly benign experience to a formal sleep disorder diagnosis requires robust evidence of clinical merit that remains, by the authors' own assessment, incompletely established.

Finally, there remain significant methodological barriers to progress: retrospective self-report as the primary assessment tool, cognitive impairment in the most affected populations, the inherent difficulty of studying experiences that occur in an altered state of consciousness and are rapidly forgotten, and the small sample sizes of most CNVH studies. Future research priorities include the development of reliable objective assessment tools capable of differentiating between features of daytime and sleep-related hallucinations, and the further characterisation of the neurophysiological substrates of CNVH using high-density EEG and simultaneous fMRI.
