Introduction to Lung Cancer

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, accounting for approximately 1.8 million deaths annually. Its pathogenesis is deeply intertwined with the anatomy and physiology of the respiratory system, as well as environmental and genetic factors.

Anatomical Considerations

The lungs are paired organs responsible for gas exchange, divided into lobes (three on the right, two on the left). The bronchial tree branches into progressively smaller airways, terminating in alveoli. The blood supply is dual: the pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood for gas exchange, while the bronchial arteries supply oxygenated blood to lung tissue itself. This complex architecture explains why lung cancers may arise from epithelial cells (bronchi, alveoli), neuroendocrine cells, or mesenchymal tissues.

Historical Context

Lung cancer was rare before the 20th century. The rise of cigarette smoking in the 1920s led to an epidemic, with Richard Doll's 1950 study definitively linking smoking to carcinoma. The discovery of oncogenes (e.g., EGFR, KRAS) and tumor suppressors (e.g., p53) in the late 20th century revolutionized our understanding of molecular pathogenesis.

Physiological Basis

Normal lung epithelium undergoes continuous renewal via basal stem cells. Carcinogens (e.g., tobacco smoke) cause DNA mutations that disrupt apoptosis and promote uncontrolled proliferation. The lung's high oxygen tension may exacerbate oxidative DNA damage. Additionally, the rich lymphatic drainage facilitates early metastatic spread to hilar and mediastinal nodes.

Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC)

Accounting for 85% of cases, NSCLC includes adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. These share similar treatment paradigms but differ in etiology and molecular profile.

Adenocarcinoma

Risk factors: Smoking (though 20% occur in never-smokers), radon exposure, asbestos. More common in women and East Asians.

Pathophysiology: Arises from peripheral glandular cells. Common mutations include EGFR, ALK, ROS1. Often produces mucin visible on histology.

Mnemonic: "Adeno Cancers Like East Asia" - Adenocarcinoma is Associated with EGFR mutations, Common in non-smokers, and Linked to East Asian ethnicity.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Risk factors: Strongly smoking-related. Associated with COPD due to chronic inflammation.

Pathophysiology: Originates in central bronchi, showing keratin pearls and intercellular bridges. Often expresses p40 and has FGFR1 amplifications.

Large Cell Carcinoma

Risk factors: Similar to adenocarcinoma but more aggressive. No defining molecular markers.

Key feature: Diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out glandular/squamous differentiation.

Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC)

An aggressive neuroendocrine tumor representing 15% of cases. Almost exclusively linked to smoking.

Pathophysiology

Derived from Kulchitsky cells in bronchial epithelium. Shows neurosecretory granules and expresses CD56, synaptophysin. Characterized by rapid doubling time and early metastases (70% present with extensive disease).

Mnemonic: "Small Cells Smoke Strongly" - SCLC is Strongly associated with Smoking, has Small cells with neuroendocrine features, and Shows early Spread.

Clinical Features

Paraneoplastic syndromes are common: SIADH (20%), Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (3%), Cushing's syndrome (ACTH secretion).

Treatment

Limited stage: Chemoradiation with etoposide/platinum. Extensive stage: Immunotherapy (atezolizumab) added to chemotherapy. Surgery is rarely indicated due to early micrometastases.

Diagnostic Approach

Imaging: CT chest (non-contrast for screening, contrast for staging). PET-CT evaluates metastatic spread. Sputum cytology has low sensitivity (20%).

Biopsy Techniques

Bronchoscopy: Best for central lesions (yield >90%). May include EBUS for nodal staging. TTNA: For peripheral nodules (risk of pneumothorax 15%). Liquid biopsy: Emerging for EGFR testing in inaccessible tumors.

Emerging Therapies

Targeted therapies: EGFR inhibitors (osimertinib for T790M+), ALK inhibitors (alectinib), ROS1 inhibitors (crizotinib). Immunotherapy: PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors (pembrolizumab for PD-L1 >50%).

Complications

Treatment-related: Pneumonitis (immunotherapy), QTc prolongation (EGFR inhibitors). Tumor-related: Superior vena cava syndrome, spinal cord compression.
