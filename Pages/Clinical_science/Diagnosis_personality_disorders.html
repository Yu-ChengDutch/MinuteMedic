Introduction to Personality Disorders

Personality disorders represent enduring patterns of inner experience and behavior that deviate markedly from the expectations of an individual's culture, are pervasive and inflexible, have an onset in adolescence or early adulthood, are stable over time, and lead to distress or impairment. These disorders are classified into three clusters (A, B, and C) based on descriptive similarities.

Historical Context

The concept of personality disorders has evolved significantly since the late 19th century. Early descriptions by Philippe Pinel (1801) of "mania without delirium" and J.C. Prichard's (1835) "moral insanity" laid groundwork for understanding these conditions. The modern conceptualization emerged through psychoanalytic theories (Freud, Jung), behavioral models, and eventually the DSM system, which operationalized diagnostic criteria.

Neurobiological Underpinnings

Current research suggests personality disorders arise from complex interactions between:

  1. Genetic factors: Heritability estimates range from 30-60% for various traits
  2. Neuroanatomical differences: Particularly in prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and anterior cingulate
  3. Neurochemical factors: Dysregulation in serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine systems
  4. Temperamental predispositions: Observable from early childhood
  5. Environmental influences: Especially childhood trauma and attachment disruptions

The diathesis-stress model best explains how these factors interact - innate vulnerabilities are activated or exacerbated by environmental stressors during critical developmental periods.

Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis requires:

  1. Assessment of long-term patterns (not episodic symptoms)
  2. Evaluation across multiple domains: cognition, affectivity, interpersonal functioning, impulse control
  3. Determination that patterns are inflexible and maladaptive
  4. Exclusion of substance-induced or medical etiology
Mnemonic: PERVADE - Key features of personality disorders:
Pervasive, Enduring, Rigid, Volitional impairment, Adolescent onset, Deviant, Ego-syntonic

Cluster A Personality Disorders

Cluster A comprises paranoid, schizoid, and schizotypal personality disorders. These disorders are characterized by odd or eccentric behaviors and share some phenomenological similarities with schizophrenia spectrum disorders, though with preserved reality testing.

Paranoid Personality Disorder

Characterized by a pervasive distrust and suspiciousness of others such that their motives are interpreted as malevolent, beginning by early adulthood and present in various contexts.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥4 of the following):

  1. Suspects others are exploiting, harming, or deceiving them
  2. Preoccupied with unjustified doubts about loyalty of friends/associates
  3. Reluctant to confide in others due to fear information will be used maliciously
  4. Reads hidden demeaning or threatening meanings into benign remarks/events
  5. Persistently bears grudges
  6. Perceives attacks on character not apparent to others; quick to react angrily
  7. Has recurrent suspicions regarding fidelity of spouse/partner

Complications: Social isolation, occupational difficulties, increased risk for delusional disorder or schizophrenia, higher rates of litigation.

Mnemonic: SUSPECT - Key features of Paranoid PD:
Suspects others, Unfounded doubts, Secretive, Perceives threats, Enemies everywhere, Confidence lacking, Touchy

Schizoid Personality Disorder

Characterized by a pervasive pattern of detachment from social relationships and restricted range of emotional expression in interpersonal settings.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥4 of the following):

  1. Neither desires nor enjoys close relationships
  2. Almost always chooses solitary activities
  3. Little interest in sexual experiences
  4. Takes pleasure in few activities
  5. Lacks close friends/confidants
  6. Appears indifferent to praise/criticism
  7. Shows emotional coldness or flattened affect

Key distinction from avoidant PD: Schizoid individuals genuinely prefer being alone, while avoidant individuals desire relationships but fear rejection.

Complications: Underemployment, vulnerability to exploitation due to social isolation, increased risk of developing schizophrenia (though most do not).

Mnemonic: DISTANT - Features of Schizoid PD:
Detached, Indifferent, Solitary, Task-focused (not people), Affect flat, No close friends, Takes pleasure in few things

Schizotypal Personality Disorder

Characterized by acute discomfort with close relationships, cognitive or perceptual distortions, and eccentricities of behavior. Considered part of the schizophrenia spectrum.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥5 of the following):

  1. Ideas of reference (excluding delusions)
  2. Odd beliefs or magical thinking influencing behavior
  3. Unusual perceptual experiences
  4. Odd thinking and speech
  5. Suspiciousness or paranoid ideation
  6. Inappropriate or constricted affect
  7. Odd, eccentric, or peculiar behavior/appearance
  8. Lack of close friends/confidants
  9. Excessive social anxiety persisting due to paranoid fears

Complications: High risk for developing schizophrenia (10-20%), depression, anxiety disorders. Many experience significant functional impairment.

Mnemonic: ME PECULIAR - Schizotypal features:
Magical thinking, Eccentric appearance, Paranoid ideation, Experiences unusual perceptions, Constricted affect, Unusual beliefs, Lacks friends, Ideas of reference, Anxious socially, Rambling speech

Cluster B Personality Disorders

Cluster B includes antisocial, borderline, histrionic, and narcissistic personality disorders. These are characterized by dramatic, emotional, or erratic behaviors and share features of impulsivity and emotional dysregulation.

Antisocial Personality Disorder

Characterized by a pervasive pattern of disregard for and violation of the rights of others occurring since age 15. Requires evidence of conduct disorder before age 15.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥3 of the following since age 15):

  1. Failure to conform to social norms/laws
  2. Deceitfulness (repeated lying, conning others)
  3. Impulsivity/failure to plan ahead
  4. Irritability and aggressiveness
  5. Reckless disregard for safety of self/others
  6. Consistent irresponsibility
  7. Lack of remorse

Neurobiological factors: Reduced gray matter in prefrontal cortex, amygdala abnormalities, low serotonin activity.

Complications: Substance use disorders, incarceration, high mortality from reckless behavior, comorbid mood disorders.

Mnemonic: CORRUPT - Antisocial PD features:
Conning others, Obligations ignored, Recklessness, Remorseless, Underhanded, Planning deficit, Temper problems

Borderline Personality Disorder

Characterized by instability in interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affect, with marked impulsivity. Central feature is fear of abandonment.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥5 of the following):

  1. Frantic efforts to avoid abandonment
  2. Unstable/intense interpersonal relationships alternating between idealization/devaluation
  3. Identity disturbance
  4. Impulsivity in ≥2 potentially self-damaging areas
  5. Recurrent suicidal behavior/gestures/self-mutilation
  6. Affective instability
  7. Chronic feelings of emptiness
  8. Inappropriate anger
  9. Transient paranoid ideation or severe dissociative symptoms

Neurobiology: Hyperactive amygdala, reduced prefrontal regulation, altered opioid systems.

Complications: High suicide completion rate (8-10%), self-injury, substance abuse, eating disorders, high utilization of medical/psychiatric services.

Mnemonic: AM SUICIDE - Borderline PD features:
Abandonment fears, Mood instability, Suicide/self-harm, Unstable relationships, Identity disturbance, Control of anger poor, Impulsivity, Dissociation/paranoia, Emptiness

Histrionic Personality Disorder

Characterized by excessive emotionality and attention-seeking behavior. Patients often appear dramatic, enthusiastic, and flirtatious.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥5 of the following):

  1. Uncomfortable when not center of attention
  2. Inappropriately seductive/provocative behavior
  3. Rapidly shifting/shallow expression of emotions
  4. Consistently uses physical appearance to draw attention
  5. Speech excessively impressionistic/lacking detail
  6. Self-dramatization/theatricality
  7. Suggestible
  8. Considers relationships more intimate than they are

Complications: Higher rates of somatization disorders, conversion symptoms, depression when attention is unavailable.

Mnemonic: PRAISE ME - Histrionic PD features:
Provocative behavior, Relationships considered intimate, Attention needed, Impressionistic speech, Suggestible, Emotions shallow, Make-up/appearance focus, Emotional lability

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Characterized by grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy. Two subtypes exist: overt (grandiose) and covert (vulnerable).

DSM-5 Criteria (≥5 of the following):

  1. Grandiose sense of self-importance
  2. Preoccupied with fantasies of success/power/brilliance
  3. Believes they are "special" and can only be understood by high-status people
  4. Requires excessive admiration
  5. Sense of entitlement
  6. Interpersonally exploitative
  7. Lacks empathy
  8. Envious of others or believes others envy them
  9. Arrogant/haughty behaviors/attitudes

Complications: Vulnerable to depression when facing failures, interpersonal difficulties, occupational problems due to inability to work in teams.

Mnemonic: SPECIAL - Narcissistic PD features:
Superiority, Power fantasies, Exploitative, Can only associate with special people, Infatuated with self, Admiration needed, Lacks empathy

Cluster C Personality Disorders

Cluster C includes avoidant, dependent, and obsessive-compulsive personality disorders. These are characterized by anxious or fearful behaviors and share features of high anxiety and inhibition.

Avoidant Personality Disorder

Characterized by social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥4 of the following):

  1. Avoids occupational activities involving significant interpersonal contact
  2. Unwilling to get involved unless certain of being liked
  3. Shows restraint in intimate relationships due to fear of shame/ridicule
  4. Preoccupied with criticism/rejection
  5. Inhibited in new interpersonal situations
  6. Views self as socially inept/unappealing
  7. Unusually reluctant to take personal risks

Key distinction from social anxiety disorder: Avoidant PD is broader, affecting self-concept and all relationships, not just performance situations.

Complications: Social isolation, underemployment, secondary depression.

Mnemonic: AVOIDER - Avoidant PD features:
Avoids jobs with contact, Views self negatively, Occupied with rejection, Inhibited in new situations, Doesn't get involved unless sure of being liked, Exaggerates risks, Restrained in intimacy

Dependent Personality Disorder

Characterized by a pervasive and excessive need to be taken care of leading to submissive/clinging behavior and fears of separation.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥5 of the following):

  1. Difficulty making everyday decisions without excessive advice/reassurance
  2. Needs others to assume responsibility for major life areas
  3. Difficulty expressing disagreement due to fear of loss of support
  4. Difficulty initiating projects due to lack of self-confidence
  5. Goes to excessive lengths to obtain nurturance/support
  6. Feels uncomfortable/helpless when alone
  7. Urgently seeks new relationship when one ends
  8. Unrealistically preoccupied with fears of being left to care for self

Complications: High risk for remaining in abusive relationships, depression when alone, vulnerability to other mental disorders.

Mnemonic: DEPENDENT - Dependent PD features:
Decision-making difficulty, Excessive need for support, Protection seeking, Expressing disagreement hard, New relationship sought urgently, Discomfort when alone, Exaggerated fears of self-care, Nurturance sought, Takes initiative poorly

Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD)

Characterized by preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control at the expense of flexibility/openness/efficiency.

DSM-5 Criteria (≥4 of the following):

  1. Preoccupied with details/rules/lists to extent that major point is lost
  2. Perfectionism interferes with task completion
  3. Excessively devoted to work (not due to financial necessity)
  4. Overconscientious/scrupulous about ethics/morals
  5. Unable to discard worthless objects
  6. Reluctant to delegate unless others submit exactly to their way
  7. Miserly spending style
  8. Rigidity/stubbornness

Key distinction from OCD: OCPD traits are ego-syntonic (vs. ego-dystonic in OCD), and lacks true obsessions/compulsions.

Complications: Occupational success but interpersonal difficulties, higher risk for anxiety/depression when unable to maintain control.

Mnemonic: LAW FIRMS - OCPD features:
Loses point in details, Activity dominated by work, Won't discard things, Flexibility lacking, Insists on own way, Reluctant to spend, Morality rigid, Stubborn
