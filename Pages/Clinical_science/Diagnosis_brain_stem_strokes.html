Introduction

The brainstem accounts for 10-15% of all strokes, with more ischemic than hemorrhagic incidents. As the brainstem is composed of the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata, a brainstem stroke could take place in any of these. While these anatomical/functional divisions are based vertically, if we split the brainstem into vertical laminae we find the tectum, tegmentum and basis:

Grey matter then, is organised in clusters forming the cranial nerve nuclei, pontine nuclei and reticular formation. White matter, i.e., various ascending and descending tracts, is found mostly in the basis lamina (anterior), and is essential in communication between the brain and spinal cord. The grey matter nuclei then, mostly lead to the cranial nerves:

Remember: 2-3-4 -> midbrain has 2 nuclei, we start at 3, and both the pons and medulla oblongata have 4 nuclei

Within the brainstem, the pons is predominantly affected, with the mdulla accounting for only 7% of ischemic brain stem strokes and isolated midbrain infarctions being even more rare.

In terms of aetiology, large artery atherothrombosis is the major cause of ischemic strokes. Dissecction of the vertebral arteries might lead to an infarction in the medulla oblongata.

When talking about individual syndroms, Wallenberg syndrome or posterior cerebellar artery syndrome, is the most common of the brainstem strokes. The second most common then, is AICAS: anterior inferior cerebellar artery syndrome.

Vascularisation

Blood supply within the brain stem is not as easy as saying a single functional region, say the pons, receives all its blood from a single artery. Instead, various parts of various regions receive blood flow from various branches of various arteries.

The Circle of Willis is a vascular structure surrounding the optic chiasm. If we look at in relation to the brainstem, it becomes clear where the various arteries lead to. Essentially, the vertebral arteries branch of from the AA. Subclavii, then run through the transverse foramina of C6 to C2 to eventually pierce the dura mater, then at the lower body of the pons, they unite to form the A. Basilaris. Given that it must run parallel to the curve of the brainstem, the inferior part is not only inferior but also more posterior. The A. Cerebellaris posterior inferior is therefore given off by the AA. Vertebrales, while the A. Cerebellaris anterior inferior is given off directly after the merging of the vertebral arteries into the A. Basilaris at the inferior margin of the pons. This A. Cerebellaris anterior inferior also gives of the Labyrinthine artery to the internal auditory system. Since the upstream trajectory follows the pons, it makes sense that it gives off various pontine arteries. Right afterwards, it gives off the A. Cerbellaris superior as a final branch before the Circle of Willis.

The Circle of Willis concerns the proper cerebral blood flow and consists of three sets of arteries: Posterior cerebral, Middle cerebral, Anterior cerebral. The posterior and anterior are special because they both respectively A1/A2 and P1/P2 segments, where the 1 segment lies within the Circle, while the 2 segment is the one pointing outwards. The A. Cerebri media is special, as it is continuous with the Internal carotid artery and thus provides a connection with the body, same as the vertebral arteries. The posterior circle is formed by the P1 segments connecting the posterior cerebral with the basilar arteries, and the posterior communicating arteries connecting the posterior cerebral with the middle cerebral. The anterior segment is formed by the A1 segments running between A2 and middle cerebral. In terms of accessory arteries just remember that all cerebral arteries have two:

  1. Posterior cerebral: Posterior communicating, and posteromedial central
  2. Medial cerebral: Anterior choroidal, and ophthalmic
  3. Anterior cerebral: Recurrent a. of Heubner, and anteromedial central

Rarely, the entire midbrain and thalamus are supplied by the A. of Percheron, which branches of the Posterior cerebral artery.

Medulla oblongata

Since the medulla oblongata is the most inferior segment, it makes sense that blood supply consists of the spinal arteries, vertebral arteries and cerebellar arteries. Anterior medulla is supplied by the anterior spinal artery, and vertebral arteries. The lateral part by the posterior inferior cerebellar artery, and the posterior bit by the posterior spinal artery.

Pons

As the basillar arteries starts right before the pons, it makes sense that most of the blood supply is done by the pontine perforators of the basilar artery.

Midbrain

The most anterosuperior segment, bloodflow to the midbrain derives mostly from the posterior cerebral artery, as well as the choroidal artery and posterior cerebellar arteries.

Syndromes and symptoms

This is where it gets challenging, as there are many different syndromes. In temrs of

Midbrain syndromes

Claude syndrome affects the posterior cerebral artery and causes disruption of the fibres from the CN III, and various motor fibres. It causes ipsilateral CN III palsy (N. Oculomotorius -> Ptosis and hypoexotropia), and contralateral hemiplegia and ataxia

Benedict syndrome a similar area, but only targets the CN III and red nucleus. It presents as ipsilateral CN III palsy, and contralateral tremor/ataxia

Weber syndrome or ventral midbrain syndrome is essential Claude syndrome, but with possible invovlement of the substantia nigra, resulting in more Parkinson-like symptoms contralaterally

Pontine syndromes

Locked-in syndrome affects the upper ventral pons and targets essentially the white matter tracts plus the CN VI nuclei. It results in quadriplegia, bilateral facial palsy and horizontal eye palsy. Affected is the A. Basilaris

Raymond syndrome is essentially and an ipsilateral lateral gaze palsy and contralateral hemiparesis / facial palsy, as it targets the CN VI fibers and the corticospinal/corticofacial fibres.

AICAS or Marie-Foix or lateral pontine syndrome affects the nulcei of CN VII and VIII, corticospinal/spinothalamic/cerebellar tracts and results in contralateral hemiparesis, contralateral loss of proprioception and vibration, ipsilateral limb ataxia, ipsilateral facial palsy. It also presents with hearing loss, vertigo and nystagmus.

Medulla oblongata

Wallenberg syndrome is ipsilateral Horner's syndrome, and facial loss of nociception; while contralateral corporal loss of nociception. It also presents with nystagmus, dysarthria, dysphagia and hyperacusis.

Risk assessment

Bubble study

A solution with air bubbles is injected in a vein in the right arm. Patient is asked to cough, which reduces pressure in right side of the heart. Normally, bubbles can be traced through the right atrium, then ventricle and then lungs. When a patent foramen ovale is present, the bubbles can be seen crossing over to the left. This PTO increases risk of stroke and is present in about 25% of people. This is because a clot formed in body circulation can bypass the lungs, and directly go to the brain.
