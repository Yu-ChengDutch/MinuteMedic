Introduction

The kidneys are retroperitoneal organs responsible for filtration, electrolyte balance, and endocrine functions. Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons, the functional units composed of a glomerulus (capillary tuft) and a tubular system.

Glomerular Filtration Barrier

The glomerular filtration barrier (GFB) consists of three layers:

  1. Endothelium: Fenestrated capillaries preventing cellular passage.
  2. Basement membrane: Collagen/IV matrix with heparan sulfate (negative charge).
  3. Podocytes: Interdigitating foot processes with slit diaphragms (nephrin proteins).

Key physiology: The GFB restricts molecules by size (≥70 kDa blocked) and charge (negative repels albumin). Disruption leads to proteinuria—a hallmark of nephrotic/nephritic syndromes.

Mnemonic: "FEBS" for Glomerular Barrier Layers

Fenestrated endothelium → Electron-dense BM → Basement membrane → Slit diaphragm (podocytes)

Nephrotic vs. Nephritic Syndromes: Pathophysiological Basis of Clinical Differences

Core Pathophysiological Mechanisms

The clinical divergence between nephrotic and nephritic syndromes stems from distinct injury patterns in the glomerulus:

1. Nephrotic Syndrome: Podocyte Injury and Permselectivity Loss

In nephrotic syndrome, the primary defect is disruption of the glomerular filtration barrier's permselectivity, particularly at the level of podocytes. Key mechanisms:

  1. Podocyte Foot Process Effacement: Structural collapse of podocyte foot processes (e.g., due to nephrin mutations in congenital nephrotic syndrome or PLA2R antibodies in membranous nephropathy). This widens filtration slits, allowing albumin loss.
  2. Loss of Charge Selectivity: Degradation of heparan sulfate proteoglycans in the basement membrane (e.g., in diabetic nephropathy) reduces negative charge repulsion, permitting albuminuria.
  3. Consequences of Proteinuria: Hypoalbuminemia → decreased oncotic pressure → edema; compensatory hepatic lipoprotein synthesis → hyperlipidemia.

Histological hallmark: Electron microscopy shows foot process effacement without inflammatory infiltrates.

2. Nephritic Syndrome: Inflammatory Glomerular Damage

Nephritic syndrome results from immune-mediated inflammation damaging the glomerular capillary walls, leading to:

  1. Immune Complex Deposition: Subendothelial (e.g., lupus nephritis) or subepithelial (e.g., post-streptococcal GN) deposits activate complement (C5a) → neutrophil recruitment and protease release.
  2. Capillary Wall Disruption: Inflammation compromises endothelial integrity, allowing RBCs and fibrinogen to leak into Bowman’s space (→ RBC casts).
  3. Proliferative Changes: Mesangial/endothelial cell hyperplasia (e.g., IgA nephropathy) or crescent formation (RPGN) reduces glomerular blood flow → azotemia and hypertension.

Histological hallmark: Light microscopy shows hypercellularity, crescents, or neutrophilic infiltrates.

Mnemonic: "PORE" vs. "FIRE" for Pathophysiology

PORE (Nephrotic): Podocyte injury → Only protein leaks → Refined charge/size selectivity loss → Edema.
FIRE (Nephritic): Fibrinoid necrosis → Inflammation → RBCs escape → Endocapillary proliferation.

Why Nephrotic and Nephritic Syndromes Present Differently

1. Proteinuria Severity

Nephrotic: Massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day) occurs because podocyte injury creates large pores, but the basement membrane remains intact enough to prevent RBC leakage.
Nephritic: Subnephrotic proteinuria (<3.5 g/day) reflects smaller "holes" from focal capillary wall breaks, allowing limited protein but significant RBCs.

2. Hematuria

Nephrotic: Typically absent because inflammation doesn’t rupture capillaries. Microscopic hematuria may occur in advanced FSGS due to segmental scarring.
Nephritic: Dysmorphic RBCs result from RBCs squeezing through fractured basement membranes. RBC casts form when clots organize in tubules.

3. Hypertension/Azotemia

Nephrotic: Usually normotensive unless volume overloaded. GFR is preserved early (no occlusion).
Nephritic: Hypertension due to renin release from ischemic glomeruli (inflammation reduces flow). Azotemia occurs from obstructed capillaries (e.g., crescents).

Mnemonic: "Nephrotic Sells Protein, Nephritic Spills Blood"

Nephrotic = Selective Proteinuria | Nephritic = Purple (RBC) + Pressure (HTN).

Histological Correlation: Why Biopsy Matters

Nephrotic Syndrome Patterns

  1. Minimal Change Disease: Normal light microscopy; EM shows foot process effacement.
  2. Membranous Nephropathy: Thickened GBM with "spike-and-dome" appearance (IgG deposits).
  3. FSGS: Segmental sclerosis (scarring) of glomeruli (tip, collapsing, or perihilar variants).

Nephritic Syndrome Patterns

  1. Proliferative GN (e.g., IgA nephropathy): Mesangial hypercellularity.
  2. Crescentic GN: Cellular/fibrous crescents in Bowman’s space (RPGN).
  3. Post-Streptococcal GN: Subepithelial "humps" on EM.

Key point: Nephritic lesions often show C3/C4 deposition (immunofluorescence), while nephrotic lesions may have IgG (membranous) or be paucimmune (FSGS).

Nephrotic Syndrome: Etiologies and Pathophysiology

Primary causes (intrinsic glomerular disease):

  1. Minimal Change Disease (MCD): Podocyte foot process effacement (loss of nephrin). Most common in children.
  2. Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS): Collapse of glomerular capillaries with scarring. Associated with HIV, obesity, or genetic mutations (e.g., TRPC6).
  3. Membranous Nephropathy: Anti-PLA2R antibodies targeting podocytes. "Spike-and-dome" appearance on EM.

Secondary Causes

Systemic diseases (e.g., diabetes mellitus (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules), amyloidosis, lupus (class V)).

Mnemonic: "MDF" for Primary Nephrotic Causes

MCD → Diabetes (secondary) → FSGS

Diagnostics for Nephrotic Syndrome

Diagnosis relies on urinalysis, blood tests, and kidney biopsy when etiology is unclear:

1. Urinalysis & Protein Quantification

Key findings:

  1. Proteinuria (>3.5 g/day): Confirmed via 24-hour urine collection or spot urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR >3.5).
  2. Hypoalbuminemia (<3 g/dL): Due to urinary loss and impaired hepatic synthesis.
  3. Lipiduria: Oval fat bodies/Maltese crosses under polarized light.

2. Serologic Workup

To identify secondary causes:

  1. Anti-PLA2R antibodies (Membranous nephropathy).
  2. HIV/HBV/HCV serology (FSGS-associated).
  3. ANA, anti-dsDNA (Lupus nephritis).

3. Kidney Biopsy

Indicated if:

  1. Adult-onset (unlike pediatric MCD, which is steroid-responsive).
  2. Suspected secondary cause (e.g., amyloidosis).
  3. Steroid resistance.

Treatment of Nephrotic Syndrome

Management targets proteinuria reduction and complication prevention:

1. Primary Therapy (Disease-Specific)

  1. Minimal Change Disease (MCD): High-dose glucocorticoids (90% respond). Relapses may require cyclophosphamide/rituximab.
  2. FSGS: Steroids + calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus/cyclosporine).
  3. Membranous Nephropathy: Anti-PLA2R+ cases may respond to rituximab or cyclophosphamide + steroids.

2. Supportive Care

  1. ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression.
  2. Statins: For hyperlipidemia (increased CVD risk).
  3. Diuretics: For edema (avoid NSAIDs—worsen AKI).

Mnemonic: "STEReOID" for Steroid-Sensitive Nephrotic Causes

Steroids work in → Tip-lesion FSGS (some cases) → Eosinophilic MCD → Rapid responders (kids).

Diagnostics for Nephritic Syndrome

Focused on identifying inflammation and underlying etiology:

1. Urinalysis & Microscopy

  1. Dysmorphic RBCs (glomerular origin).
  2. RBC casts (pathognomonic for glomerulonephritis).
  3. Subnephrotic proteinuria (<3.5 g/day).

2. Serologic Workup

  1. Anti-GBM antibodies (Goodpasture’s syndrome).
  2. Low C3/C4 (Post-streptococcal GN, lupus).
  3. ANCA (MPO- or PR3-positive in vasculitis).

3. Kidney Biopsy

Essential for definitive diagnosis. Key findings:

  1. Diffuse proliferative GN (lupus, PSGN).
  2. Crescents (RPGN, e.g., ANCA vasculitis).

Treatment of Nephritic Syndrome

Targets immune suppression and preventing ESRD:

1. Disease-Specific Therapy

  1. Post-Streptococcal GN: Supportive care (usually self-limiting).
  2. Lupus Nephritis: High-dose steroids + mycophenolate/cyclophosphamide.
  3. ANCA Vasculitis: Rituximab or cyclophosphamide + steroids.
  4. Anti-GBM Disease: Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide.

2. Supportive Care

  1. Blood pressure control (ACEi/ARBs preferred).
  2. Dialysis if RPGN with oligoanuria.

Mnemonic: "CRESCENT" for RPGN Causes

C3/4 low (lupus) → Rapid decline → Endocapillary proliferation → Strep infection → CANCA/PR3 → Extracapillary crescents → Necrotizing GN → Target anti-GBM.
