Patellofemoral Syndrome

Overview and Nomenclature

Patellofemoral syndrome (PFS), also known as patellofemoral pain syndrome (PFPS) and runner's knee, is recognized as one of the most common causes of anterior knee pain seen by clinicians. Other names include retropatellar pain syndrome, lateral facet compression syndrome, or idiopathic anterior knee pain. PFS is considered a diagnosis of exclusion, meaning other intra-articular or peripatellar pathologies must be ruled out before confirming the diagnosis.

Etiology (Causes)

The cause of PFS is likely multifactorial, and no clear consensus exists, though training practices are often implicated. The syndrome is thought to involve six anatomic areas: subchondral bone, synovium, retinaculum, skin, nerve, and muscle. Four major contributing factors have been identified:

  1. Overactivity and Overload: Overuse appears to be the most important factor. Patients often describe that pain began during a period of increased activity or increased workload on the joint, such as miles ran or volume of work. Risk factors for overload include a BMI greater than 25, prior fitness level, and prior exercise regimen.
  2. Malalignment and Muscular Imbalance: This involves static components (e.g., leg length discrepancies, hamstring tightness, abnormal foot morphology) and dynamic components (e.g., muscle weakness, ground reaction forces). Hip abductor weakness may play a major role. Tight hamstrings can increase compressive forces across the patellofemoral joint.
  3. Trauma: Direct or indirect injuries to the patellar area can cause damage to structures leading to PFS.

Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

The diagnosis relies heavily on a detailed history and physical examination. Patients typically complain of generalized anterior knee pain that is usually poorly localized and felt behind or around the patella. The pain is described as achy, though it can also be sharp. Symptoms are worsened by loading a flexed knee, which occurs during activities like running, squatting, climbing stairs, and prolonged sitting. The diagnosis of PFS is typically clinical. Imaging, such as plain radiographs, is usually reserved for cases where symptoms fail to improve after one to two months of conservative management, as imaging is used primarily to rule out other causes like loose bodies, occult fracture, or osteoarthritis.

Management and Treatment

Treatment is typically conservative, focusing on pain reduction, improving patellar tracking, and restoring function.

  1. Acute Phase: This involves activity modification, ice, and NSAID use. NSAIDs have been shown to decrease overall pain but are usually not recommended for long-term treatment.
  2. Recovery Phase: This phase seeks to correct the underlying dysfunction. The most effective intervention is the combination of knee and hip exercises designed to increase lower extremity strength, mobility, and function.
  3. Adjunct Therapy: Patellar taping can be used to decrease pain when combined with physical therapy.
  4. Referral to orthopedic surgery is considered a last resort, and non-operative therapy should be pursued for up to 24 months before considering operative interventions.

Prognosis

The prognosis for PFS is generally good, with studies showing that up to two-thirds of patients can be successfully treated with a proper rehabilitation protocol. However, approximately 40% of patients experience continued symptoms one year after typical treatment. A potential complication of PFS is the development of patellofemoral osteoarthritis, which may result from inadequate tracking of the patella.
