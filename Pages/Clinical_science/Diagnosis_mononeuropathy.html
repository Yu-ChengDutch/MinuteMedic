Introduction to Mononeuropathies

Mononeuropathies are disorders affecting a single peripheral nerve, leading to motor, sensory, or autonomic dysfunction. These conditions arise from localized compression, trauma, ischemia, or inflammation, distinguishing them from polyneuropathies, which involve multiple nerves diffusely.

Anatomy of Peripheral Nerves

Peripheral nerves consist of axons surrounded by Schwann cells, which produce myelin in myelinated fibers. The endoneurium surrounds individual axons, the perineurium bundles axons into fascicles, and the epineurium encases the entire nerve. Nerves are vulnerable at specific anatomical sites, such as tunnels or fibrous bands, where compression is common.

Historical Context

The study of mononeuropathies dates to the 19th century, with early descriptions of carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) by Sir James Paget in 1854. Advances in electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies (NCS) in the 20th century revolutionized diagnosis.

Pathophysiology

Mononeuropathies result from:

  1. Compression: Mechanical pressure (e.g., CTS) disrupts axonal transport and causes demyelination.
  2. Ischemia: Reduced blood flow (e.g., in vasculitis) leads to nerve infarction.
  3. Trauma: Direct injury (e.g., lacerations) severs axons.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS)

Aetiology

CTS is caused by compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel, a rigid structure bounded by the carpal bones and the transverse carpal ligament. Common causes include:

  1. Repetitive hand use (e.g., typing, assembly line work)
  2. Pregnancy (fluid retention increases tunnel pressure)
  3. Obesity (adipose tissue reduces space)

Epidemiology

CTS affects 3–6% of adults, with a female-to-male ratio of 3:1. Risk factors include diabetes, hypothyroidism, and rheumatoid arthritis.

Symptoms

Patients report nocturnal paresthesias (tingling) in the thumb, index, and middle fingers, often relieved by shaking the hand (Flick sign). Advanced cases may show thenar atrophy and weakness in thumb abduction.

Mnemonic: "CARPAL" for CTS Risk Factors
Congenital (narrow tunnel), Arthritis (rheumatoid), Repetitive motion, Pregnancy, Acromegaly, Lumbrical incursion.

Diagnosis

Tinel’s sign (tapping the nerve elicits paresthesias) and Phalen’s maneuver (wrist flexion for 60 seconds reproduces symptoms) are classic clinical tests. Confirm with NCS showing delayed median nerve conduction at the wrist.

Treatment

First-line therapy includes wrist splinting and corticosteroid injections. Refractory cases require surgical decompression (carpal tunnel release).

Ulnar Neuropathy

Aetiology

The ulnar nerve is most commonly compressed at the elbow (cubital tunnel syndrome) or wrist (Guyon’s canal). Causes include:

  1. Chronic elbow flexion (e.g., sleeping posture)
  2. Trauma (e.g., "hitting the funny bone")
  3. Cyclist’s palsy (pressure from handlebars)

Symptoms

Patients experience paresthesias in the 4th and 5th fingers and weakness in intrinsic hand muscles (e.g., interossei). Severe cases cause claw hand (hyperextension of MCP joints, flexion of PIP/DIP joints).

Diagnosis

Froment’s sign (compensatory thumb flexion when pinching paper due to adductor pollicis weakness) is pathognomonic. NCS localizes the lesion.

Mnemonic: "ULNAR" for Ulnar Nerve Motor Innervation
Ulnar deviators (FCU), Lumbricals (3rd/4th), No thumb muscles (except adductor pollicis), All interossei, Ring/little finger flexion (FDP).

Peroneal (Fibular) Neuropathy

Aetiology

The common peroneal nerve is vulnerable at the fibular head, where compression may occur from:

  1. Prolonged squatting or leg crossing
  2. Weight loss (loss of protective fat)
  3. Trauma (e.g., fibular fracture)

Symptoms

Patients present with foot drop (weakness in dorsiflexion) and steppage gait (exaggerated hip flexion to avoid tripping). Sensory loss occurs over the lateral calf and dorsum of the foot.

Diagnosis

EMG/NCS shows denervation in peroneal-innervated muscles (e.g., tibialis anterior). MRI may reveal nerve compression.

Mnemonic: "PERONEAL" for Peroneal Nerve Branches
Peroneus longus/brevis, Extensor digitorum brevis, Recurrent articular branch, Other (superficial/deep branches), Nerve to tibialis anterior, Extensor hallucis longus, All sensory branches, Lateral cutaneous nerve.
