Introduction to Muscular Dystrophies

Muscular dystrophies represent a heterogeneous group of inherited myopathic disorders characterized by progressive muscle weakness and degeneration due to defects in various proteins crucial for muscle integrity. These conditions share the common pathological feature of ongoing muscle fiber necrosis and regeneration, ultimately leading to replacement by fibrous and adipose tissue.

Anatomical Considerations

The sarcolemma, or muscle cell membrane, is stabilized by a complex of proteins that link the intracellular cytoskeleton (particularly actin) to the extracellular matrix. This dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) serves as a shock absorber during muscle contraction. Disruption of any component of this complex leads to membrane instability, calcium influx, and eventual fiber necrosis.

Historical Perspective

The first description of muscular dystrophy dates back to 1836 by Gaetano Conte, but it was Guillaume Duchenne who provided the classic description of Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) in 1861. The discovery of dystrophin in 1987 by Louis Kunkel revolutionized our understanding of these disorders.

Pathophysiological Classification

Muscular dystrophies can be classified based on the affected protein:

  1. Sarcolemmal proteins (e.g., dystrophin in DMD/Becker)
  2. Nuclear envelope proteins (e.g., emerin in Emery-Dreifuss)
  3. Extracellular matrix proteins (e.g., laminin α2 in congenital MD)
  4. Sarcomeric proteins (e.g., titin in tibial MD)
  5. Enzymes (e.g., fukutin-related protein in LGMD)

Clinical Presentations

The presentation varies widely based on the specific dystrophy:

  1. Proximal weakness (most common pattern, seen in DMD, Becker, LGMD)
  2. Distal weakness (e.g., FSHD, Miyoshi myopathy)
  3. Oculopharyngeal (ptosis and dysphagia predominant)
  4. Cardiomyopathy-predominant (e.g., Emery-Dreifuss)

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

Aetiology

DMD is caused by mutations in the DMD gene located at Xp21.2, which encodes the protein dystrophin. About 70% of cases are due to large deletions, 10% to duplications, and 20% to point mutations. The mutations result in a frameshift that prevents production of functional dystrophin.

Epidemiology

DMD has an incidence of approximately 1 in 3,500-5,000 male births. It follows an X-linked recessive pattern, though about 30% of cases result from de novo mutations. Female carriers may manifest mild symptoms due to skewed X-inactivation.

Pathophysiology

The absence of dystrophin leads to:

  1. Disruption of the DGC, causing sarcolemmal instability
  2. Abnormal calcium influx and protease activation
  3. Repeated cycles of necrosis and regeneration until regenerative capacity is exhausted
  4. Replacement by fibrosis and fat

Symptoms and Signs

The clinical course follows a predictable progression:

  1. Early childhood (2-5 years): Delayed motor milestones, frequent falls, difficulty running/jumping
  2. School age (6-12 years): Progressive proximal weakness, Gowers' sign, waddling gait, calf pseudohypertrophy
  3. Adolescence (13+ years): Loss of ambulation (typically by age 12), scoliosis, respiratory insufficiency
Mnemonic: DMD Presentation - "3 F's and a C"
Frequent falls
Fatigability

Diagnosis

The diagnostic workup includes:

  1. Serum CK: Markedly elevated (10-100x normal) even in presymptomatic stage
  2. Genetic testing: Multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA) first-line to detect deletions/duplications
  3. Muscle biopsy: Shows variation in fiber size, necrosis, regeneration, and absent dystrophin staining
  4. EMG: Myopathic pattern with early recruitment of small, polyphasic units

Treatment

Management is multidisciplinary and includes:

  1. Corticosteroids: Prednisone (0.75 mg/kg/day) or deflazacort shown to prolong ambulation by 2-5 years
  2. Cardiac: ACE inhibitors/ARBs initiated once EF <55% or by age 10
  3. Respiratory: Nocturnal BiPAP when FVC <50%, cough assist devices
  4. Orthopedic: Spinal fusion for scoliosis >25°, tendon releases
  5. Emerging therapies: Exon-skipping (e.g., eteplirsen for exon 51), stop codon read-through (ataluren)

Complications

Major complications include:

  1. Cardiomyopathy: Present in all patients by age 18, leading cause of death
  2. Respiratory failure: Due to diaphragmatic weakness and scoliosis
  3. Contractures: Achilles, iliotibial band, elbow flexors
  4. Endocrine: Growth retardation, adrenal suppression from steroids, osteoporosis

Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)

Aetiology

BMD is allelic to DMD, also caused by mutations in the DMD gene. However, these mutations are in-frame deletions/duplications or missense mutations that allow production of a partially functional dystrophin protein. The reading frame hypothesis explains the phenotypic difference between DMD (out-of-frame) and BMD (in-frame).

Epidemiology

BMD has an incidence of approximately 1 in 18,000 male births. Like DMD, it follows X-linked recessive inheritance. The milder phenotype means some cases may go undiagnosed until adulthood.

Pathophysiology

The partially functional dystrophin leads to:

  1. Reduced but not absent DGC stabilization
  2. Slower progression of muscle damage
  3. Later onset of fibrofatty replacement

Symptoms and Signs

BMD presents with:

  1. Later onset (typically 5-15 years vs. 2-5 in DMD)
  2. Slower progression (ambulation often preserved into 20s-40s)
  3. Calf pseudohypertrophy (similar to DMD)
  4. Cardiomyopathy that may be disproportionate to skeletal weakness
Mnemonic: BMD vs. DMD - "Later, Longer, Less"
Later onset (school age vs. preschool)
Longer ambulation (into adulthood vs. loss by ~12)
Less severe cognitive impairment

Diagnosis

Similar to DMD but with important distinctions:

  1. Serum CK: Elevated but may be less dramatic than DMD (5-20x normal)
  2. Genetic testing: Same approach as DMD, but mutations preserve reading frame
  3. Muscle biopsy: Shows dystrophic changes but with patchy dystrophin staining (vs. absent in DMD)

Treatment

Management principles:

  1. Corticosteroids: Evidence less robust than for DMD, but may be considered
  2. Cardiac surveillance: Annual echocardiogram and ECG from diagnosis
  3. Physical therapy: Focus on maintaining mobility and preventing contractures
  4. Genetic counseling: Important for female carriers due to X-linked inheritance

Complications

Major complications include:

  1. Cardiomyopathy: May be the presenting feature in some cases
  2. Respiratory insufficiency: Occurs later than in DMD
  3. Contractures: Less severe than DMD but still problematic

Facioscapulohumeral Muscular Dystrophy (FSHD)

Aetiology

FSHD is caused by a contraction of the D4Z4 macrosatellite repeat on chromosome 4q35. Normally, there are 11-100 repeats; FSHD occurs when this is reduced to 1-10 repeats. This leads to derepression of the DUX4 gene, which is toxic to muscle cells.

Epidemiology

FSHD has a prevalence of about 1 in 20,000. It shows autosomal dominant inheritance with nearly complete penetrance by age 20. About 30% of cases represent de novo mutations.

Pathophysiology

The D4Z4 contraction results in:

  1. Chromatin relaxation and aberrant expression of DUX4
  2. DUX4 activates pro-apoptotic pathways and inhibits muscle differentiation
  3. Preferential involvement of facial, scapular, and humeral muscles

Symptoms and Signs

FSHD has distinct clinical features:

  1. Facial weakness: Inability to whistle, drink through a straw, or fully close eyes during sleep
  2. Scapular winging: Prominent when arms extended forward ("Mermaid sign" - inability to raise arms like a mermaid)
  3. Asymmetric weakness: Often more pronounced on one side
  4. Extramuscular manifestations: High-frequency hearing loss, retinal vasculopathy
Mnemonic: FSHD Features - "Face the Scapular Hum"
Face weakness (difficulty whistling)
Scapular winging
Humeral weakness (biceps/triceps affected early)
Distal leg involvement later (foot dorsiflexors)

Diagnosis

Diagnostic approach includes:

  1. Genetic testing: Southern blot for D4Z4 repeat contraction (gold standard)
  2. EMG: Myopathic pattern in affected muscles
  3. Muscle biopsy: Often shows inflammatory infiltrates (may mimic myositis)
  4. Ancillary tests: Audiometry, retinal examination

Treatment

Management is supportive:

  1. Physical therapy: Focus on scapular stabilization exercises
  2. Orthotics: Ankle-foot orthoses for foot drop
  3. Surgical: Scapulothoracic fusion for severe winging
  4. Monitoring: Annual evaluation for hearing/vision changes

Complications

Potential complications include:

  1. Severe facial weakness: Leading to exposure keratitis
  2. Respiratory insufficiency: In advanced cases with thoracic involvement
  3. Pain: Myalgias and joint pain due to abnormal biomechanics

Limb-Girdle Muscular Dystrophy (LGMD)

Aetiology

LGMD represents a heterogeneous group of disorders caused by mutations in various genes encoding proteins essential for muscle membrane stability, repair, or contractility. Over 30 subtypes exist, classified as:

  1. LGMD1: Autosomal dominant forms (rare)
  2. LGMD2: Autosomal recessive forms (more common)

Common subtypes include:

  1. LGMD2A: Calpain-3 deficiency (most common worldwide)
  2. LGMD2B: Dysferlinopathy
  3. LGMD2I: FKRP-related (common in Northern Europe)

Epidemiology

The collective prevalence is approximately 1 in 15,000. The most common subtypes vary by population:

  1. LGMD2A: Prevalent in Brazil, Italy, and Spain
  2. LGMD2I: Common in Northern Europe
  3. LGMD2B: Higher frequency in certain ethnic groups (e.g., Libyan Jews)

Pathophysiology

The mechanisms vary by subtype but generally involve:

  1. Sarcolemmal instability: Due to defects in dystrophin-associated proteins (e.g., sarcoglycans in LGMD2C-F)
  2. Impaired membrane repair: In dysferlinopathies (LGMD2B)
  3. Abnormal proteolysis: Calpain-3 deficiency (LGMD2A) affects sarcomeric remodeling

Symptoms and Signs

Despite heterogeneity, shared features include:

  1. Proximal > distal weakness: Affecting pelvic or shoulder girdle first
  2. Variable progression: From mild to severe disability
  3. Cardiac involvement: Particularly in LGMD2I (FKRP), LGMD1B (lamin A/C)
  4. Calf hypertrophy: Seen in sarcoglycanopathies (LGMD2C-F)
Mnemonic: LGMD Clues - "CALF DYSfunction"

Autosomal recessive (most subtypes)

FKRP (LGMD2I - cardiac involvement)

Diagnosis

Diagnostic approach:

  1. Serum CK: Typically elevated (5-50x normal), highest in dysferlinopathies
  2. Muscle biopsy: Immunohistochemistry can identify specific protein deficiencies
  3. Genetic testing: Targeted panels or whole exome sequencing
  4. Cardiac evaluation: ECG and echocardiogram in selected subtypes

Treatment

Management is subtype-specific but generally includes:

  1. Physical therapy: To maintain mobility and prevent contractures
  2. Cardiac monitoring: Especially in subtypes with known cardiac risk
  3. Respiratory support: Nocturnal ventilation if needed
  4. Experimental therapies: Gene therapy trials ongoing for certain subtypes

Complications

Potential complications include:

  1. Severe disability: Loss of ambulation in some subtypes
  2. Respiratory failure: Due to diaphragmatic weakness
  3. Cardiomyopathy: Particularly in LGMD2I and LGMD1B

Myotonic Dystrophy Type 1 and 2 (DM1/2)

Aetiology

Myotonic dystrophies are multisystem trinucleotide repeat disorders:

  1. DM1: Caused by CTG repeat expansion in DMPK gene on 19q13.3
  2. DM2: Caused by CCTG repeat expansion in CNBP gene on 3q21

The expansions lead to RNA toxicity affecting splicing of multiple other genes.

Epidemiology

DM1 has a prevalence of 1 in 8,000, while DM2 is rarer (1 in 20,000). DM1 shows anticipation (worsening in successive generations), especially when inherited maternally.

Pathophysiology

The expanded RNA forms nuclear foci that sequester RNA-binding proteins, leading to:

  1. Mis-splicing of multiple transcripts (e.g., CLCN1, INS)
  2. Muscle membrane hyperexcitability (causing myotonia)
  3. Multisystem involvement due to widespread splicing defects

Symptoms and Signs

DM1 and DM2 share features but differ in severity:

  1. Myotonia: Delayed relaxation after contraction (e.g., handgrip, percussion)
  2. Distal weakness: DM1 affects hands, ankles; DM2 more proximal
  3. Extramuscular: Cataracts, cardiac conduction defects, insulin resistance, frontal balding
  4. Congenital DM1: Severe form with hypotonia, respiratory failure at birth

Diagnosis

Diagnostic approach:

  1. Genetic testing: PCR/Southern blot for repeat expansions
  2. EMG: Shows myotonic discharges ("dive bomber" sound)
  3. Slit lamp: Early posterior subcapsular cataracts
  4. Cardiac: Holter monitoring for conduction abnormalities

Treatment

Management focuses on symptom control:

  1. Myotonia: Mexiletine (first-line), carbamazepine
  2. Cardiac: Pacemaker for significant conduction defects
  3. Endocrine: Diabetes screening and management
  4. Respiratory: Nocturnal ventilation if needed

Complications

Serious complications include:

  1. Sudden cardiac death: Due to arrhythmias
  2. Respiratory failure: Both restrictive and central hypoventilation
  3. Anesthetic risks: Increased sensitivity to sedatives and paralytics
