Today, we'll be learning about various cancers of the haematopoietic tissues.

Haematological malgnancies

There is three kinds of such malignancies:

  1. Leukemias: malignancies located in the blood
  2. Lymphomas: malignancies located in the lymph nodes
  3. Myelomas: malignancies located in the bone marrow

Statistically, lymphomas represent about 50% of all haematological malignancies, while leukemias represent about 30% and myelomas represent about 14%.

Leukemia

Characteristic for leukemia is the buildup of blasts, here also referred to as leukemia cells. However, what ends up causing symptoms is lack of normal blood cells. Subtypes of leukemia are subdivided by cell type and type of proliferation:

  1. Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL)
  2. Chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL)
  3. Acute myeloid leukemia (AML)
  4. Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML)

The acute forms are characterised by a rapid buildup of immature blood cells. These then crowd the bone marrow, halting production of healthy blood cells, which obviously results in low Hb and platelets. The chronic form hwoever, is characterised by the excessive buildup of mature white blood cells<./p>

Statistically, remember the following: ALL occurs in children, AML occurs in addults, Chronic leukemia occurs in the elderly. While not a hard-and-fast rule, it covers most cases.

Symptomatology

Symptoms are derived from two causes: lack of red blood cells/platelets, excess of white blood cells. The first results in bruising, pale skin, enlarged spleen/liver, petechiae, anaemia (dyspnea, palor) while the second results in frequent infection, fever, sores etc.. Concomittant headaches or ventricular hypertensive symptoms are an important warning sign for CNS invasion by leukemic cells.

Diagnosis

After clinical suspicion arises from the symtomatology, a blood count could show leukemia (high leukocyte counts) but similarly a condition called aleukemia exists in which the leukemic cells are still present in the bone marrow, but no leukemia shows up on a WBC count. This may occur in any subtype, but notably in hairy cell leukemia. A definitive diagnosis then, is only made after bone marrow biopsy with repeated lumbar punctures to monitor CNS invasion. On histology, typical for myeloid variants are so-called Auer rods which are cyrstalline, cytoplasmic inclusion bodies in myeloid blasts.

Genetics

Risk factors include formaldehyde, smoking, exposure to certain nuclear isotypes etc. but similarly, a genetic risk exists. Notably, people with Down's syndrome have a significantly increased risk of acute leukemia, while Fanconi anaemia carries a large risk of developing AML. Furthermore, 95% of people with CML carry the so-called Philadelphia translocation in which a gene from chromosome 9 gets translocated to chromosome 22.

Lymphomae

In essence, lymphomae are cancers of the immune system. Hence, risk factors include various infections: Epstein-Barr (Lyme) virus for Hodgkin and HIV/AIDS or immunosuppressants for non-Hodgkin. Symptomatology then is notably lymphadenopathy but similarly, B-symptoms (fever, night sweats and weight loss may occur.

If lymphadenopathy is present, diagnosis is made through a lymph-node biopsy.

Hodgkin lymphoma

Accounting for about 15% of all lymphomae, the Hodgkin variant is relatively common. Notably, about 50% of cases are due to Epstein-Barr virus. Prognosis is generally good, with 5-year survival being over 90%. Notably symptoms are hepatosplenomegaly and pain post alcohol consumption (pathognomic). However, what characterises Hodgkin lymphoma is the presence of Reed-Sternberg cells which are unusually large lymphocytes with multiple nuclei, each with thickened nuclear membranes.

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma

This being a very heterogenous group, it is split into two main subcategories: B-cell lymphocytes and T-cell lymphocytes. Of these, the most common are both of the B-type and are diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) and Follicular lymphoma (FL). The differentiating aspect is mainly histological: FL is characterised by both centroblasts and centrocytes while DLBCL is characterised by centroblasts uniquely, but spread out diffusely over the tissue. Macroscopically, FL may be recognised by a follicular or lobular appearance of the lymph node.

Staging of lymphomae

For both variants, the same Ann-Arbour staging is used:

  1. Stage I: Cancer is located in one lymph node
  2. Stage II: Cancer is located in two regions, both on the same side of the diaphragm
  3. Stage III: Cancer has spread to both sides of the diaphragm with one region near the lymph nodes or spleen
  4. Stage IV: Diffuse or disseminated involvement of one or more extralymphatic organs

Additionaly, "A" means the absence of B-symptoms, while "B" logically denotes their presence. "S" is then used to denote spread to the spleen, and "E" indicates extranodality.

Multiple Myeloma

This type of haematological carcinoma proceeds from the plasma B-cell and is the end-stage of a sequence of conditions:

  1. Monoclonal Gammopathy of Undetermined Significance (MGUS)
  2. Smouldering Myeloma (SMM)
  3. Multiple Myeloma (MM)

The old diagnostic criteria for MM are known by the acronym CRAB:

  1. C: Calcium -> Hypercalcaemia
  2. R: Renal insufficiency
  3. A: Anaemia
  4. B: Bone -> Osteolytic lesions on skeletal radiography

While a familial disposition to MM exists, another important risk factor is obesity with a 5 point increase leading to an RR increase of 11%. In order to support diagnosis, paraproteins (faulty proteins produced by the malignant cells) may be found; an example being Bence-Jones protein. However, final diagnosis is made based on bone-marrow biopsy. Histologically, the biopsy might show a perinuclear halo, bizarre cells, flame cells (bright red cytoplasm) and Mott cells (multiple clustered cytoplasmic droplets).
