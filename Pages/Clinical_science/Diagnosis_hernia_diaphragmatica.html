Introduction

Aetiologies

Most commonly, an acquired diaphragmatic hernia is secondary to trauma and thus diaphragmatic rupture. Thereafter, the most common cause is iatrogenic, notably liver resections and transplants.

Diaphragmatic hernia is notably also a congenital condition, where the fetal diaphragm develops incompletely. As it allows abdominal contents to creep into the thorax, it leads to abdominal lung development and persistent pulmonary hypertension. This congenital defect occurs in roughly 25 per 100.000 live births.

Embryology

The diaphragm originates from 4 main structures:

  1. Week 4: the septum transversum and esophageal mesentery merge from respectively posterior and anterior to form a separation between thorax and abdomen
  2. Week 5: the pleuroperitoneum forms to cover the separating membrane
  3. Week 8: the muscular structures making up the diaphragm form

Structure

The diaphragm is made up of an upward c-shaped dome dividing the thorax from the abdomen. It has peripheral muscular attachments which come together in a central tendon forming the crest of the dome. The attaching muscle is one whole, but has two distinct portions: costal, which is the actual driver of breathing and the crural which attaches the diaphragm to the lower limbs and lumbar vertebrae. The latter part is named such because it arises from the crural and arcuate ligaments which in turn arise from the lumbar vertebrae.

The diaphgram has three natural openings: caval hiatus, esophageal hiatus and aortic hiatus. The vena cava passes through the central tendon, the esophagus through the crural muscle and the aorta between the ligaments. This is as good a time as any to rehearse the fact that the vena cava lies anterior to the esophagus, which lies anterior to the aorta.

The following structures pass these openings at the following points:

  1. Caval hiatus (T8): Inferior vena cava
  2. Esophageal hiatus (T10): Esophagus, vagal trunks, left gastric artery
  3. Aortic hiatus (T12): Aorta, thoracic duct, Azygous vein

Of note is the sternocostal triangle or the foramina of Morgagni which lie between the costal and sternal attachments of the thoracic diaphragm and contain no structures.

Subtypes

Based on location

Based on location, there are three types of congenital hernia diaphragmatica:

  1. Bochdalek type: Postero-lateral costal muscle
  2. Morgagni type: Through the foramina of Morgagni
  3. Central type: Through the central tendon

The Bochdalek type is by far the most common and in 80% of cases occurs on the left side.

Based on passing structures

A hiatal hernia is a hernia in which abdominal organs slip through the diaphragm in to the middle compartment of the chest. Complications may involve GERD and laryngopharyngeal reflux. It is often associated with obesity, older age, major trauma and scoliosis. There are two main types:

  1. Type I: Sliding hernia -> Here, the entire oesophageal-stomach complex slides up into the thoracic cavity. It is due to widening of the muscular hiatal tunnel, and laxity of the phrenoesophageal ligament which allows the gastric cardia to herniate upward
  2. Type II: Paraesophageal hernia -> Here, the fundus and greater curvature of the stomach roll up through the diaphragm. It occurs when there is a local defect in the phrenoesophageal ligament. Then, the fundus leads the hearniation

A sliding type is by far the most common, representing 95% of all hiatal herniations. In general, 60% of those aged 50 and over have a hiatal hernia, while only 9% of these are symptomatic. As they are common in developed areas, but not in underdeveloped ones, low fibre diets with seated toilets are possible causes.
