Types and subtypes

Dementia exists in various forms and may occur at various stages. Most causes are neurodegenerative, but external causes such as syphilis, HIV and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease may occur. Here, we will go through the most common types to learn and discriminate them.

Testing for dementia

Two famous cognitive tests are used in the diagnosis or monitoring of patients with MCI or dementia: the MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) and MMSE (Mini Mental State Exam). Of these the MMSE is less influenced by educational level attained, while the MoCA seems to be better suited for recognition in early stages. However, neither tool is useful as the sole tool for diagnosis of dementia. As for scoring, on the MoCA, the cutoff for MSI is 26/30 while the cutoff for the MMSE is 24/30 for mild, 18/30 for moderate and 10/24 for severe.

Alzheimer's dementia

Accouning for 60-70% of dementia cases, AD is characterised by problems with short-term memory and word-finding. Pathophysiologically, the most affected part is the hippocampus with even the temporal and parietal lobes showing signs of atrophy. The histopathological hallmark is the overproduction of amyloid leading to both extracellular senile plaques (SPs) consisting of beta-amyloid peptides and intracellular neurocellular neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) formed by tau-proteins. What amyloid then does, is inflame these SPs which leads to brain changes. The NFTs eventually cause the microtubules to disintegrate, which destroys the cells cytoskeleton.

Development is gradual but indeed progressive, with early stages being focussed on memory problems. In the middle stage, speech difficulties develop due to recall problems leading to paraphasias (i.e., word substitutions), as slowly long-term memory is affected and neuropsychiatric changes become more evident. In this stage, about 30% of patients also develop delusional symptoms. In late-stage AD, language is reduced to simple phrases or single words, with extreme apathy / exhaustion being common from a neuropsychiatric point of view, as mobility deteriorates. The cause of death then, is usually an external factor: pressure ulcers, pneumonia etc.

A 70% heritability has been shown, with the most strongly connected gene being APOE-epsilon-4. Early-onset AD exists as a disease entity responsible for 1-2% of cases and is a highly polygenic autosomal dominant genetic condition.

Vascular dementia

Vascular dementia comes in multiple types, all of which are caused by restriction of blood flow due to strokes: subcortical vascular dementia, multi-infarct dementia, stroke-related dementia and mixed dementia. The subcortical type is caused by damage to small blood-vessels in the brain, the multi-infarct type is caused by a series of small strokes affecting several brain regions, the stroke-related type is caused by successive small strokes causing a gradual decline.

Typical is the step-wise decline over a period of 5-10 years. Because the damage is localised to the site of vascular damage, symptoms may vary between patients. However cognitive decline and memory impairment are typical. Being the second-most common form of dementia, it is responsible for 20-50% of all cases of dementia.

Lewy body dementia

This is an umbrella-term, covering two types of dementia: dementia with Lewy bodies and Parkinson's disease dementia. Typical are so-called Lewy-bodies which are spherical masses displacing other cell components of which there are two types: classical and cortical. The classical variant is strongly eosinophilic with a 10-nm halo of fibrils, while the cortical variant does not have the same halo. Typically, these bodies are found in the substantia nigra or the cortex.

Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB) is then marked by the deposition of these bodies in the cortex. It presents with fluctuating cognition, recurrent visual hallucinations, REM-sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) and parkinsonism. The RBD being typical, it often occurs in the pre-demential phase together with constipation and hyposmia i.e., a reduced sense of smell. DLB is notably not memory-focused but rather presents cognitively with problems in the domains of attentions, executive functions and visuospatial function with a typical patient being "easily distracted" with spells of agitation and confusion. The RBD symptom (i.e., loss of paralysis of muscles during sleep) is so commong that 95% of RBD patients have a Lewy-Body related autoposy. Furthermore, 80% of DLB patients have visual halluciations, which may already occur very early on in the course of disease. Other symptoms include sensitivity to antipsychotics, dysautonomia, hypersomnia, hyposmia and postural instability.

After the occurance of dementia, DLB and Parkinson's Disease Dementia are clinically similar. The difference notably, is the onset: PDD starts with motor-symptoms associated with PD and only develops dementia later. Notably, while still common, hallucinations and elsuions are less marked in PDD while Parkinsonisms are more common.

Similar to AD, the gene APO-episilon-4 has been shown to predispose to Lewy-body dementia.

Frontotemporal dementia

There exist many different subtypes of FD, but the three to know are: Pick's disease (i.e., behavioural variant), semantic primary progressive aphasia (svPPA) and nonfluent primary progressive aphasia (nfvPPA). The behavioural variant may present as either impulsive and disinhibited or apaethetic and listless; both of which are caused by tau-fibril inclusion-bodies. svSDD is characterised by loss of semantic understanding, while nfvSDD results in progressive difficulties in speech production.

In later stages, the subtypes start to overlap and all show marked differences in executive functioning and working memory with binge eating being an often-seen symptom. The first frontal-releaese sign to appear is the palmomental reflex.

Notably, but rarely, FD may occur in people with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) as a form of motor neuron disease.

A list of six neuropsychiatric characteristics exists for Pick's disease: disinhibition, apathy, loss of sympathy/empathy, perseveration, hyperorality, dysexecutive neuropsychological profile.

Huntington's dementia

There is two main subtypes of Huntington's disease: juvinile and classical. The juvinile variant sets on before 20 y/o and presents, in the early stages, with slow, Parkinson's disease-like movement disorder, while the classical variant, setting on between 30-50 y/o, presents with a typical triad of chorea, cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms. In about 50% of patients, the psychiatric symptoms appear first. As the disease progresses, chorea evolves into rigidity, writhing motions and abnormal posturing resulting in dystonia and even dysarthria. Demential symptoms only occur later: short-term memory deficits, later long-term memory difficulties and working memory decline. On the psychiatric side, anxiety, egocentrism, aggression and compulsion often appear.

Aetiologically, we're looking a genetic condition where the normal Huntingtin Gene (HTT) has a repeat component, which has a variable amount of repetitions. After a certain threshold, the product is no longer a normal huntingtin protein, but a mutant variant. Such a mutation is autosomal dominant and nearly fully penetrant. Juvinile HD is then caused by a very large amount of repeats. \

External causes

HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND)

A HIV-infection may cause a metabolic encephalopathy which is induced by the infection and fueled by immune activation of macrophages and microglia. While presenting similarly to AD, visuaspatial dysfunction and alexithymia (problems with emotional recognition) are characteristic for the condition. In terms of motor symptoms, early stages can show the return of the snout response.

Creutzfeldt-Jakob dementia

Caused, in 85% of cases, by infection with prions, CJD may also be autosomally dominant inherited. When contracted from infected meat, it may be referred to as mad cow disease. Clinically, it presents with progressive dementia, memory loss, ersonality changes and hallucinations. What sets it apart is myoclonus in 90% of patients; other involuntary movements may also occur. As coughing reflexes are suppressed, most affected people die 6 months after onset; from pneumonia.

Alcohol-related dementia

Here too, we see subtypes: alcohol-related frontal lobe damage (i.e., disinhibition, executive disfunction etc.), Wernicke encephalopathy and Korsakoff's psychosis. As for motor symptoms, heavy drinking leads to peripheral neuropathy and cerebellar ataxia and patients may be recognised by those symptoms. While those symptoms are caused by the effect of alcohol as a toxin per-se, the most famous alcohol-abuse syndromes described below are caused by alcohol-related malnutritionarry problems.

Wernicke's encephalopthy

Caused by a lack of vitiamin B1 (thiamin), it falls within a larger group including beriberi and Korsakoff's psychosis. Classically, symptoms are described as a triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia and confusion. Typically, the ophthalmoplegia presents as lateral nystagmus due to paralysis of the lateral rectus muscle.

Korsakoff's psychosis

Often coupled with Wernicke's encephalopathy into a single syndrome, it is characterised by the neuropsychiatric consequences of B1 (thiamin) deficiency: amnesia, deficitis in explicit memory, and confabulation. It may similarly occur in pregant women with hyperemesis gravidarum, or in the context of mercury poisoning.
