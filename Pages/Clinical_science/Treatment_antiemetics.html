Introduction to Antiemetics and GERD Medications

Anatomical and Physiological Foundations

The emetic reflex and gastroesophageal reflux share common anatomical pathways and neurochemical mediators, making their pharmacological management interrelated. The vomiting center, located in the medulla oblongata, receives inputs from:

  1. The chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ) in the area postrema (outside blood-brain barrier)
  2. The vestibular system (motion sickness)
  3. The peripheral visceral afferents from GI tract
  4. The cerebral cortex (psychogenic stimuli)

The lower esophageal sphincter (LES), a 3-4 cm segment of tonically contracted smooth muscle, serves as the primary barrier against GERD. Its pressure is influenced by:

  1. Hormonal factors (cholecystokinin, progesterone decrease tone)
  2. Neural control (vagal pathways via muscarinic receptors)
  3. Mechanical factors (hiatal hernia disrupts function)

Historical Perspective

The development of antiemetics paralleled advances in neuropharmacology. Phenothiazines (e.g., chlorpromazine), initially developed as antipsychotics in the 1950s, were found to have potent antiemetic effects through dopamine receptor blockade. The discovery of histamine H2-receptors by James Black in 1972 revolutionized GERD treatment, leading to cimetidine's development.

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Nausea and vomiting involve multiple neurotransmitter systems:

  1. Dopamine (D2): CTZ stimulation
  2. Serotonin (5-HT3): Especially in chemotherapy-induced vomiting
  3. Muscarinic (M1)/Histamine (H1): Motion sickness pathway
  4. Substance P (NK1): Central emetic pathway

GERD pathophysiology involves:

  1. Transient LES relaxations (TLESRs) (most common mechanism)
  2. Hypotensive LES (<15 mmHg)
  3. Delayed gastric emptying
  4. Esophageal mucosal resistance impairment

Antiemetics: Pharmacological Agents

5-HT3 Receptor Antagonists

Ondansetron, granisetron, palonosetron are first-line for chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting (CINV). They block serotonin receptors in the CTZ and peripheral vagal afferents. Palonosetron has a 40-hour half-life and allosteric binding properties, making it superior for delayed CINV.

NK1 Receptor Antagonists

Aprepitant, fosaprepitant, rolapitant target substance P in the central pattern generator. Used in combination with 5-HT3 antagonists and dexamethasone for highly emetogenic chemotherapy (e.g., cisplatin). They are potent CYP3A4 inhibitors requiring dose adjustments of concomitant medications.

Dopamine Antagonists

Prochlorperazine, metoclopramide, droperidol act at D2 receptors. Metoclopramide also has 5-HT4 agonist properties enhancing gastric emptying. High doses can cause extrapyramidal symptoms (acute dystonia, akathisia) via nigrostriatal pathway blockade.

Antihistamines/Anticholinergics

Diphenhydramine, meclizine, scopolamine prevent motion sickness by blocking vestibular H1/M1 receptors. Scopolamine patches provide 72-hour coverage but can cause blurred vision and urinary retention.

Cannabinoids

Dronabinol, nabilone act on CB1 receptors in the dorsal vagal complex. Used as refractory antiemetics but can cause euphoria, hypotension, and dependency.

Corticosteroids

Dexamethasone is a cornerstone of antiemetic regimens, particularly for delayed CINV. Mechanism may involve prostaglandin inhibition and blood-brain barrier permeability reduction.

GERD Medications: Pharmacological Management

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

Omeprazole, esomeprazole, lansoprazole, pantoprazole, rabeprazole, dexlansoprazole are irreversible inhibitors of H+/K+ ATPase. They require acidic activation and have delayed onset (2-3 days for full effect). Dexlansoprazole's dual-release formulation provides 24-hour coverage.

H2 Receptor Antagonists

Famotidine, ranitidine (withdrawn), cimetidine, nizatidine competitively block histamine at parietal cell H2 receptors. Less potent than PPIs but faster onset (1 hour). Cimetidine inhibits CYP450 (warfarin, theophylline interactions).

Antacids/Alginates

Calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide, sodium alginate provide immediate symptom relief by neutralizing acid and forming raft barriers. Magnesium salts cause diarrhea; aluminum causes constipation.

Potassium-Competitive Acid Blockers (P-CABs)

Vonoprazan (available in some countries) reversibly blocks H+/K+ ATPase without requiring acid activation. Faster onset and less dependent on CYP2C19 polymorphism than PPIs.

Prokinetics

Metoclopramide, domperidone, erythromycin enhance LES pressure and gastric emptying. Domperidone doesn't cross BBB (fewer EPS effects). Erythromycin acts as motilin agonist.

Baclofen

GABA-B agonist that reduces TLESRs. Limited by CNS side effects (drowsiness, dizziness).

Clinical Considerations and Special Populations

Antiemetic Selection by Etiology

Tailoring antiemetics to the underlying cause improves efficacy:

  1. Chemotherapy-induced: 5-HT3 antagonist + NK1 antagonist + dexamethasone
  2. Post-operative: 5-HT3 antagonist ± droperidol
  3. Motion sickness: Scopolamine patch ± antihistamine
  4. Pregnancy: Pyridoxine (B6) + doxylamine (Category A)
  5. Gastroparesis: Metoclopramide (limited duration due to EPS risk)

GERD Treatment Strategies

Step-up vs step-down approaches:

  1. Step-up: Lifestyle modifications → antacids → H2RA → PPI
  2. Step-down: Initial PPI → taper to lowest effective dose

Refractory GERD evaluation requires endoscopy, pH monitoring, and consideration of PPI pharmacokinetics (CYP2C19 polymorphisms affect metabolism).

Adverse Effects and Monitoring

PPIs:

  1. Long-term risks: Hypomagnesemia, B12 deficiency, osteoporosis, C. difficile infection
  2. Drug interactions: Clopidogrel (omeprazole/esomeprazole inhibit CYP2C19 activation)

Metoclopramide: Black box warning for tardive dyskinesia (>12 weeks use).
