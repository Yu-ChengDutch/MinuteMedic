Electrolyte imbalances

All electrodes play significant roles in homeostasis throughout the body. There are two ways elektrolyte imbalances may come to be:

  1. Consuming too little / too much electrolytes
  2. Excreting too little / too much electrolytes

The importance of elektrodes is based on their role in the maintenance of voltages across cell membranes. It is hence good to know that all electrolytes implicated in disorders are cations (+) except for chloride and phosphate, which are anions (-).

Increased loss may occur through a GI-route (diarrhea, laxatives, vomiting), a urinary route (kidney disorders) or a transdermal route (sweat). The latter mostly excretes potassium and sodium.

Sodium

The most common type of electrolyte imbalance is hyponatraemia which thus deserves a more in-depth treatment. There are three types of hyponatraemia, with associated aetiologies:

  1. Hypovolaemic hyponatraemia: Thiazide diuretic-use
  2. Euvolaemic hyponatraemia: SIADH or ACTH-deficiency
  3. Hypervolaemic hyponatraemia: CHF (Congestive Heart Failure)

Important is that the osmolality in these disorders is all decreased, which is what is expected in hyponatraemia. However, there exists also non-hypotonic hyponatraemia which is usually caused by hyperglycaemia which causes water to shift from the intra- to the extracellular fluid, diluting sodium.

Hypovolaemic hyponatraemia

Most commonly presenting in the elderly, further risk factors include low BMI, hypokalaemia and female gender. Examples of thiazide diurects include hydrochlorothizide, chlorthalidone and indapamide. They inhibit the reabsorption of sodium in the distal convoluted tubule of the nephron. Hence they stimulate natiuresis.

SIADH

"Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuresis" is a common complication of a wide variety of disorders and treatments, the most common of which are malignancy, pulmonary disorders, CNS disorders and medication. It has five essential diagnostic criteria:

  1. Plasma hypoosmolality
  2. Urine hyperconcentration
  3. Urinary hypernatraemia
  4. Clinical euvolaemia (i.e., no edema etc.)
  5. Exclusion of ACTH-deficiency or hypothyroidism

Congestive Heart Failure

Similar to liver cirrhosis, in case of CHF the mean arterial pressure falls which increases baroregulated vasopressin secretion, which in turns actives the RAAS-axis and leads to water retention.

The opposite, namely hypernatraemia, is not common in healthy people and is usually due to dehydration.

Symptomatology

Mild symptoms are shared between the two and involve the GI-system, namely: nausea, vomiting, fatigue and weakness. Hyponatraemia may present with more severe symptoms often involving the CNS, namely: seizures, coma and brain herniation (fatal).

Chloride

After sodium, chloride is the most common electrolyte in the body; with most of it entering the body together in the form of normal salt. It plays an important role in acids throughout the body, such as gastric acid (HCl) and is hence often inverse with bicarbonate which determines acid-base status.

Hyperchloraemia

Usually associated with excess intake (e.g., saltwater drowning) or fluid loss, it might also characteristically be caused by metabolic acidosis! However, patients often remain asymptomatic.

Hypochloraemia

Usually associated with excess loss (e.g., vomiting). Rarely, it is associated with chronic respiratory acidosis.

Magnesium

More than 90% of magnesium is stored intracellularly, with the vast majority being located in the bones. Hypermagnesaemia is largely only seen in magnesium-containing medication such as aripriprazole, methylphenidate and magnesium(hydr)oxide (e.g., antagel). Typically, it may cause decreased deep tendon reflexes and in severe cases respiratory failure, bradycardia and paralysis.

Potassium

Residing mainly intracellularly, potassium has a relatively low concentration in the blood stream.

Hyperkalaemia

Besides true hyperkalaemia, a type of faux-hyperkaelemia named haemolytic hyperkalaemia is due to breakdown of a sample. True hyperkalaemia however, is almost always caused by a renal problem. In terms of symptomatology, it is considered to the be the most dangerous elektrolyte disorder, as severe hyperkalaemia is associated with muscle cramps, numbness, tingling, absence of reflexes, paralysis and notably arrhythmias. Treatment is achieved using the stabilisation of cardiac cells (calcium perfusion), shift of potassium intracellularly (insulin perfusion), or removal of potassium from the body (haemodialysis or loop diuretics). On an EKG, a mild hyperkalaemia may show as peaked T-waves, while a severe hyperkalaemia causes progressive atrial paralysis (P-wave flattening) and eventually bradyarrthymias resulting in asystole.

Hypokalaemia

This is the most common electrolyte disorder and often occurs concurrently with hypomagnesemia. Both various forms of acidosis, as well as hyperaldosteronism are common culprits. Similar to hyperkalaemia, common mild symptoms are cramping and fatigue, while severe symptoms include arrhythmias with on the EKG ST-depression with eventual T-wave inversion.
