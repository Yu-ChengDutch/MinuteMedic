Introduction to Neuropathies

Anatomy of Peripheral Nerves

Peripheral nerves are composed of axons, Schwann cells, connective tissue, and blood vessels. The endoneurium surrounds individual axons, the perineurium bundles axons into fascicles, and the epineurium encases the entire nerve. Myelinated fibers are ensheathed by Schwann cells, which produce myelin, whereas unmyelinated fibers are embedded within Schwann cells without concentric wrapping.

Histology and Physiology

Motor neurons have cell bodies in the anterior horn of the spinal cord, while sensory neurons reside in the dorsal root ganglia. Autonomic fibers (sympathetic and parasympathetic) originate from the intermediolateral column and autonomic ganglia. Nerve conduction depends on ion channels (Na⁺, K⁺) and myelin integrity. Demyelination slows conduction velocity, whereas axonal degeneration reduces signal amplitude.

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Neuropathies arise from axonal degeneration (e.g., diabetes), demyelination (e.g., Guillain-Barré syndrome), or neuronopathy (e.g., sensory neuronopathy in Sjögren’s). Common mechanisms include ischemia, metabolic dysfunction, immune-mediated attack, and toxin accumulation.

Historical Context

The term "neuropathy" was coined in the 19th century, but descriptions of peripheral nerve disorders date back to ancient Egypt. Landmark discoveries include Guillain, Barré, and Strohl’s 1916 report on acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy and Charcot-Marie-Tooth’s 1886 description of hereditary motor-sensory neuropathy.

Inflammatory Neuropathy: Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

Aetiology

GBS is an immune-mediated disorder, often triggered by preceding infections (e.g., Campylobacter jejuni, CMV, EBV, or Mycoplasma pneumoniae). Molecular mimicry between microbial antigens (e.g., gangliosides GM1, GD1a) and peripheral nerve components drives autoantibody production.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

GBS has an incidence of 1–2/100,000/year, with a slight male predominance. Risk factors include recent gastroenteritis (especially C. jejuni), vaccinations (e.g., influenza), and surgery.

Pathophysiology

Autoantibodies target myelin (acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy, AIDP) or axonal membranes (acute motor axonal neuropathy, AMAN). Complement activation leads to macrophage infiltration, myelin stripping, and conduction block.

Symptoms

Ascending paralysis begins distally (e.g., foot weakness) and progresses proximally. Autonomic dysfunction (e.g., labile blood pressure, arrhythmias) and sensory symptoms (e.g., paresthesias) are common. Respiratory failure occurs in 25% due to diaphragm weakness.

Mnemonic: "GBS RISES" – Gait difficulty, Bilateral weakness, Sensory loss, Reflex loss, Infection preceding, Symmetric, Elevated CSF protein, Slow conduction velocity.

Diagnosis

Clinical criteria include progressive weakness in ≥2 limbs and areflexia. CSF analysis shows albuminocytologic dissociation (high protein, normal WBCs). Electrodiagnostic studies reveal prolonged distal latencies and conduction block.

Treatment

IV immunoglobulin (2 g/kg over 5 days) or plasmapheresis is first-line. Supportive care (e.g., mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure) is critical. Corticosteroids are ineffective.

Complications

Respiratory failure, dysautonomia, venous thromboembolism, and chronic fatigue. 10% of patients die, and 20% have permanent disability.

Polyneuropathies

Diabetic Polyneuropathy

Aetiology: Chronic hyperglycemia leads to advanced glycation end-products (AGEs), oxidative stress, and microvascular ischemia. Axonal degeneration predominates, affecting longest nerves first ("length-dependent").

Symptoms: Distal symmetric sensory loss ("stocking-glove"), neuropathic pain (burning, lancinating), and autonomic dysfunction (gastroparesis, erectile dysfunction).

Treatment: Tight glycemic control, pregabalin/gabapentin for pain, and TCAs/SNRIs (e.g., amitriptyline, duloxetine).

Mnemonic: "DIABETES" – Distal symmetric, Insidious onset, Axonal, Burning pain, Elevated glucose, Temperature insensitivity, Early autonomic signs, Slow progression.

Thiamine Deficiency (Beri-Beri)

Aetiology: Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency impairs ATP production (Krebs cycle dysfunction). Common in alcoholism, malnutrition, or bariatric surgery.

Symptoms: Wet beri-beri (high-output cardiac failure) and dry beri-beri (neuropathy: painful paresthesias, weakness). Wernicke’s encephalopathy (ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, confusion) may coexist.

Treatment: High-dose IV thiamine (500 mg TID), then oral maintenance.

Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease (CMT)

Aetiology: Inherited mutations (e.g., PMP22 duplication in CMT1A) cause demyelination (CMT1) or axonal loss (CMT2).

Symptoms: Distal wasting ("inverted champagne bottle" legs), foot deformities (pes cavus), and steppage gait.

Treatment: Supportive (orthotics, physical therapy). No disease-modifying therapy exists.
