Introduction, History and Epidemiology

Neuropathic pain is a complex and often chronic condition resulting from a lesion or disease of the somatosensory system, whether peripheral or central. It poses a significant clinical challenge as current conventional medications, such as pregabalin, gabapentin, duloxetine, and tricyclic antidepressants, provide substantial relief to less than 50% of patients.

Neuropathic pain encompasses a broad category of pain syndromes originating from a wide variety of peripheral or central disorders. The International Association for the Study of Pain (IASP) defines it as pain directly caused by a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system, a definition that replaced older ones to more precisely delineate its characteristics and mechanisms. The term also distinguishes it from other types of pain, such as nociceptive pain, which arises from actual or threatened damage to non-neural tissue. Historically, pain following nervous system injury has been recognised under various names, including nerve injury pain, neuralgia, deafferentation pain, neurogenic pain, and central pain. Early descriptions of heightened sensibility and conditions like causalgia were provided by Silas Weir Mitchell in 1872. The concepts of epicritic and protopathic sensitivity were introduced by Henry Head and Rivers in 1908, with Melzack and Wall later developing the influential gate control theory.

The overall prevalence of neuropathic pain in the general population is reported to be between 7% and 10%, accounting for 20–25% of individuals experiencing chronic pain. Central neuropathic pain (CNP), which stems from a lesion or disease of the central somatosensory nervous system, includes conditions such as central post-stroke pain (CPSP), pain after spinal cord injury (SCI), and pain associated with multiple sclerosis (MS). CPSP is the most prevalent form of CNP worldwide, affecting an estimated 11% of stroke patients, and up to 50% in cases of medullary, thalamic, and operculo-insular strokes. For individuals with SCI, neuropathic pain has a pooled prevalence of about 53%, predominantly CNP. In MS patients, neuropathic extremity pain, Lhermitte sign, and trigeminal neuralgia have prevalence estimates of 26%, 16%, and 3.8% respectively. Data on CNP after traumatic brain injury (TBI) are currently limited.

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

Neuropathic pain's pathophysiology is complex and involves ectopic activity in damaged nerve fibres and pathways, alongside peripheral and central sensitisation.
- Ectopic Activity and Hyperexcitability: Damaged peripheral or central nerve pathways, including the dorsal root ganglia (DRG), can generate abnormal spontaneous activity and lead to neuronal hyperexcitability, a key feature in both peripheral and central neuropathic pain.
- Sensitisation: This involves maladaptive plasticity within spinal and brain circuits associated with nociception and antinociception. A disrupted balance between excitation and inhibition in spinal circuits is a significant factor, potentially due to reduced expression of the potassium-chloride exporter KCC2, which diminishes inhibition.
- Glial Involvement: Non-neuronal cells, specifically microglia and astrocytes, play a central role. Injured peripheral afferents can release colony-stimulating factor 1 (CSF1), activating microglia, which then proliferate and release various pro-inflammatory mediators such as TNF, interleukins, fractalkine, and chemokines. Astrocytes are subsequently activated, further propagating the neuroinflammatory cascade and enhancing neuronal activity.
- Mitochondrial Dysfunction: This is a common underlying factor, observed in human conditions and animal models of diverse neuropathic pain types. It can result from direct nerve injury, chemotherapy, anti-HIV treatments, or high glucose levels in diabetic neuropathy, leading to energy deficits and cellular degeneration. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) production is closely linked to this dysfunction, and stabilising hypoxia-inducible factor 1 (HIF1α) has shown promise in suppressing ROS production.

Aetiologies of neuropathic pain are diverse:
- Peripheral Neuropathic Pain is often caused by painful peripheral neuropathies due to metabolic conditions (e.g., diabetes mellitus, alcohol), inflammatory conditions (e.g., post-herpetic neuralgia), or traumatic nerve injury. Other causes include Fabry disease, sodium channel gene mutations, autoimmune diseases, vasculitis, chemotherapy-induced neuropathy, and paraneoplastic syndromes.
- Central Neuropathic Pain can occur after a stroke, spinal cord injury, or in patients with multiple sclerosis or syringomyelia. Brain injury and neuroinflammatory conditions are also identified causes.
- Specific Conditions:
- -> Trigeminal Neuralgia typically involves unilateral, short, severe pain attacks in the face. It can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary (trigeminal neuropathy), often linked to a lesion or compression of the trigeminal nerve, sometimes due to postherpetic neuralgia or MS.
- -> Painful Diabetic Neuropathy (PDNP) is a common complication of diabetes mellitus.
- -> Postherpetic Neuralgia (PHN) results from Herpes Zoster virus reactivation, causing inflammation and necrosis in the affected ganglion and nerve root, leading to persistent pain, hyperaesthesia, and allodynia in the dermatomal area.
- -> Polyneuropathy refers to a usually symmetrical disorder of peripheral nerves, affecting multiple nerves, often distally first. Common causes include diabetes mellitus, HIV, and chemotherapy, though many cases are idiopathic.

Mixed pain syndromes, like lumbar or cervical radiculopathies, involve both neuropathic and non-neuropathic mechanisms.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing neuropathic pain is challenging due to the lack of a gold standard or specific biomarkers. Instead, a hierarchical grading system is used to determine the certainty of diagnosis, classifying it into possible, probable, and definite neuropathic pain.
- Possible Neuropathic Pain relies on a patient's history indicating a relevant lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system, with pain distribution being neuroanatomically plausible.
- Probable Neuropathic Pain adds a physical examination revealing sensory signs, such as deficits in mechanical or thermal perception, or hypersensitivity like allodynia, in a neuroanatomically plausible area. This level is typically sufficient for initiating specific treatment.
- Definite Neuropathic Pain requires confirmatory tests to verify the central somatosensory nervous system lesion or disease that explains the pain's spatial distribution, while ruling out other potential causes.

Clinical examination may reveal allodynia (pain from a normally non-painful stimulus), hyperalgesia (increased pain from a normally painful stimulus), or sensory loss. Patients often describe spontaneous pain as burning, shooting, pricking, pins and needles, squeezing, or freezing, sometimes with intermittent electric-shock-like paroxysms. Non-painful sensations like dysaesthesia (unpleasant abnormal sensation) and paraesthesia (non-unpleasant abnormal sensation) can also occur. Challenges in diagnosis include the dynamic nature of the nociceptive system and the subjective nature of symptoms. While sensory profiles have been identified, they may reflect neuropathy itself rather than specific pain mechanisms.

Treatment (Current and New Developments)

The management of neuropathic pain is primarily symptomatic, focusing on pain relief and improving quality of life, rather than treating the underlying cause. A multimodal approach is recommended, prioritising self-management strategies and patient education. Addressing underlying somatic causes like suboptimal glycaemic control or nutritional deficiencies is also advised. Pharmacotherapy has a modest role due to varied responses and common side effects.

Current Pharmacological Treatments (First-line):
- Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Such as amitriptyline (or nortriptyline for older patients), are strongly recommended and are often a first-line choice for polyneuropathy. They likely modulate descending pain control systems by inhibiting serotonin and noradrenaline reuptake, and have effects on sodium channels and opioid receptors.
- Serotonin Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs): Including duloxetine (and venlafaxine, often off-label), are also strongly recommended, with duloxetine preferred for painful diabetic neuropathy. Their mechanism is similar to TCAs, modulating descending pain pathways.
- Gabapentinoids: Gabapentin and pregabalin are strongly recommended. Pregabalin can be a first-line choice for patients with anxiety, while gabapentin is preferred for severe spasticity. They work by attenuating neuronal hyperexcitability via presynaptic calcium channels. Regulatory bodies recommend monitoring for misuse or abuse, and caution against co-administration with CNS depressants due to respiratory depression risk.
- Carbamazepine/Oxcarbazepine: These are first-line treatments for trigeminal neuralgia. Carbamazepine, however, has notable side effects, including severe skin reactions, and interacts with other medications due to liver enzyme induction.

Other Pharmacological Options:
- Topical Agents: Capsaicin 8% patches are weakly recommended for peripheral neuropathic pain, but not for trigeminal neuralgia due to eye risk. Lidocaine patches are used for postherpetic neuralgia.
- Botulinum Toxin Type A: Weakly recommended for peripheral neuropathic pain, and while some evidence suggests benefit in diabetic neuropathy, it's not a standard recommendation in Dutch guidelines.
- Tramadol and Opioids: Some evidence of effect, but generally not recommended for chronic non-cancer pain.
- Sodium Channel Blockers: While evidence is inconclusive for general use, some, like carbamazepine and oxcarbazepine, are specifically effective in subgroups such as trigeminal neuralgia patients.

Combination Therapy: When monotherapy is insufficient, combining two agents with different mechanisms (e.g., an antidepressant and an anti-epileptic) is often considered, typically under specialist care.

Cannabis-based Medicines (CbMs) and Medical Cannabis (MC):
- These are considered potential treatment options for chronic neuropathic pain. They are available in various forms, including licensed medications, magistral preparations like dronabinol, and herbal cannabis with defined content of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and/or cannabidiol (CBD).
- Systematic reviews present low-quality evidence and divergent conclusions regarding their efficacy, ranging from "not effective" to "clinically meaningful benefit".
- Clinically relevant side effects, particularly central nervous system and psychiatric disorders, have been reported.
- Recommendations from medical associations vary widely, from negative to third-line treatment. Some acknowledge their common use in practice, justifying a third-line recommendation.
- There's a recognised lack of robust evidence for efficacy and safety, with concerns about long-term adverse effects based on recreational use. The evidence is also noted to be biased due to industry sponsorship and patient populations (cannabis-naïve vs. recreational users).
- No single CbM or MC preparation is favoured by current evidence.
- Guidance for clinical use includes a comprehensive clinical evaluation, integration into multimodal treatment plans, shared decision-making, careful dose adjustment, and long-term monitoring of effectiveness and adverse events. There remains much uncertainty about their true place as a therapeutic option, underscoring the need for more rigorous research.

New Developments and Challenges:
- Despite decades of research, surprisingly few novel, effective, and safe treatments have been introduced, and most have arisen from empirical observation rather than targeted translational approaches.
- Future directions include investigating neuroinflammation using advanced imaging (diffusion-weighted MRI, PET) and developing neuromodulation therapies to address aberrant neural states.
- The field of precision medicine in pain is developing but faces challenges due to insufficient biological understanding of drug mechanisms and pain phenotypes.

Prognosis

Neuropathic pain remains a difficult-to-treat condition, representing a significant unmet medical need. The response to pharmacological treatments is often modest, and many patients do not achieve sufficient pain relief at tolerable doses. Side effects are also common, impacting the overall benefit. For conditions like postherpetic neuralgia, pain can be persistent, lasting for months or even returning after pain-free periods. The long-term safety and efficacy of emerging treatments, particularly cannabis-based medicines, still require more robust real-world data and rigorous research to ascertain their true place and potential for abuse or dependence. Ultimately, a clearer understanding of potential benefits versus harms requires continued and thorough scientific investigation.
