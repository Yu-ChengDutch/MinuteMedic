Introduction to Cardiac Arrhythmias

Anatomical Foundations

The cardiac conduction system is a specialized network of myocardial cells responsible for generating and conducting electrical impulses that coordinate heart contractions. The system consists of:

  1. Sinoatrial (SA) node: The primary pacemaker located at the junction of the superior vena cava and right atrium, generating impulses at 60-100 bpm.
  2. Atrioventricular (AV) node: Situated in the interatrial septum near the coronary sinus ostium, delaying impulse transmission to allow atrial contraction before ventricular activation.
  3. Bundle of His: The only electrical connection between atria and ventricles, running through the fibrous skeleton.
  4. Bundle branches: The right bundle branch and left bundle branch (with anterior and posterior fascicles) that conduct impulses down the interventricular septum.
  5. Purkinje fibers: Terminal conduction network that rapidly distributes impulses throughout ventricular myocardium.

Electrophysiological Principles

Cardiac myocytes maintain a resting membrane potential of -90 mV due to high potassium permeability. Action potentials occur in five phases:

  1. Phase 0: Rapid depolarization due to sodium influx (fast sodium channels).
  2. Phase 1: Early repolarization from transient potassium efflux.
  3. Phase 2: Plateau phase maintained by calcium influx (L-type calcium channels).
  4. Phase 3: Repolarization from potassium efflux.
  5. Phase 4: Resting potential maintained by Na+/K+ ATPase.

These properties differ between pacemaker cells (SA/AV nodes) and non-pacemaker cells (atria, ventricles), explaining their distinct roles in rhythm generation versus contraction.

Historical Perspective

The understanding of arrhythmias evolved through key milestones:

  1. 1906: Einthoven's electrocardiograph enabled graphical representation of cardiac electrical activity.
  2. 1920s: Lewis's experimental work mapped conduction pathways.
  3. 1969: Scherlag developed His bundle electrocardiography.
  4. 1980s: Advent of catheter ablation revolutionized treatment.

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Arrhythmias arise from three fundamental mechanisms:

  1. Abnormal impulse formation: Enhanced automaticity or triggered activity (early/late afterdepolarizations).
  2. Abnormal impulse conduction: Re-entry circuits requiring unidirectional block and slow conduction.
  3. Combined disorders: Most clinically significant arrhythmias involve both mechanisms.

Supraventricular Arrhythmias

Sinus Tachycardia

Sinus tachycardia is defined as a sinus rhythm with rate >100 bpm in adults. The P wave morphology remains normal (upright in II, III, aVF) but occurs more frequently. Common etiologies include:

  1. Physiological: Exercise, anxiety, pain
  2. Pharmacological: Sympathomimetics, anticholinergics, caffeine
  3. Pathological: Fever, anemia, hyperthyroidism, hypovolemia, heart failure

The pathophysiology involves increased automaticity of the SA node due to enhanced sympathetic tone or reduced parasympathetic influence. Clinical significance depends on underlying cause - while usually compensatory, persistent tachycardia can reduce coronary perfusion time and increase myocardial oxygen demand.

Management

Treatment focuses on the underlying condition rather than the rhythm itself:

  1. Address precipitating factors (pain, fever, anemia)
  2. Beta-blockers (e.g., metoprolol) if symptomatic and no contraindications
  3. Calcium channel blockers (e.g., verapamil) as second-line

Sinus Bradycardia

Sinus bradycardia is a sinus rhythm with rate <60 bpm. Normal P wave morphology and PR interval are maintained. Causes include:

  1. Physiological: Athletes, vagal stimulation, sleep
  2. Pharmacological: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin
  3. Pathological: Hypothyroidism, hypothermia, increased intracranial pressure

The pathophysiology involves decreased automaticity of the SA node due to increased parasympathetic tone or intrinsic nodal dysfunction. Symptoms range from none to fatigue, dizziness, syncope, or heart failure depending on cerebral/perfusion impact.

Management

Asymptomatic bradycardia requires no treatment. Symptomatic cases may need:

  1. Discontinuation of offending agents
  2. Atropine (0.5mg IV) for acute symptomatic bradycardia
  3. Pacemaker implantation for persistent symptomatic bradycardia

Atrial Fibrillation

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is characterized by chaotic, rapid atrial activation (400-600 bpm) with irregular ventricular response. The ECG shows:

  1. Absent P waves
  2. Irregularly irregular R-R intervals
  3. Narrow QRS complexes (unless pre-existing conduction abnormality)
  4. Possible fibrillatory waves (fine or coarse)

Etiologies include:

  1. Cardiac: Hypertension, heart failure, valvular disease (especially mitral), CAD
  2. Non-cardiac: Hyperthyroidism, alcohol ("holiday heart"), pulmonary disease
  3. Idiopathic: Lone AF in younger patients

The pathophysiology involves multiple re-entrant wavelets in the atria due to:

  1. Atrial structural remodeling (fibrosis, dilation)
  2. Electrical remodeling (shortened refractory periods)
  3. Triggering foci (often pulmonary vein origins)

Management

Management focuses on three pillars:

  1. Rate control: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin
  2. Rhythm control: Cardioversion (electrical or chemical), antiarrhythmics (flecainide, amiodarone)
  3. Thromboembolism prevention: CHA2DS2-VASc score guides anticoagulation (warfarin, DOACs)

Atrial Flutter

Atrial flutter is a macroreentrant atrial tachycardia (typically 250-350 bpm) with characteristic "sawtooth" flutter waves on ECG. The ventricular rate depends on AV node conduction (often 2:1 or 4:1 block).

Types include:

  1. Typical (isthmus-dependent): Counterclockwise (common) or clockwise reentry around tricuspid annulus
  2. Atypical: Non-isthmus dependent circuits (scar-related)

Clinical features are similar to AF but may be more symptomatic at onset due to rapid ventricular rates. Management includes:

  1. Rate control (more difficult than AF due to persistent 2:1 conduction)
  2. Anticoagulation (similar stroke risk to AF)
  3. Catheter ablation (high success rate for typical flutter)

Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)

PSVT refers to episodic, regular narrow-complex tachycardias (140-250 bpm) with abrupt onset/termination. Mechanisms include:

  1. AV nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT): Most common (60%), uses fast and slow AV nodal pathways
  2. AV reentrant tachycardia (AVRT): Uses AV node and accessory pathway (e.g., WPW)
  3. Atrial tachycardia: Focal automaticity or reentry within atria

ECG features include:

  1. Regular narrow QRS (unless aberrant conduction)
  2. P waves often buried in QRS (AVNRT) or shortly after (AVRT)
  3. Sudden onset/termination

Management

Acute termination maneuvers:

  1. Vagal maneuvers (carotid sinus massage, Valsalva)
  2. Adenosine (6-12mg IV rapid push)
  3. Calcium channel blockers (verapamil) or beta-blockers

Long-term prevention:

  1. Catheter ablation (curative for AVNRT/AVRT)
  2. Antiarrhythmics if ablation contraindicated

Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome

WPW is characterized by an accessory pathway (bundle of Kent) connecting atria and ventricles, creating:

  1. Preexcitation: Early ventricular activation via accessory pathway (short PR, delta wave)
  2. Tachyarrhythmias: Orthodromic AVRT (most common), antidromic AVRT, or AF with rapid conduction

ECG findings in sinus rhythm:

  1. Short PR interval (<120ms)
  2. Delta wave (slurred QRS upstroke)
  3. Wide QRS (>120ms)
  4. Secondary ST-T changes

Dangerous features include:

  1. History of rapid AF (>250 bpm)
  2. Multiple accessory pathways
  3. Short accessory pathway refractory period

Management

Acute treatment depends on tachycardia type:

  1. Orthodromic AVRT: Adenosine, procainamide
  2. Antidromic AVRT/AF: Procainamide, ibutilide (avoid AV node blockers)

Definitive treatment is catheter ablation of accessory pathway.
