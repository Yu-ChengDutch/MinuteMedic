Introduction to Food-Borne Infections

Food-borne infections are illnesses caused by ingestion of contaminated food or water containing pathogenic microorganisms or their toxins. These infections primarily affect the gastrointestinal tract, though some can have systemic manifestations.

Relevant Anatomy

The gastrointestinal tract has several defense mechanisms against food-borne pathogens: gastric acid, intestinal motility, gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), and the intestinal microbiome. The small intestine (particularly duodenum and jejunum) is most commonly affected due to its large surface area and role in nutrient absorption.

Historical Context

The link between contaminated food and illness was first scientifically established by John Snow in 1854 during the London cholera outbreak. Modern food safety practices stem from this understanding.

Pathophysiology

Pathogens cause disease through three main mechanisms:

  1. Mucosal invasion (e.g., Salmonella, Shigella)
  2. Toxin production (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Clostridium perfringens)
  3. Cytotoxic effects (e.g., EHEC, Campylobacter)

Epidemiology

Food-borne illnesses cause approximately 600 million cases worldwide annually. In developed countries, norovirus and Campylobacter are most common, while in developing regions, enterotoxigenic E. coli and Salmonella predominate.

1. Norovirus Infection

Aetiology

Norovirus is a non-enveloped, single-stranded RNA virus of the Caliciviridae family. It's the most common cause of viral gastroenteritis worldwide.

Pathophysiology

The virus infects enterocytes in the small intestine, causing villous blunting and increased intestinal permeability. It also stimulates the enteric nervous system, leading to secretory diarrhea.

Presentation

Patients typically present with:

  1. Acute onset vomiting (often projectile) and watery diarrhea
  2. Low-grade fever
  3. Abdominal cramps
  4. Symptoms lasting 24-72 hours

Diagnosis

Primarily clinical, but can be confirmed by RT-PCR of stool samples during outbreaks.

Therapy

Supportive care with oral rehydration. Antiemetics may be used for severe vomiting. No specific antiviral exists.

Mnemonic: NORO - No Oral tolerance, Rapid onset, Outbreaks common

2. Salmonella Enteritis

Aetiology

Salmonella enterica serotypes (especially Typhimurium and Enteritidis) are gram-negative rods. Common sources include undercooked poultry, eggs, and dairy.

Pathophysiology

Salmonella invades M cells in Peyer's patches, then replicates within macrophages. This triggers IL-8 release, causing neutrophil recruitment and inflammation.

Presentation

Classic presentation includes:

  1. Fever (often high-grade)
  2. Watery diarrhea that may become bloody
  3. Abdominal cramps
  4. Symptoms lasting 4-7 days

Diagnosis

Stool culture is gold standard. Blood cultures may be positive in systemic cases.

Therapy

Antibiotics (ciprofloxacin or azithromycin) are reserved for severe cases or immunocompromised patients due to risk of prolonging carrier state.

Mnemonic: SALMONELLA - Severe fever, Abdominal pain, Lasts 4-7 days, Macrophage invasion, Often from poultry/eggs, No antibiotics usually, Enteric fever possible, Look for bloody stool, Lab culture needed, Avoid in immunocompromised

3. Campylobacter Jejuni Infection

Aetiology

Campylobacter jejuni is a gram-negative, spiral-shaped, microaerophilic bacterium. Main sources are undercooked poultry and unpasteurized milk.

Pathophysiology

The bacterium produces cytolethal distending toxin (CDT), which causes cell cycle arrest and apoptosis. It also invades intestinal epithelium, causing inflammation.

Presentation

Characteristic features include:

  1. Prodrome of fever, headache, myalgia (1-2 days before GI symptoms)
  2. Severe colicky abdominal pain
  3. Watery diarrhea progressing to bloody stools
  4. Symptoms lasting 2-10 days

Complications

May trigger Guillain-Barré syndrome (2-3 weeks post-infection) due to molecular mimicry between bacterial lipooligosaccharides and human gangliosides.

Diagnosis

Stool culture on selective media (requires microaerophilic conditions).

Therapy

Azithromycin or fluoroquinolones (though resistance is increasing). Early treatment may reduce duration.

4. Escherichia coli (EHEC) Infection

Aetiology

Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (serotype O157:H7 most common) produces Shiga toxin. Sources include undercooked beef, unpasteurized milk, and contaminated produce.

Pathophysiology

Shiga toxin binds globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) receptors on endothelial cells, causing:

  1. Inhibition of protein synthesis
  2. Endothelial damage
  3. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia

Presentation

Classic triad:

  1. Bloody diarrhea (often without fever)
  2. Severe abdominal pain
  3. Possible progression to HUS (hemolytic uremic syndrome)

Diagnosis

Stool culture on sorbitol-MacConkey agar (O157:H7 doesn't ferment sorbitol). Shiga toxin immunoassay is more sensitive.

Therapy

Supportive care only. Antibiotics are contraindicated as they may increase Shiga toxin release. Monitor for HUS (CBC, creatinine, LDH, haptoglobin).

Mnemonic: EHEC - Expect bloody diarrhea, HUS risk, Endothelial damage, Contraindicated antibiotics

5. Listeria Monocytogenes Infection

Aetiology

Listeria monocytogenes is a gram-positive, facultative intracellular bacterium. Common sources include soft cheeses, deli meats, and unpasteurized dairy.

Pathophysiology

Listeria crosses intestinal epithelium using internalin proteins, then spreads cell-to-cell via actin polymerization ("actin rockets"). It can cross blood-brain and placental barriers.

Presentation

Two main syndromes:

  1. Gastroenteritis (fever, diarrhea, vomiting) in healthy adults
  2. Invasive disease (meningitis, septicemia) in pregnant women, neonates, elderly, immunocompromised

Diagnosis

Blood or CSF culture (stool culture not reliable). Gram stain shows gram-positive rods with tumbling motility.

Therapy

Ampicillin (often with gentamicin for synergy). TMP-SMX for penicillin-allergic patients.

Mnemonic: LISTERIA - Loves soft cheese, Immunocompromised at risk, Spreads via actin, Transplacental infection, Elderly vulnerable, Rods on gram stain, Intracellular pathogen, Ampicillin treatment

Bonus: Cat-Scratch Disease (Bartonella henselae)

Aetiology

Bartonella henselae is a gram-negative bacterium transmitted via cat scratches/bites or possibly fleas. Not strictly food-borne but included due to GI manifestations.

Pathophysiology

Bacteria infect erythrocytes and endothelial cells, causing vascular proliferation and granuloma formation.

Presentation

Classic triad:

  1. Regional lymphadenopathy (often axillary/cervical)
  2. Fever
  3. History of cat exposure

GI manifestations include hepatic/splenic granulomas (seen as hypoechoic lesions on ultrasound).

Diagnosis

Serology (IgG titers) or PCR. Warthin-Starry silver stain can identify bacteria in tissue.

Therapy

Typically self-limited in immunocompetent patients. Azithromycin may reduce lymph node size. Severe cases (e.g., hepatosplenic) require doxycycline + rifampin.

Mnemonic: CAT SCRATCH - Cervical/axillary nodes, Azithromycin sometimes, Tender lymphadenopathy, Silver stain shows bacteria, Cat exposure, Resolves spontaneously, Atypical presentations possible, Three-week incubation, Culture difficult, Hepatosplenic lesions possible
