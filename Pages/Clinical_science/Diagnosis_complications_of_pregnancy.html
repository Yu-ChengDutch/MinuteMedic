Introduction to Pregnancy Complications

Pregnancy represents a profound physiological adaptation where the maternal body undergoes significant changes to support fetal development. Understanding normal pregnancy physiology is essential to recognize and manage complications when they arise. The uterus expands from about 70g to 1100g at term, with blood flow increasing from 50 mL/min to 500-800 mL/min. The cardiovascular system adapts with increased blood volume (40-50%), cardiac output (30-50%), and decreased systemic vascular resistance.

The placenta, the critical interface between mother and fetus, develops from trophoblastic tissue and is fully formed by 20 weeks. It functions as an endocrine organ (producing hCG, progesterone, estrogen, human placental lactogen), as well as facilitating nutrient/waste exchange. Placental dysfunction underlies many pregnancy complications.

Epidemiology of Pregnancy Complications

Globally, about 10-15% of pregnancies experience significant complications. The most common include:

  1. Hypertensive disorders (5-10% of pregnancies)
  2. Gestational diabetes (3-9% depending on population)
  3. Postpartum hemorrhage (1-5%)
  4. Placental disorders (1-2% for placenta previa)

Risk factors span across maternal age extremes (<18 or >35), pre-existing conditions (hypertension, diabetes), multifetal gestation, and prior obstetric complications. Socioeconomic factors and access to prenatal care significantly impact outcomes.

(Pre)Eclampsia: Pathophysiology and Management

Preeclampsia is a multisystem disorder characterized by new-onset hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg) and proteinuria (≥300 mg/24h) or end-organ dysfunction after 20 weeks gestation. It affects 2-8% of pregnancies globally and remains a leading cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity/mortality.

Pathophysiology

The central pathology involves abnormal placentation leading to reduced uteroplacental perfusion. Incomplete remodeling of spiral arteries results in placental ischemia/hypoxia, releasing anti-angiogenic factors (sFlt-1, soluble endoglin) that cause widespread endothelial dysfunction. This leads to:

  1. Vasoconstriction (hypertension, reduced organ perfusion)
  2. Increased vascular permeability (edema, proteinuria)
  3. Hypercoagulability (microthrombi, HELLP syndrome)

Mnemonic: "PREECLAMPSIA"

Proteinuria Renal dysfunction Elevated BP End-organ damage CNS symptoms Liver enzymes elevated Abnormal placentation Multi-system Placental factors Severe outcomes IUGR Angiogenic imbalance

Clinical Features

Preeclampsia can present with a spectrum from mild to life-threatening:

  1. Hypertension: Typically develops after 20 weeks (often 3rd trimester)
  2. Proteinuria: May be absent in 10-15% of cases (non-proteinuric preeclampsia)
  3. Neurological: Headaches, visual changes, hyperreflexia, seizures (eclampsia)
  4. Hepatic: RUQ/epigastric pain, elevated transaminases (may progress to HELLP)
  5. Renal: Oliguria, elevated creatinine, uric acid
  6. Hematologic: Thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic hemolysis

Diagnosis

Diagnosis requires blood pressure criteria (systolic ≥140 or diastolic ≥90 mmHg on two occasions ≥4 hours apart) PLUS either:

  1. Proteinuria (≥300 mg/24h or protein:creatinine ratio ≥0.3)
  2. OR evidence of end-organ dysfunction (thrombocytopenia, elevated creatinine, liver enzymes, pulmonary edema, new-onset cerebral/visual symptoms)

Management

Definitive treatment is delivery, but timing depends on gestational age and severity:

  1. Mild preeclampsia at ≥37 weeks: Deliver
  2. Mild preeclampsia <37 weeks: Close monitoring, deliver for worsening
  3. Severe preeclampsia at ≥34 weeks: Deliver
  4. Severe preeclampsia <34 weeks: Consider corticosteroids for lung maturity and magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection if continuing pregnancy

Magnesium sulfate is the drug of choice for seizure prophylaxis (4-6g IV load then 1-2g/hr maintenance). Antihypertensives (labetalol, nifedipine, hydralazine) are used for severe hypertension (SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥110).

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

Gestational diabetes is defined as glucose intolerance first recognized during pregnancy, affecting 3-9% of pregnancies depending on population and diagnostic criteria. It results from the diabetogenic effects of placental hormones (human placental lactogen, progesterone, cortisol) exacerbating underlying insulin resistance.

Pathophysiology

Normal pregnancy induces progressive insulin resistance (up to 50-60% reduction in insulin sensitivity by late pregnancy) to ensure glucose availability for the fetus. In GDM, maternal β-cell compensation is inadequate, leading to:

  1. Maternal hyperglycemia: Especially postprandial hyperglycemia
  2. Fetal hyperinsulinemia: Fetal pancreas responds to maternal glucose crossing placenta
  3. Increased fetal growth: Insulin acts as primary fetal growth hormone

Screening and Diagnosis

Two approaches are commonly used:

  1. One-step (IADPSG): 75g OGTT at 24-28 weeks, diagnose if any value abnormal:
    1. Fasting ≥92 mg/dL
    2. 1-hour ≥180 mg/dL
    3. 2-hour ≥153 mg/dL
  2. Two-step (ACOG):
    1. 50g glucose challenge (non-fasting), 1-hour ≥130-140 mg/dL →
    2. 100g OGTT, diagnose if ≥2 values abnormal:
      1. Fasting ≥95
      2. 1-hour ≥180
      3. 2-hour ≥155
      4. 3-hour ≥140

Complications

Poorly controlled GDM increases risks for:

  1. Fetal macrosomia (birth weight >4000g): Risk of shoulder dystocia, birth trauma
  2. Neonatal hypoglycemia: Due to fetal hyperinsulinemia after cord clamping
  3. Preterm birth: Often iatrogenic due to complications
  4. Preeclampsia: 2-4 fold increased risk
  5. Future diabetes: 50% risk of developing T2DM within 10 years

Mnemonic: "SWEET BABY" for GDM complications

Shoulder dystocia Weight gain (excessive) Eclampsia Early delivery Trauma (birth) Big baby Acidosis (neonatal) Blood sugar issues (neonatal) Years later diabetes (maternal)

Management

Treatment aims to maintain normoglycemia (fasting <95, 1-hour postprandial <140, 2-hour <120):

  1. Medical nutrition therapy: Carbohydrate-controlled diet (175g/day minimum)
  2. Exercise: Moderate physical activity improves insulin sensitivity
  3. Monitoring: Self-blood glucose monitoring 4x daily (fasting + 1-2h post meals)
  4. Pharmacologic therapy if targets not met in 1-2 weeks:
    1. Insulin (gold standard): No placental transfer
    2. Metformin: Crosses placenta but no clear fetal harm
    3. Glyburide: 2nd line, may increase macrosomia risk

Delivery timing is typically 39-40 weeks if well-controlled, earlier if complications arise. Postpartum, 75g OGTT should be performed at 4-12 weeks to assess glucose tolerance.

Placenta Previa

Placenta previa occurs when the placenta implants over or near the internal cervical os, affecting approximately 0.3-0.5% of pregnancies at term. The condition classically presents with painless vaginal bleeding in the second or third trimester.

Classification

Four types based on placental relationship to internal os:

  1. Complete previa: Placenta completely covers internal os
  2. Partial previa: Placenta partially covers internal os
  3. Marginal previa: Placental edge reaches margin of internal os
  4. Low-lying placenta: Placenta within 2cm of but not reaching internal os

Early in pregnancy, many placentas appear "low-lying" due to differential growth of the uterus (trophotropism). By term, only ~10% of early findings persist as true previa.

Risk Factors

Factors associated with abnormal placentation:

  1. Prior cesarean delivery (risk increases with number of prior sections)
  2. Multiparity
  3. Advanced maternal age (>35 years)
  4. Multiple gestation
  5. Smoking
  6. Uterine abnormalities (fibroids, prior surgery)

Clinical Presentation

The classic presentation is sudden, painless, bright red vaginal bleeding, often occurring:

  1. First episode typically in late 2nd or early 3rd trimester
  2. May be provoked by intercourse or cervical examination
  3. Bleeding often stops spontaneously but tends to recur
  4. Uterus typically soft and non-tender
  5. Fetal status usually normal unless significant maternal hypotension

Mnemonic: "PREVIA" for placenta previa features

Painless bleeding Red blood Early 3rd trimester common Vaginal delivery contraindicated Internal os covered Avoid cervical exam

Diagnosis

Transvaginal ultrasound is the gold standard (98% accuracy):

  1. Should be performed if bleeding occurs after 20 weeks
  2. Transabdominal ultrasound has higher false positive/negative rates
  3. MRI may be helpful in complex cases (e.g., placenta accreta spectrum)

Digital cervical examination is contraindicated if previa suspected due to risk of provoking hemorrhage.

Management

Depends on gestational age, bleeding severity, and maternal/fetal status:

  1. Asymptomatic previa:
    1. Serial ultrasounds to monitor placental location
    2. Pelvic rest (no intercourse, avoid cervical exams)
    3. Consider corticosteroids if delivery likely before 37 weeks
  2. Symptomatic previa (bleeding):
    1. Hospitalization for significant bleeding
    2. Blood transfusion if hemodynamically unstable
    3. Tocolytics may be considered if contractions present (controversial)
    4. Delivery if:
      1. Life-threatening hemorrhage
      2. Fetal distress
      3. Term gestation (37+ weeks)

Cesarean delivery is required for complete/partial previa. The optimal timing is typically 36-37 weeks after confirming lung maturity or giving corticosteroids.

Abruptio Placentae

Placental abruption (abruptio placentae) is the premature separation of a normally implanted placenta from the uterine wall after 20 weeks gestation but before delivery, occurring in 0.4-1% of pregnancies. It is a leading cause of vaginal bleeding in late pregnancy and can result in significant maternal and fetal morbidity.

Pathophysiology

The primary event is hemorrhage into the decidua basalis, leading to separation of the placenta from the uterine wall. This can result from:

  1. Vascular disruption: Chronic hypertension, preeclampsia, cocaine use
  2. Mechanical factors: Trauma, rapid uterine decompression (ROM in polyhydramnios)
  3. Thrombophilia: Inherited or acquired hypercoagulable states

Bleeding may be revealed (external via cervix) or concealed (retroplacental, more dangerous). Severe cases can lead to Couvelaire uterus (blood infiltrating myometrium) and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).

Risk Factors

Major risk factors include:

  1. Hypertensive disorders (most significant risk factor)
  2. Prior abruption (recurrence risk 5-17%)
  3. Trauma (motor vehicle accidents, domestic violence)
  4. Smoking (2.5x increased risk)
  5. Cocaine use (vasospasm)
  6. PPROM (especially with infection)
  7. Thrombophilias (Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome)

Clinical Presentation

The classic triad is vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, and fetal distress, though presentation varies:

  1. Bleeding: May be absent in concealed abruption (20% cases)
  2. Uterine hypertonus: "Woody" consistency, tetanic contractions
  3. Abdominal pain: Sudden onset, constant (not intermittent like labor)
  4. Fetal distress: Late decelerations, bradycardia, or demise
  5. Maternal shock: Out of proportion to visible blood loss in concealed abruption

Mnemonic: "ABRUPTIO" for placental abruption features

Abdominal pain Bleeding (may be concealed) Rigid uterus Uterine tenderness Preterm labor Trauma/HTN risk factors IUFD risk Outcome poor if severe

Diagnosis

Primarily a clinical diagnosis with supporting evidence from:

  1. Ultrasound: Sensitivity only 25% for detection (retroplacental clot may be seen)
  2. Laboratory tests:
    1. Decreased fibrinogen (often <200 mg/dL in severe cases)
    2. Elevated D-dimer
    3. Thrombocytopenia
    4. Prolonged PT/PTT in DIC
  3. Fetal monitoring: Non-reassuring patterns common

Management

Depends on gestational age and severity:

  1. Immediate delivery indicated for:
    1. Severe abruption with fetal distress (category II/III tracing)
    2. Maternal hemodynamic instability
    3. Term gestation (≥37 weeks)
  2. Vaginal delivery preferred if mother and fetus stable
  3. Cesarean delivery if:
    1. Fetal distress with viable fetus
    2. Maternal indications (severe hemorrhage, DIC)
  4. Expectant management may be considered for:
    1. Mild abruption at <34 weeks with stable maternal/fetal status
    2. Administer corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity

Supportive care includes IV fluids, blood transfusion if needed, and monitoring for DIC. Rhogam should be given to Rh-negative women.

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

Postpartum hemorrhage is defined as blood loss ≥500 mL after vaginal delivery or ≥1000 mL after cesarean, or any amount causing hemodynamic instability. It is the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, accounting for 25% of maternal deaths.

Classification

PPH can be categorized by timing and etiology:

  1. Primary (early) PPH: Within 24 hours of delivery (most common)
  2. Secondary (late) PPH: 24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum

The "4 T's" mnemonic organizes major causes:

  1. Tone: Uterine atony (70% of cases)
  2. Trauma: Genital tract lacerations, hematomas
  3. Tissue: Retained placenta, clots
  4. Thrombin: Coagulopathy (inherited or acquired)

Risk Factors

Antepartum and intrapartum factors increase PPH risk:

  1. Uterine overdistension (multiple gestation, polyhydramnios, macrosomia)
  2. Prolonged labor (especially 3rd stage >30 minutes)
  3. Chorioamnionitis
  4. Placental abnormalities (previa, accreta, abruption)
  5. Coagulopathy (preeclampsia, HELLP, inherited disorders)
  6. Medications (magnesium sulfate, tocolytics)

Clinical Presentation

Signs vary by etiology but may include:

  1. Excessive bleeding: Soaking >1 pad/hour, blood pooling
  2. Signs of shock: Tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, oliguria
  3. Boggy uterus (with atony) or firm uterus with continued bleeding (trauma)
  4. Perineal/vaginal hematoma (with trauma)

Mnemonic: "HEMORRHAGE" for PPH management steps

Help (activate team) Estimate blood loss Massage uterus Oxytocin Remove clots Repair lacerations Hemodynamic support Advanced measures (tamponade, surgery) Give blood products Ensure monitoring

Management

Treatment follows a stepwise approach:

  1. Initial measures:
    1. Call for help (activate massive transfusion protocol if needed)
    2. Bimanual uterine massage
    3. Oxytocin (first-line, 40 units in 1L NS at 200 mL/hr)
    4. Examine for/repair lacerations
  2. Second-line uterotonics if bleeding continues:
    1. Methylergonovine (0.2mg IM, contraindicated in HTN)
    2. Carboprost (Hemabate, 250mcg IM, avoid in asthma)
    3. Misoprostol (800mcg PR)
  3. Mechanical/surgical interventions:
    1. Uterine tamponade (Bakri balloon, packing)
    2. Uterine artery embolization
    3. Compression sutures (B-Lynch)
    4. Hysterectomy (last resort)
  4. Resuscitation:
    1. Two large-bore IVs, crystalloids
    2. Blood products (PRBC:FFP:platelets in 1:1:1 ratio for massive transfusion)
    3. Monitor for coagulopathy (check fibrinogen, platelets)

Secondary PPH is often due to retained products or infection, requiring ultrasound evaluation, antibiotics, and possible dilation & curettage.
