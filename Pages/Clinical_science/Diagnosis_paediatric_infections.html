Introduction to Paediatric Exanthems

Childhood exanthems are febrile illnesses characterised by distinctive rashes, often caused by viral pathogens. Historically numbered as "1st through 6th diseases", these infections remain clinically relevant due to their epidemic potential and complications in immunocompromised hosts.

Historical Context

The numbering system originated in 1905 when physicians classified then-unknown rash-causing diseases:

  1. 1st disease: Measles (rubeola)
  2. 2nd disease: Scarlet fever (Group A Strep)
  3. 3rd disease: Rubella
  4. 4th disease: "Dukes' disease" (now considered non-existent or Staphylococcal)
  5. 5th disease: Erythema infectiosum (parvovirus B19)
  6. 6th disease: Roseola infantum (HHV-6/7)

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Viral exanthems typically follow this sequence:

  1. Viremia: Systemic spread after mucosal replication
  2. Endothelial infection: Capillary damage → rash
  3. Immune complex deposition (e.g., parvovirus B19)

The skin manifestations reflect direct viral cytopathy (vesicles in varicella) or host immune response (maculopapular rash in rubella).

Epidemiology

Most are ubiquitous, with >90% seroprevalence by adulthood for HHV-6. Varicella and pertussis remain vaccine-preventable threats in under-immunised populations.

Measles (Mazelen; 1st Disease)

Aetiology

Measles morbillivirus (Paramyxoviridae), a single-stranded RNA virus. Highly contagious (R0=12-18), transmitted via respiratory droplets.

Pathophysiology

Virus binds CD150/SLAM on immune cells → viremia → systemic infection of epithelial/endothelial cells. Immunosuppression occurs via lymphocyte depletion.

Presentation

Prodrome (2-4 days): High fever, "3 Cs":

  1. Cough
  2. Coryza
  3. Conjunctivitis

Exanthem: Erythematous maculopapular rash beginning at hairline → cephalocaudal spread. Koplik spots (white lesions on buccal mucosa) pathognomonic.

Complications

Acute: Pneumonia (primary or bacterial), croup. Chronic: SSPE (subacute sclerosing panencephalitis), occurring years later.

Diagnosis

IgM serology or RT-PCR (nasopharyngeal swab). Lymphopenia is typical.

Therapy

Supportive care. Vitamin A reduces mortality in deficient children. Post-exposure prophylaxis with MMR vaccine (if within 72h) or immunoglobulin (if immunocompromised).

Mnemonic: MEASLES - Morbilliform rash, Epidemic potential, Airborne transmission, SSLAM receptor, Lymphopenia, Encephalitis risk, SSPE late complication

Scarlet Fever (Roodvonk; 2nd Disease)

Aetiology

Group A Streptococcus pyogenes (GAS), producing erythrogenic toxins (SpeA, B, C). Not a viral exanthem but historically classified as 2nd disease.

Pathophysiology

Toxins act as superantigens, activating 5-20% of T-cells → massive cytokine release → rash and systemic symptoms.

Presentation

Key features:

  1. Sandpaper rash: Blanching erythema with punctate elevations, sparing the face (circumoral pallor)
  2. Strawberry tongue (white coating → red papillae)
  3. Pastia's lines: Accentuated rash in skin folds
  4. Pharyngitis, fever

Diagnosis

Rapid strep test or throat culture. ASO titers rise later.

Therapy

Penicillin V (10-day course) or amoxicillin. Macrolides if penicillin-allergic.

Mnemonic: SCARLET - Strep pyogenes, Circumoral pallor, Antibiotics required, Rash like sandpaper, Lines of Pastia, Erythrogenic toxin, Tongue changes

Rubella (Rodehond; 3rd Disease)

Aetiology

Rubella virus (Togaviridae), an RNA virus. Less contagious than measles (R0=5-7).

Pathophysiology

Virus replicates in nasopharynx → lymph nodes → viremia. Congenital infection causes fetal damage via vascular necrosis and apoptosis.

Presentation

Postnatal infection:

  1. Prodrome: Low-grade fever, lymphadenopathy (postauricular/suboccipital)
  2. Rash: Pink maculopapular, cephalocaudal spread (like measles but milder)
  3. Forscheimer spots: Petechiae on soft palate (20%)

Congenital rubella syndrome (CRS): Cataracts, PDA, sensorineural deafness.

Diagnosis

IgM serology or RT-PCR. CRS diagnosed via viral detection in infant.

Therapy

Supportive care. Prevention via MMR vaccine (live attenuated).

Mnemonic: RUBELLA - Retroauricular lymph nodes, Unremarkable in kids (severe in fetus), Birth defects if congenital, Erythematous rash, Low contagion, Live vaccine, Arthralgia in adults

Erythema Infectiosum (5th Disease)

Aetiology

Parvovirus B19, a single-stranded DNA virus targeting erythroid progenitor cells.

Pathophysiology

Virus binds P antigen on RBC precursors → apoptosis → transient reticulocytopenia. Immune complexes cause rash/arthropathy.

Presentation

Children:

  1. "Slapped cheek" rash (erythema on face)
  2. Lacy reticular rash on trunk/limbs
  3. Mild fever

Adults: Arthralgia (hands/wrists/knees), aplastic crisis in sickle cell disease.

Diagnosis

IgM serology (appears at rash onset). PCR for immunocompromised.

Therapy

Supportive care. IVIG for chronic infection in immunocompromised.

Mnemonic: FIFTH DISEASE - Facial "slapped cheek", Immune complex rash, Fetal hydrops risk, Transient aplasia, Hematopoietic target, DNA virus, IgM for diagnosis, Sickle cell crisis, Erythema, Arthralgia adults, Self-limited, Erythroid P antigen

Roseola Infantum (6th Disease)

Aetiology

Human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6), less commonly HHV-7. Nearly universal infection by age 2.

Pathophysiology

Virus infects salivary glands (latent persistence) → viremia → rash likely due to immune reconstitution as fever resolves.

Presentation

Classic triad:

  1. High fever (3-5 days) without localising signs
  2. Rash appears as fever breaks: Blanching macules on trunk → spread
  3. Irritability (may precede rash)

Complication: Febrile seizures (10-15% of cases).

Diagnosis

Typically clinical. PCR for CSF in encephalitis.

Therapy

Supportive care. Antipyretics for comfort. Ganciclovir reserved for immunocompromised.

Mnemonic: ROSEOLA - Rash after fever, Occurs in infants, Salivary latency, Encephalitis rare, Otherwise benign, Lasts 3-5 days, Antivirals not needed

Varicella (Waterpokken; Chickenpox)

Aetiology

Varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a herpesvirus. Highly contagious (R0=10-12) via respiratory droplets or direct contact.

Pathophysiology

Virus replicates in oropharynx → viremia → skin infection causing vesicular rash. Establishes latency in dorsal root ganglia.

Presentation

Prodrome: Fever, malaise (1-2 days before rash). Rash stages:

  1. Macules → papules → vesicles ("dewdrop on rose petal") → crusts
  2. Crops of lesions in varying stages
  3. Centripetal distribution (concentrated on trunk)

Complications

Bacterial superinfection (S. aureus/S. pyogenes), pneumonia, cerebellar ataxia, encephalitis.

Diagnosis

Clinical. PCR or DFA of vesicular fluid if uncertain.

Therapy

Acyclovir (oral/IV) for high-risk patients. Varicella-zoster immunoglobulin (VZIG) for exposed immunocompromised.

Mnemonic: CHICKENPOX - Crops of lesions, Herpesvirus family, Immunocompromised at risk, Centripetal rash, Kids commonly affected, Encephalitis possible, Needs acyclovir if severe, Pneumonia complication, Oral vesicles, Xtremely contagious

Mumps (Bof)

Aetiology

Mumps virus (Paramyxoviridae), an RNA virus. Spread via respiratory droplets or direct contact.

Pathophysiology

Virus infects parotid gland ductal epithelium → inflammation → swelling. May also infect testes, pancreas, CNS.

Presentation

Classic triad:

  1. Parotitis (unilateral/bilateral, "ear lobe elevation" sign)
  2. Fever
  3. Pain with sour foods

Complications: Orchitis (post-pubertal males), aseptic meningitis, pancreatitis.

Diagnosis

IgM serology or RT-PCR (oral swab). Amylase elevated in parotitis/pancreatitis.

Therapy

Supportive care. MMR vaccine prevents infection.

Mnemonic: MUMPS - Meningitis risk, Unilateral parotitis, Male orchitis, Pancreatitis possible, Sour foods painful

Pertussis (Kinkhoest; Whooping Cough)

Aetiology

Bordetella pertussis, a gram-negative coccobacillus producing pertussis toxin (PTx) and other virulence factors.

Pathophysiology

PTx inhibits G-protein signaling → impaired chemotaxis → lymphocytosis. Tracheal cytotoxin damages ciliated epithelium → mucus accumulation.

Presentation

Catarrhal stage (1-2 weeks): Rhinorrhea, mild cough. Paroxysmal stage (2-6 weeks):

  1. Paroxysmal coughing fits ending with inspiratory "whoop" (children) or post-tussive vomiting
  2. Cyanosis during spells
  3. Apnea (infants, no whoop)

Diagnosis

PCR (nasopharyngeal swab, highest yield early). Culture (less sensitive). Lymphocytosis (>20,000/mm³) common.

Therapy

Macrolides (azithromycin) to reduce transmission. Supportive care; ICU for infants with apnea.

Mnemonic: PERTUSSIS - Paroxysmal cough, Ends with whoop, Rib fracture possible, Toxin-mediated, Uncontrollable fits, Spreads via droplets, Stage-dependent presentation, Infants severe, Supportive care mainstay
