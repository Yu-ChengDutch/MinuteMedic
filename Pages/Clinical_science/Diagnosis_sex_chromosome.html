Introduction to Sex Chromosome Syndromes

Sex Chromosome Disorders: Basic Genetics and Clinical Relevance

Why Study These Together?

Sex chromosome syndromes refer to a group of conditions arising from numerical or structural abnormalities in the X or Y chromosomes. They are studied together due to their shared mechanisms involving aneuploidy, X-inactivation, and distinct effects on sexual development and fertility.

Basic Anatomy & Physiology

  1. Humans have 46 chromosomes, including two sex chromosomes: XX in females and XY in males.
  2. The Y chromosome carries the SRY gene, responsible for testicular development and male phenotype.
  3. In females, one X chromosome undergoes lyonization (X-inactivation), forming a Barr body to equalize gene expression.

Common Mechanisms

  1. Non-disjunction during meiosis leads to aneuploidy, causing syndromes like Turner (45,X) or Klinefelter (47,XXY).
  2. Structural abnormalities such as isochromosomes, mosaicism, or deletions result in variable phenotypes.
  3. The number of X chromosomes affects severity, due to dosage of X-linked genes escaping inactivation.

Turner Syndrome (45,X)

The Only Viable Monosomy

Etiology and Genetics

  1. Caused by complete or partial monosomy of the X chromosome (45,X), or mosaicism (e.g., 45,X/46,XX).
  2. Often results from paternal meiotic non-disjunction.
  3. Lack of a second sex chromosome leads to gonadal dysgenesis and failure of secondary sexual development.

Clinical Features

  1. Short stature due to SHOX gene haploinsufficiency.
  2. Streak gonads and primary amenorrhea due to ovarian failure.
  3. Webbed neck, broad chest, widely spaced nipples, low posterior hairline.
  4. Associated with bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation of the aorta, horseshoe kidney.

Diagnosis and Management

  1. Karyotype confirms 45,X or mosaic pattern.
  2. Fertility counseling essential; some mosaics may conceive via assisted reproduction.
  3. Treat with GH in childhood and estrogen replacement at puberty.
Mnemonic – TURNER:
Tiny (short stature), Underdeveloped ovaries, Regressive neck folds, Nipples widely spaced, Endocrine therapy needed, Renal anomalies.

Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)

The Most Common Sex Chromosome Aneuploidy in Males

Etiology and Genetics

  1. Results from meiotic nondisjunction in either parent, leading to an extra X chromosome (47,XXY).
  2. Some individuals may have higher grade aneuploidy (e.g., 48,XXXY) or mosaicism.

Clinical Features

  1. Small, firm testes with primary testicular failure.
  2. Gynecomastia due to estrogen excess.
  3. Infertility due to azoospermia.
  4. May have tall stature, reduced facial/body hair, and mild learning difficulties.

Diagnosis and Management

  1. Karyotype shows 47,XXY pattern.
  2. FSH and LH elevated, testosterone low.
  3. Managed with testosterone replacement, gynecomastia surgery, and fertility options (sperm retrieval + ICSI).
Mnemonic – XXY = eXtra X in a boY:
eXtra breast tissue, Xtra height, You can’t reproduce.

Other Sex Chromosome Syndromes

Less Common, But Clinically Relevant

47,XYY (Jacob's Syndrome)

  1. Occurs only in males; results from paternal nondisjunction in meiosis II.
  2. Patients are usually phenotypically normal males with tall stature.
  3. May have mild learning disabilities and behavioral issues.

47,XXX (Triple X)

  1. Results from maternal meiosis I error.
  2. Usually asymptomatic; normal fertility, tall stature, possible mild cognitive delay.
  3. Often incidentally diagnosed on prenatal testing.
