Positive vs. Negative Symptoms in Psychiatric Disorders

In psychiatric assessment, symptoms are broadly categorized as positive (additions to normal experience/behavior) or negative (subtractions from normal functioning). This distinction originated in schizophrenia research but applies to other disorders as well. Understanding this dichotomy is crucial for phenomenology, prognosis, and treatment planning.

Positive Symptoms

Positive symptoms represent pathological excesses or distortions of normal functions. They are often more dramatic and recognizable, typically responding better to pharmacotherapy.

Characteristics of Positive Symptoms:

  1. Hallucinations: Perceptions without external stimulus (auditory most common in schizophrenia)
  2. Delusions: Fixed false beliefs resistant to reason (persecutory, referential, grandiose, etc.)
  3. Disorganized speech (derailment, incoherence, word salad)
  4. Grossly disorganized behavior (agitation, bizarre actions)
  5. Catatonic behavior (excitement, posturing, negativism)

Neurobiology: Primarily associated with dopaminergic hyperactivity in mesolimbic pathways and temporal lobe abnormalities.

Negative Symptoms

Negative symptoms represent diminution or loss of normal functions. They are often more insidious and debilitating, typically less responsive to treatment.

Characteristics of Negative Symptoms:

  1. Affective flattening: Reduced emotional expression (facial, vocal, gestural)
  2. Alogia: Poverty of speech (brief, empty replies)
  3. Avolition: Lack of goal-directed behavior (hygiene, work, school)
  4. Anhedonia: Inability to experience pleasure (social, physical)
  5. Asociality: Lack of interest in social interactions
  6. Attentional impairment

Neurobiology: Associated with dopaminergic hypoactivity in mesocortical pathways, prefrontal cortex dysfunction, and structural brain changes.

Clinical Significance of the Distinction

  1. Prognosis: Negative symptoms predict poorer long-term outcomes
  2. Treatment response: Positive symptoms respond better to antipsychotics
  3. Pathophysiology: Different neural circuits involved
  4. Diagnostic specificity: Negative symptoms persist during remission of positive symptoms
Mnemonic: POSITIVE vs. NEGATIVE
POSITIVE = Present (added phenomena), Overt, Salient, Improves with meds, Transient (in episodes), Impressive to observers, Vivid, Episodic
NEGATIVE = Not present (lost functions), Enduring, Gradual onset, Apathic, Treatment-resistant, Insidious, Vague, Everyday dysfunction

Primary vs. Secondary Negative Symptoms

Important to distinguish because treatment implications differ:

  1. Primary: Intrinsic to illness (core feature of schizophrenia)
  2. Secondary: Result from other factors:
    1. Positive symptoms (e.g., social withdrawal due to paranoia)
    2. Depression
    3. Medication side effects (e.g., parkinsonism causing blunting)
    4. Environmental deprivation (institutionalization)

Assessment of Negative Symptoms

Accurate assessment requires careful observation and specific tools, as negative symptoms are often subtle and may be confused with depression or medication effects.

Clinical Assessment Techniques

  1. Observation of spontaneous behavior:
    1. Facial expressivity during interview
    2. Gestures and body language
    3. Speech quantity and quality
  2. Structured interviews:
    1. Scale for Assessment of Negative Symptoms (SANS)
    2. Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS)
    3. Brief Negative Symptom Scale (BNSS)
  3. Collateral history:
    1. Change from premorbid functioning
    2. Daily activities and self-care
    3. Social engagement patterns

Differential Diagnosis of Negative Symptoms

  1. Depression:
    1. Dysphoric mood present
    2. Diurnal variation common
    3. Anhedonia may be more selective
  2. Extrapyramidal side effects:
    1. Correlates with medication timing
    2. Improves with anticholinergics/dose reduction
  3. Frontal lobe lesions:
    1. Executive function deficits prominent
    2. May have disinhibition features
Mnemonic: 5 A's of Negative Symptoms
Affect (flat), Alogia, Avolition, Anhedonia, Attention

Treatment Approaches

Positive Symptoms

  1. Pharmacotherapy:
    1. First-generation antipsychotics (D2 antagonists)
    2. Second-generation antipsychotics (serotonin-dopamine antagonists)
    3. Clozapine for treatment-resistant cases
  2. Psychosocial interventions:
    1. Cognitive-behavioral therapy for psychosis
    2. Reality testing techniques

Negative Symptoms

  1. Pharmacotherapy (limited efficacy):
    1. Second-generation antipsychotics preferred
    2. Adjunctive antidepressants may help
    3. Emerging agents targeting glutamatergic systems
  2. Psychosocial interventions:
    1. Social skills training
    2. Cognitive remediation therapy
    3. Supported employment programs
    4. Behavioral activation
  3. Address secondary causes:
    1. Anticholinergics for drug-induced parkinsonism
    2. Reduce high-dose antipsychotics when possible
