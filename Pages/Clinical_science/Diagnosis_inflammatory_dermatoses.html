Introduction to Inflammatory Dermatoses

Anatomy of the Skin Relevant to Inflammation

The skin is composed of three primary layers: the epidermis, dermis, and subcutaneous tissue. The epidermis itself is stratified into several layers (from deep to superficial): stratum basale, stratum spinosum, stratum granulosum, stratum lucidum (only in thick skin), and stratum corneum. The dermis contains blood vessels, nerves, appendages, and immune cells that play crucial roles in inflammatory processes.

The skin hosts a complex immune surveillance system including Langerhans cells (antigen-presenting cells in the epidermis), dermal dendritic cells, mast cells, and tissue-resident memory T cells. These components form the skin immune system (SIS), which maintains homeostasis and responds to threats.

Physiology of Cutaneous Inflammation

Inflammation is the skin's response to injury, infection, or immune dysregulation. The process involves vascular changes (vasodilation, increased permeability), cellular recruitment (leukocyte migration), and mediator release (cytokines, chemokines, prostaglandins).

The inflammatory cascade can be initiated through multiple pathways:

  1. Innate immune activation via pattern recognition receptors (e.g., TLRs)
  2. Adaptive immune responses mediated by T cells and antibodies
  3. Direct tissue damage triggering danger signals

Histopathology of Inflammatory Dermatoses

Microscopic examination reveals characteristic patterns:

  1. Spongiotic dermatitis: Intercellular edema in the epidermis (e.g., eczema)
  2. Psoriasiform dermatitis: Regular epidermal hyperplasia with elongated rete ridges
  3. Interface dermatitis: Lymphocytic infiltration at the dermoepidermal junction
  4. Vasculitis: Inflammation and damage to blood vessel walls
Mnemonic: "SPIV" for Inflammatory Patterns
Spongiotic - Psoriasiform - Interface - Vasculitis

Overview and Diagnostic Approach to Inflammatory Dermatoses

Inflammatory dermatoses present with a variety of morphologies including erythema, scaling, papules, plaques, and vesicles. The diagnostic approach involves:

  1. Morphology: Primary and secondary lesions
  2. Distribution: Pattern of involvement (flexural, extensor, photodistributed, etc.)
  3. Time course: Acute, chronic, or relapsing
  4. Associated symptoms: Pruritus, pain, systemic manifestations

Key Differential Diagnostic Features

Several clinical clues help distinguish between common inflammatory dermatoses:

Scale characteristics:

  1. Psoriasis: Thick, silvery-white, adherent scale
  2. Eczema: Fine, white, loose scale
  3. Seborrheic dermatitis: Greasy, yellowish scale

Border characteristics:

  1. Psoriasis: Well-demarcated borders
  2. Nummular eczema: Less distinct borders
  3. Tinea corporis: Active, advancing scaly border
Mnemonic: "PETS" for Scaly Dermatoses
Psoriasis - Eczema - Tinea - Seborrheic dermatitis

Psoriasis

Introduction

Psoriasis is a chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory skin disorder characterized by hyperproliferation of keratinocytes and dermal inflammation. It affects approximately 2-3% of the population worldwide and has significant impact on quality of life.

Signs and Symptoms

The classic presentation includes:

  1. Plaque psoriasis (most common): Well-demarcated, erythematous plaques with adherent silvery scale, typically on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, and lumbosacral area
  2. Ausspitz sign: Pinpoint bleeding when scale is removed (due to elongated dermal papillae)
  3. Koebner phenomenon: Lesions developing at sites of trauma
  4. Nail changes: Pitting, oil spots, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

Psoriasis results from complex interactions between genetic predisposition (PSORS loci, HLA-Cw6) and environmental triggers (stress, infection, medications). The pathophysiology involves:

  1. T cell activation: Particularly Th1, Th17, and Th22 subsets
  2. Cytokine release: TNF-α, IL-17, IL-22, IL-23
  3. Keratinocyte proliferation: Cell cycle shortened from 28 to 3-5 days

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Biopsy shows:

  1. Regular epidermal hyperplasia (psoriasiform pattern)
  2. Parakeratosis (nuclei retained in stratum corneum)
  3. Munro microabscesses (neutrophils in stratum corneum)
  4. Dilated capillaries in dermal papillae

Treatment

Treatment depends on disease severity:

  1. Topical therapy: Corticosteroids, vitamin D analogs, retinoids
  2. Phototherapy: Narrowband UVB, PUVA
  3. Systemic therapy: Methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin
  4. Biologics: TNF-α inhibitors, IL-17/23 inhibitors

Prognosis and Complications

Psoriasis is chronic with periods of exacerbation and remission. Associated comorbidities include:

  1. Psoriatic arthritis (in 30% of patients)
  2. Cardiovascular disease
  3. Metabolic syndrome
  4. Psychosocial impact
Mnemonic: "KIND Psoriasis" for Histologic Features
Keratinocyte proliferation - Inflammation - Neutrophils (microabscesses) - Dilated capillaries

Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)

Introduction

Atopic dermatitis is a chronic, pruritic, inflammatory skin condition characterized by impaired skin barrier function and immune dysregulation. It is the most common inflammatory skin disease, affecting up to 20% of children and 3% of adults.

Signs and Symptoms

Clinical features vary by age:

  1. Infantile phase (0-2 years): Erythematous, oozing, crusted plaques on cheeks and extensor surfaces
  2. Childhood phase (2-12 years): Flexural lichenified plaques (antecubital/popliteal fossae, neck)
  3. Adult phase: Chronic lichenified plaques with excoriations, often widespread

Associated features include:

  1. Xerosis (dry skin)
  2. Pruritus (often severe, "the itch that rashes")
  3. Atopic stigmata: Dennie-Morgan folds (infraorbital folds), hyperlinear palms, keratosis pilaris

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

Atopic dermatitis results from complex interactions between:

  1. Genetic factors: Filaggrin mutations (FLG), other epidermal barrier genes
  2. Immune dysregulation: Th2 skewing with elevated IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, IL-31
  3. Environmental triggers: Irritants, allergens, microbes (S. aureus), stress

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical based on Hanifin and Rajka criteria (requires 3 of 4 major criteria and ≥3 minor criteria). Biopsy shows:

  1. Spongiosis (intercellular edema)
  2. Lymphocytic infiltrate
  3. Hyperkeratosis and parakeratosis in chronic lesions

Treatment

Management involves:

  1. Skin barrier repair: Emollients, gentle cleansers
  2. Topical anti-inflammatory: Corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)
  3. Systemic therapy: Dupilumab (IL-4/13 inhibitor), cyclosporine, methotrexate
  4. Trigger avoidance: Irritants, allergens, stress management

Prognosis and Complications

Course is chronic with flares. Complications include:

  1. Skin infections: Impetigo, eczema herpeticum
  2. Sleep disturbance from pruritus
  3. Atopic march: Progression to asthma/allergic rhinitis
Mnemonic: "ITCH" for Atopic Dermatitis Features
Inflammation - Th2 cytokines - Chronic pruritus - Hyperlinear palms

Contact Dermatitis

Introduction

Contact dermatitis is an inflammatory skin reaction to exogenous substances that may occur through irritant or allergic mechanisms. It accounts for approximately 80% of occupational skin diseases and affects 15-20% of the general population at some point in their lives.

Signs and Symptoms

Clinical presentation varies by type:

  1. Irritant contact dermatitis:
    1. Erythema, edema, vesicles (acute) or dryness, fissuring (chronic)
    2. Sharp borders corresponding to area of exposure
    3. Burning/stinging more than pruritus
  2. Allergic contact dermatitis:
    1. Pruritic erythema, papules, vesicles with indistinct borders
    2. Delayed onset (24-72 hours post-exposure)
    3. May spread beyond contact area (autoeczematization)

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

Irritant contact dermatitis (80% of cases):

  1. Direct cytotoxic damage to keratinocytes
  2. No prior sensitization required
  3. Common irritants: Detergents, solvents, acids/alkalis

Allergic contact dermatitis (Type IV hypersensitivity):

  1. Sensitization phase (10-14 days after first exposure)
  2. Elicitation phase (24-72 hours after re-exposure)
  3. Common allergens: Nickel, fragrance mix, balsam of Peru, paraphenylenediamine

Diagnosis

Diagnostic approach includes:

  1. Clinical history: Exposure patterns, temporal relationship
  2. Physical examination: Distribution often suggests causative agent
  3. Patch testing (for allergic type): Application of common allergens to back for 48 hours, read at 96 hours

Treatment

Management strategies:

  1. Avoidance: Identify and eliminate causative agent
  2. Topical therapy: Corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors
  3. Systemic therapy: Oral corticosteroids for severe cases
  4. Barrier protection: Gloves, emollients
Mnemonic: "CONTACT" for Common Allergens
Cobalt - Oils (plants) - Nickel - Thimerosal - Acrylates - Chromates - Topical antibiotics (neomycin)

Lichen Planus

Introduction

Lichen planus is a T cell-mediated inflammatory disorder affecting skin and mucous membranes, characterized by pruritic, purple, polygonal papules. It has a worldwide prevalence of 0.5-1% and typically affects adults aged 30-60 years.

Signs and Symptoms

Clinical features include:

  1. Cutaneous lesions:
    1. Violaceous, flat-topped papules with fine white lines (Wickham striae)
    2. Preferred locations: Flexor wrists, ankles, oral mucosa
    3. Koebner phenomenon present
  2. Mucosal involvement (in 50% of cases):
    1. Reticular white streaks on buccal mucosa
    2. Painful erosions may occur
  3. Variants: Hypertrophic, follicular, actinic, vesiculobullous

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

The exact cause remains unknown but involves:

  1. Autoimmune process: CD8+ T cells attack basal keratinocytes
  2. Genetic predisposition: Association with HLA-DR1, DR10
  3. Triggers: Hepatitis C infection, certain drugs (β-blockers, NSAIDs), dental metals

Diagnosis

Diagnostic methods:

  1. Clinical examination: Characteristic morphology and distribution
  2. Biopsy findings:
    1. Hyperkeratosis without parakeratosis
    2. Saw-tooth rete ridges
    3. Liquefaction degeneration of basal layer
    4. Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate at dermoepidermal junction

Treatment

Management options:

  1. Topical therapy: High-potency corticosteroids, tacrolimus (for mucosal lesions)
  2. Systemic therapy: Oral corticosteroids, retinoids, methotrexate
  3. Phototherapy: Narrowband UVB
  4. Monitoring: Oral lesions require cancer surveillance (1% risk of SCC)
Mnemonic: "5 P's of Lichen Planus"
Pruritic - Purple - Polygonal - Papules - Plaques

Seborrheic Dermatitis

Introduction

Seborrheic dermatitis is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory dermatosis characterized by erythema and greasy scales in sebum-rich areas. It affects 3-5% of the population, with two peaks of incidence: infancy (cradle cap) and adulthood (ages 30-60).

Signs and Symptoms

Clinical presentation includes:

  1. Distribution:
    1. Scalp (dandruff), eyebrows, nasolabial folds
    2. Postauricular area, presternal area
    3. Beard area in men
  2. Lesion characteristics:
    1. Erythematous patches or thin plaques
    2. Yellowish, greasy scales
    3. Mild pruritus common

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis involves three key factors:

  1. Malassezia yeast (M. globosa, M. restricta): Lipase production leads to inflammatory free fatty acids
  2. Sebum production: Androgen-mediated, explains distribution
  3. Immune response: Both innate and adaptive immunity contribute

Predisposing factors include:

  1. Parkinson's disease
  2. HIV infection (often severe and refractory)
  3. Certain medications (e.g., psoralen, lithium)

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical based on:

  1. Characteristic distribution and morphology
  2. Response to antifungal/anti-inflammatory treatment
  3. KOH preparation may show yeast forms (but not required)

Treatment

Management approaches:

  1. Topical antifungals: Ketoconazole, ciclopirox (shampoos, creams)
  2. Low-potency steroids: Hydrocortisone 1% for facial lesions
  3. Calcineurin inhibitors: Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus for maintenance
  4. Keratinolytics: Salicylic acid, coal tar for scalp scaling
Mnemonic: "SCALP" for Seborrheic Dermatitis Features
Sebum-rich areas - Chronic course - Antifungal responsive - Low-potency steroids - Pruritus mild

Pityriasis Rosea

Introduction

Pityriasis rosea is an acute, self-limited inflammatory dermatosis characterized by oval, scaly plaques following the cleavage lines of the skin. It primarily affects adolescents and young adults (ages 10-35), with a slight female predominance.

Signs and Symptoms

The classic presentation includes:

  1. Herald patch (50-90% of cases):
    1. Single 2-10 cm oval plaque with central clearing
    2. Appears 1-2 weeks before generalized eruption
    3. Often on trunk, neck, or proximal extremities
  2. Secondary eruption:
    1. Multiple 0.5-2 cm oval papules/plaques
    2. "Christmas tree" distribution on back (following Langer lines)
    3. Collarette scale (peripheral scaling with central clearance)
  3. Mild pruritus (25% of cases)

Aetiology and Pathophysiology

While the exact cause remains uncertain, evidence suggests:

  1. Viral trigger: Associated with human herpesviruses 6/7 (HHV-6/7)
  2. Seasonal variation: More common in spring and fall
  3. Immune-mediated: Likely a host response to viral antigens

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Differential diagnosis includes:

  1. Tinea corporis (KOH preparation negative in pityriasis rosea)
  2. Secondary syphilis (RPR/VDRL negative in pityriasis rosea)
  3. Guttate psoriasis (more scaly, no herald patch)

Treatment

As a self-limiting condition (resolves in 6-12 weeks), treatment focuses on symptom relief:

  1. Emollients: For dry skin
  2. Topical steroids: For pruritus
  3. Phototherapy: UVB may hasten resolution
  4. Antivirals: Acyclovir may be beneficial if started early
Mnemonic: "ROSEA" for Pityriasis Rosea Features
Recent viral illness - Oval plaques - Seasonal - Evanescent (self-limiting) - Adolescents affected
