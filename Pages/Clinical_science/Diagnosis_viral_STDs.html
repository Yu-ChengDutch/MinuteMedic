Genital Warts (HPV)

Human Papillomavirus: Pathophysiology and Clinical Management

Etiology and Epidemiology

Genital warts are caused primarily by low-risk HPV types 6 and 11, which account for approximately 90% of external anogenital warts. HPV is a non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA virus in the Papillomaviridae family. It is transmitted via direct epithelial contact, with a high prevalence among sexually active individuals aged 15–30 years.

Pathogenesis

  1. HPV infects basal keratinocytes through microabrasions in the mucosa.
  2. Viral replication induces cellular proliferation, leading to the characteristic verrucous lesions.
  3. Low-risk types remain episomal (non-integrating), while high-risk types can integrate into the host genome.

Clinical Features

  1. Exophytic, flesh-colored to hyperpigmented, cauliflower-like verrucous lesions on the vulva, perineum, penis, perianal area, and urethra.
  2. Often asymptomatic, but can be associated with pruritus, pain, or bleeding during intercourse or defecation.
  3. In immunocompromised patients, lesions may be more extensive and refractory to treatment.

Diagnosis

  1. Primarily clinical based on morphology and anatomical distribution.
  2. Colposcopy may be used to evaluate subclinical lesions, especially in high-risk patients.
  3. Biopsy indicated if atypical appearance, rapid growth, ulceration, or suspicion of malignancy.

Treatment

  1. Patient-applied: Imiquimod 5% cream, podophyllotoxin, sinecatechins.
  2. Provider-administered: Cryotherapy, trichloroacetic acid, electrosurgery, CO₂ laser therapy, surgical excision.
  3. HPV vaccination (Gardasil 9) is recommended for all individuals up to age 26; covers both low-risk and high-risk types.

Genital Herpes (HSV)

Herpes Simplex Virus Infection: Diagnosis and Management

Etiology and Pathophysiology

Genital herpes is caused by HSV-2 in approximately 70–90% of cases and HSV-1 in the remaining. After primary infection, HSV establishes latency in sacral dorsal root ganglia with periodic reactivation. Shedding can occur even in the absence of visible lesions.

Clinical Features

  1. Primary infection: Painful grouped vesicles on an erythematous base, progressing to ulcers; may be associated with fever, dysuria, tender lymphadenopathy, and malaise.
  2. Recurrent episodes: Typically milder and shorter; prodrome of tingling or burning precedes lesions.
  3. Complications include urinary retention, autoinoculation (herpetic whitlow), and neonatal herpes in peripartum transmission.

Diagnosis

  1. PCR from swabbed lesion is gold standard due to high sensitivity and specificity.
  2. Tzanck smear shows multinucleated giant cells but lacks specificity.
  3. Serology may assist in differentiating HSV-1 vs HSV-2 but not helpful in acute diagnosis.

Treatment

  1. Initial: Acyclovir 400 mg TID for 7–10 days or valacyclovir/famciclovir equivalents.
  2. Recurrent: 5-day regimens or episodic therapy within 48 hours of prodrome.
  3. Suppressive therapy indicated for ≥6 recurrences/year or in discordant couples.

HIV/AIDS

Human Immunodeficiency Virus: Virology and Disease Progression

Virology and Transmission

HIV is a retrovirus of the Lentivirus genus. It primarily infects CD4+ T helper cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells. Transmission occurs via blood, semen, vaginal secretions, rectal fluids, and breast milk. Routes include unprotected sexual contact, IV drug use, transfusions (rare), and vertical transmission.

Natural History

  1. Acute HIV syndrome (2–4 weeks post-infection): fever, sore throat, rash, lymphadenopathy, mucocutaneous ulcers.
  2. Clinical latency: asymptomatic for years; gradual CD4 decline.
  3. AIDS: CD4 count < 200/mm³ or presence of AIDS-defining illness (e.g., Pneumocystis pneumonia, Kaposi’s sarcoma).

Diagnosis

  1. Screening: 4th-generation ELISA (p24 antigen + HIV-1/2 antibodies).
  2. Confirmation: HIV-1 RNA PCR or Western blot.
  3. Monitoring: CD4 count and viral load every 3–6 months.

Treatment

  1. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) recommended for all, regardless of CD4 count.
  2. Standard regimen: 2 NRTIs + 1 integrase inhibitor (e.g., tenofovir/emtricitabine + dolutegravir).
  3. Prophylaxis for opportunistic infections based on CD4 thresholds (e.g., TMP-SMX for PCP at CD4 < 200).
