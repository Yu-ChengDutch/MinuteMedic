Welcome to an introduction to vasculitis!

It'll go into the different varieties, and classification, and attempt to help you remember them all.

Vasculitis

Vasculitis is the inflammation of blood vessels of various size. Individually, they're called phlebitis when it pertains to veins, and arteritis when it pertains to arteries. A primary division is made based on aetiology:

  1. Primary vasculitis
  2. Secondary vasculitis
  3. Single origin vasculitis

Withing the category of primary vasculitis a further division is made into:

  1. Large vessel vasculitis
  2. Medium vessel vasculitis
  3. Small vessel vasculitis
  4. Variable size vessel vasculitis

Primary vasculitis

To remember what vasculitis belong to what, remember the following:

1-2-3, all primary vasculitis have two variants, except small (i.e., nr. 3) vasculitis which has two categories, each with three variants

Large vessel vasculitis

In this category, we find Takayasu arteritis, also known as aortic arch syndrome, as well as Giant cell arteritis which is also known as temporal arteritis.

Medium vessel vasculitis

In this category we find polyarteritis nodosa and Kawasaki disease.

Small vessel vasculitis

As mentioned in the mnemonic, there are two types of small vessel vasculitis, namely ANCA-associated and immune complex-associated. The difference is whether the disease is associated with anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCAs) which are a group of antibodies of the IgG type against antigens in the cytoplasm of neutrophils and monocytes. While it is poorly understood how these ANCAs originate (two theories might be that they're a type II hypersensitivity reaction to chronic infection or a result of defective apoptosis), their direct role in pathogenesis seems to have been proven in animal models. They seem to be able to activate neutrophils which may then adhere to endothelial cells where they start causing an inflammatory chain reaction.

All of the small vessel vasculitis are refered to as polyangiitis, and both categories have a Nazi eponym.

ANCA-associated

Of this type, there are three variants: Microscopic polyangiitis (Wegener), Granulomatosis with polyangiitis, Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis.

Immune complex

Of this type, there are three variants: Anti-glomerular basement membrane disease, Cryoglobulinemic vasculitis, IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein)

Primary variable vessel vasculitis

Here, we find two diseases that are closely related to the eye, namely: Behçet's disease and Cogan syndrome.

Secondary vasculitis

These vasculites are associated with systemic inflammtory disorders, namely Lupus vasculitis and Rheumatoid vasculitis.

Single-organ vasculitis

For some reason, the only described single-organ vasculites are those occuring in ectodermal tissue: nervous system and skin. Here we find the following variants:

  1. Small-vessel cutaneous vasculitis
  2. Golfer's vasculitis (a.k.a. exercise-induced vasculitis or Disney rash)
  3. Retinal vasculitis
  4. Primary central nervous system vasculitis

Diagnosis

In general, laboratory tests will show elevated markers of inflammation: bezinking, CRP, anaemia, leukocytosis and eosinophilia. Depending on the type of vasculitis, it is possible to find elevated ANCA. However, for a definite diagnosis, a biopsy of involved tissue is necessary. On these biopsies, it is sometimes possible to find leukocytoclasis, which is nuclear debris from infiltrating neutrophils. Alternatively, one could perfrom an angiogram (vessel x-ray) in order to demonstrate a pattern of inflammation. Furthermore, in large vessel vasculites it is useful to perform a FDG-PET/CT which may show enhanced glucose metabolism in the vessels.

Treatment

Usually, therapy is immunosuppressive using such drugs as prednisone or cyclophosphamide. If an infection plays a role, one might consider antimmicrobial agents.

Lymphangitis

Just like how blood vessels may inflame, so too may lymphatic channels inflame. This occurs as a result of an infection distal to the channel and it presents as long red streaks spreading away from the site of infection. As the involvement of the lymphatic system allows for rapid spread of the infection it might be a medical emergency. Aetiologically, this disease is most commonly associated with Streptococcus Pyogenes (group A streptococcus).

Besides the characteristic clearly demarcated long red streaks spreading away from the infection, this disease may also present with warm skin over the infection, chills, high fever, throbbing pain, oedema or other signs of systemic disease. It also possible to find lymphadenopathy (enlarged, swollen, tender lymph nodes) or signs of sepsis.

Therapy depends on the extent of spread: if it is local, then debridement and good wound care should suffice. If it has spread further, immediate antibiotic drug therapy is necessary.

NB: Lymphagitis may also occur secondary to a malignancy as the cells spread from a tumour along the vessel, or as a tumour block drainage.
