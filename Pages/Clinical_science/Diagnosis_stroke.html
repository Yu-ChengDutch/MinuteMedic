Introduction to Acute Stroke

Historical Perspective

The understanding of stroke has evolved significantly over centuries. The term "apoplexy" was used by Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) to describe sudden paralysis, likely due to stroke. In the 17th century, Johann Jacob Wepfer identified intracranial hemorrhage as a cause of apoplexy after postmortem examinations. The 19th century saw the distinction between ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes, while the 20th century brought revolutionary imaging techniques and thrombolytic therapy.

Neurovascular Anatomy

The brain's blood supply originates from two paired arteries: the internal carotid arteries (anterior circulation) and the vertebral arteries (posterior circulation), which merge to form the basilar artery. These vessels give rise to:

  1. Anterior cerebral artery (ACA): Supplies medial frontal and parietal lobes
  2. Middle cerebral artery (MCA): Supplies lateral hemispheres (dominant for language in 95% right-handers)
  3. Posterior cerebral artery (PCA): Supplies occipital lobes and medial temporal lobes

The Circle of Willis provides collateral circulation, though its completeness varies among individuals.

Pathophysiology

Stroke occurs due to disruption of cerebral blood flow, leading to neuronal ischemia. The ischemic cascade begins within seconds:

  1. Failure of Na+/K+ ATPase → membrane depolarization
  2. Glutamate excitotoxicity via NMDA/AMPA receptors
  3. Calcium influx → mitochondrial dysfunction
  4. Free radical formation → lipid peroxidation
  5. Apoptosis/necrosis

The ischemic penumbra represents salvageable tissue surrounding the irreversibly damaged core, forming the target for acute interventions.

Etiology and Risk Factors

Stroke etiology follows the TOAST classification:

  1. Large artery atherosclerosis (e.g., carotid stenosis)
  2. Cardioembolism (e.g., atrial fibrillation)
  3. Small vessel occlusion (lacunar strokes)
  4. Other determined cause (e.g., vasculitis, dissection)
  5. Undetermined cause

Modifiable risk factors include hypertension (most significant), diabetes, smoking, dyslipidemia, and physical inactivity. Non-modifiable factors include age, male sex, and genetic predisposition.

Diagnosis of Acute Stroke

Clinical Localization

Time is brain: Approximately 1.9 million neurons die each minute during acute ischemia. Rapid assessment follows the FAST mnemonic:

Face drooping
Arm weakness
Speech difficulty
Time to call emergency services

Specific syndromes include:

Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Stroke

Contralateral hemiparesis (face/arm > leg), hemisensory loss, gaze preference toward lesion, and:

  1. Dominant hemisphere (usually left): Broca's, Wernicke's, or global aphasia
  2. Non-dominant hemisphere: Neglect, anosognosia, constructional apraxia

Posterior Circulation Stroke

"Crossed" deficits (ipsilateral cranial nerve palsy + contralateral motor/sensory), vertigo, ataxia, visual field defects (homonymous hemianopia), or cortical blindness.

Imaging

Non-contrast CT remains first-line to exclude hemorrhage. Early ischemic changes may include:

  1. Hyperdense vessel sign (clot visibility)
  2. Loss of gray-white differentiation
  3. Insular ribbon sign

MRI with DWI is superior for detecting acute ischemia (within minutes), showing restricted diffusion as bright signal. Perfusion imaging identifies penumbra.

Acute Treatment (Dutch Guidelines)

Prehospital Care

The chain of survival includes:

  1. Recognition using FAST
  2. Immediate emergency call (112 in the Netherlands)
  3. Rapid transport to stroke center
  4. Pre-notification to hospital

Avoid glucose-containing fluids unless hypoglycemia is confirmed. Maintain oxygen saturation >94%.

Intravenous Thrombolysis (IV-tPA)

Alteplase (0.9 mg/kg, max 90 mg) is administered within 4.5 hours of symptom onset after exclusion of:

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
Current intracranial hemorrhage
Oral anticoagulation with INR >1.7
Neurosurgery/trauma in past 3 months
Time unknown or >4.5 hours
Recent myocardial infarction
Arterial puncture at non-compressible site
Intracranial neoplasm/AVM
Notable hypertension (>185/110 mmHg)
Deficit rapidly improving

Endovascular Thrombectomy (EVT)

For large vessel occlusion (LVO) in anterior circulation within 6 hours (selected cases up to 24 hours with perfusion mismatch). Requires:

  1. NIHSS ≥2 for anterior circulation, ≥6 for posterior
  2. ASPECTS score ≥6 on CT or infarct core <70 mL on MRI

Supportive Care

Includes:

  1. Blood pressure management: Permissive hypertension unless >220/120 mmHg or thrombolysis candidate
  2. Glycemic control: Target 140-180 mg/dL
  3. Fever control: Paracetamol for temperature >37.5°C
  4. Dysphagia screening: Before oral intake

Subacute Management and Secondary Prevention

Early Rehabilitation

Initiated within 24-48 hours if medically stable. Includes:

  1. Mobilization to prevent complications (DVT, pneumonia)
  2. Speech therapy for aphasia/dysphagia
  3. Constraint-induced movement therapy for motor deficits

Secondary Prevention

Based on stroke subtype:

Noncardioembolic Stroke

Antiplatelet therapy:

  1. Aspirin 100 mg/day + clopidogrel 75 mg/day for 21 days (CHANCE protocol), then single agent
  2. Alternatively, monotherapy with aspirin, clopidogrel, or aspirin/dipyridamole

Cardioembolic Stroke

Anticoagulation (after exclusion of hemorrhage, typically after 3-14 days):

  1. Non-valvular AF: DOACs preferred over warfarin
  2. Mechanical valves: Warfarin

Risk factor control: Target BP <140/90 mmHg, LDL <1.8 mmol/L, HbA1c <7%.
