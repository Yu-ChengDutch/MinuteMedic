Introduction

Upper limb (UL) amputations involve the removal of any part of the upper extremity due to surgery, trauma, or pathology. These amputations significantly impact an individual's life, affecting physical functionality, social engagement, self-esteem, psychosocial well-being, and employment prospects. The complex multi-joint movements and functions of the human upper limb are challenging to replicate with prostheses. A critical limitation and common reason for prosthesis refusal is the lack of purposely delivered sensory feedback, which affects control and hinders the device's integration into the user's body schema. Proprioception, defined as the sense of position, movement, tension, force, effort, and balance, is crucial for dexterous and effortless body movement. This summary will explore the epidemiology, assessment, rehabilitation, current prosthetic models, future developments, and the vital role of proprioception in upper limb prostheses.

Epidemiology

Upper limb amputations are less prevalent than lower limb amputations, accounting for about 10% of all limb losses. A significant estimation from 2017 indicated over 20 million people globally living with an upper limb amputation due to traumatic causes. The primary cause of UL amputations in general is trauma, often stemming from work-related accidents such as crush injuries, electrical burns, gunshot wounds, or combat injuries. These traumatic amputations typically affect individuals aged 20-40, with a male-to-female ratio of 4:1. The most frequent major upper limb amputation is at the transradial level (below elbow), accounting for over half of all major arm amputations. Non-traumatic UL amputations are primarily due to cancer or tumors, which often result in more extensive amputations like shoulder disarticulation. Other causes include congenital anomalies, thermal or electrical injury, frostbites, dysvascular disease, and infection.

Assessment and Prognosis

Assessment for upper limb amputations involves a multidisciplinary approach. Key components include:
- History: Reason for amputation, co-morbid injuries, handedness, future vocational/recreational goals, baseline functional status (including cognition), musculoskeletal history (contralateral limb and cervical spine), and social history (support system, home/work environment).
- Physical Examination: Visual inspection of the residual limb for length, volume, atrophy, incisional healing, wounds, scar tissue, and shape. It also includes bilateral upper extremity (UE) range of motion (ROM) and muscle strength (including scapular/glenohumeral joint), neck ROM, sensation, and cognition.
- Functional Assessment: Evaluation of specific UE movements (e.g., scapular protraction/retraction, shoulder flexion/extension for body-powered prostheses, and suitable muscle sites for myoelectric prostheses). Early assessment of independence level, hand dominance, and Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) performance is recommended, utilizing standardized assessments like the Box and Block test or the Activities Measure for UE Amputation (AM-ULA).
- Imaging: X-rays to assess for bony fragments or degenerative joint diseases; MRI/CT for tumor diagnosis, infection assessment, and surgical planning; Technetium bone scans for osteomyelitis; Ultrasonography for soft tissue injuries; and Doppler ultrasonography to assess blood supply for wound healing.
- Supplemental Tools: Electromyography (EMG)/Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) to identify optimal surgical plans or reasons for non-progression, and myoelectric control training with EMG or pattern recognition feedback to assess remaining musculature for sensor placement.

Prognosis:
Pre-amputation function, level, and cause of amputation significantly impact functional outcomes, with higher-level amputations generally leading to greater limitations. Early prosthetic fitting and training are major factors for prosthetic acceptance and rehabilitation success. Studies show a 93% rehabilitation success rate and 100% return-to-work rate for those fit within 30 days, compared to 42% and 15% respectively for those fit later. However, the rejection rate for UL prostheses is higher than for lower extremity prostheses, reaching as high as 44% in recent studies. Reasons for rejection include lack of training, development of one-handedness, non-cosmetic appearance, discomfort, weight, and cost. Psychological care is crucial due to high rates of psychiatric conditions like major depressive disorder (71%), suicide ideation (30.5%), and PTSD (20.3%) in traumatic UE amputees.

Rehabilitation

Upper limb amputation rehabilitation follows a phased approach:
- Phase 0: Pre-operation Education: Often not possible due to the traumatic nature of UL amputations, but beneficial for elective procedures to educate patients on potential post-surgery abilities.
- Phase 1: Immediate Postoperative Management: Focuses on wound care, edema reduction, residual limb shaping, pain control, scar tissue management, contracture prevention, total body muscle endurance and strengthening, changing hand dominance, basic ADLs training, psychological support, and home/DME assessment.
- Phase 2: Pre-prosthetic Training: Aims to prepare patients for prosthesis use by enhancing endurance, mobility, and strength, focusing on specific motor skills like scapular movement. Myoelectric users undergo myosite testing. This phase includes ROM, physical conditioning, desensitization, and limb shaping, concluding with prosthesis acquisition.
- Phase 3: Intermediate Prosthetic Training: Goals include increasing prosthesis wear time, mastering control, expanding the functional envelope, and integrating the prosthesis into activities.
- Phase 4: Advanced Prosthetic Training: Highly individualized, focusing on vocational and avocational goals, often involving tool use or onsite work assessments for environmental modifications. The aim is energy conservation, minimized biomechanical stress, and return to pre-injury activities.

Regular follow-up with a multidisciplinary team (physiatrists, physical therapists, occupational therapists, prosthetists, psychologists) is essential throughout all phases.

Prosthetics

Current Available Models
The main components of a prosthetic prescription include the socket, suspension, interface, and terminal device (TD). There are three general classes of upper extremity prostheses:
- Passive Functional/Cosmetic: Mimic the appearance of the non-affected limb, providing balance aid and opposition, but limited functional utility.
- Body-Powered/Conventional: Operated by a cable and harness system, controlled by specific body movements. Advantages include durability, lighter weight, lower cost, and proprioceptive feedback through the harness. They are generally more tolerant to wet/dirty environments and may require shorter training periods.
- Myoelectric/External Power: Powered by batteries and controlled by EMG signals from muscle contractions. They offer increased functional movements, better cosmesis, increased pinch/grip force, and reduced/eliminated harnessing for comfort and ROM. Myoelectric systems can provide digital (on/off) or proportional control, where motor velocity is proportional to EMG signal amplitude, allowing variable speed and force.
- Hybrid: Combine elements of body-powered and external power systems, aiming for a balance of weight, cost, grip force, and reduced harnessing.

Specific Components:
- Terminal Devices (TDs): Include passive, body-powered hooks/hands, and externally powered hooks/hands. Hooks are durable and provide lateral/tip pinch. Hands offer a 3-jaw chuck pinch, with newer multiarticulate hands offering more grip options. Voluntary opening (VO) TDs are most practical as tension doesn't need to be maintained to hold objects.
- Wrists: Range from friction control units for passive pronosupination to locking/quick disconnect wrists for rapid interchange of TDs, and spring-assisted wrist flexion for bilateral amputees.
- Elbows: Body-powered elbows can have spring-assisted flexion and a locking mechanism. Externally powered elbows can be controlled with switches or myoelectric signals.
- Shoulders: Most allow passive abduction and flexion, with desired position maintained by friction or lock. Powered shoulders are available for high-level amputations.
- Sockets: Typically double-walled, made of carbon graphite or plastic resin, fabricated from a cast of the residual limb. Flexible inner sockets and frame designs can accommodate volume changes.
- Suspension Systems: Include harnesses (e.g., figure 8 for transradial/transhumeral amputees), anatomical (self-suspension) systems, silicone sleeves (creating negative pressure/adhesive bond, useful for delicate skin), and suction suspension (preferred for transhumeral externally controlled devices, requiring intimate fit).

Future Developments
Advancements in upper limb prosthetics are continually striving to recreate the complex functions of the human hand. Key areas of development include:
- Osseointegration: A surgical technique where a device is inserted directly into the residual limb bone, eliminating traditional sockets to improve suspension, mobility, and quality of life.
- Targeted Muscle Reinnervation (TMR): Motor nerves are reimplanted into denervated muscles to create new myoelectric control sites, allowing more intuitive control and potentially restoring sensation.
- Agonist-Antagonist Myoneural Interface (AMI) Surgery: Utilizes natural agonist-antagonist muscle coupling to enhance motor control and proprioceptive sensory feedback.
- Implantable Myoelectric Sensors (bions): Miniature electrodes transmit EMG signals wirelessly, demonstrating greater reliability, less impedance, and improved resistance to fibrosis compared to surface electrodes.
- Regenerative Peripheral Nerve Interfaces (RPNI) Surgery: Places free peripheral nerve endings into muscle or dermal grafts to reduce neuroma and phantom limb pain, and can bioamplify motor action potentials for more complex prosthesis control.
- Virtual and Augmented Reality (VR/AR): Used to enhance rehabilitation by reducing pain, improving prosthetic training, and promoting prosthesis usage. AR feedback, for example, has been successfully integrated into sensorimotor control loops, allowing users to adapt their behavior.
- Pattern Recognition-based Myoelectric Control: Algorithms correlate specific EMG patterns with preprogrammed upper limb actions, enabling simultaneous control of multiple degrees of freedom and more intuitive operation.
- Powered Terminal Devices: Coupled with TMR, these offer refined sensory feedback, potentially restoring some sensations and improving control.
- 3D Printing: Used to develop more affordable, personalized prostheses, especially for pediatric patients and in underserved areas.

Proprioception

Proprioception, encompassing the senses of position, movement, tension, force, and effort, is essential for skilled and effortless body movements. Despite its key role, most research on supplemental feedback for prosthetics has focused on touch restitution, leaving a major knowledge gap in comprehensive analysis of proprioceptive feedback.

Challenges in Restitution:
Emulating the richness of physiological sensory return artificially is a primary hurdle. This complex process is hindered by:
- Time-consuming signal processing and transmission.
- Large area required for components relative to available skin surface.
- Need for repeated calibrations for reliable sensation and perception.
- Inability of current technology to elicit force and heaviness sensation in a homomodal (same sensory system/modality) way.

Non-Invasive Strategies for Proprioception Restitution:
These strategies aim to extract information from instrumented prostheses and convey it to the user's remaining sensory system.
- Vibrotactile Stimulation: Applied directly to the skin via mechanical vibration, modulating frequency and amplitude to convey information. It can generate a kinesthetic illusion by stimulating muscle spindle afferents. While low-power and embeddable, it suffers from habituation and low spatial resolution. Studies show mixed results: some report improved accuracy in matching tasks, while others show no improvement or even detrimental effects. More recently, while not always improving performance over vision, it was preferred by users.
- Electrotactile Stimulation: Delivers electric current to the skin, causing nerve endings to fire. Advantages include flexibility in parameters, good spatial resolution, and small electrode size. However, it can cause sharp/burning pain and requires frequent calibration. It is faster and requires less power than vibrotactile devices. It has been effective for hand aperture, wrist rotation, and finger flexion feedback, showing high success rates for both able-bodied and amputee subjects.
- Skin-Stretch Stimulation: Haptic devices that stretch or apply pressure to the skin. They are accurate with good intensity range and resolution. They are capable of transmitting complex combinations of velocity, timing, and static force sense. Skin stretch generally provides a more intuitive mapping for position and a more realistic sense of velocity compared to vibrotactile feedback. However, they can be heavy, cumbersome, energy-consuming, and have slow responses due to mechanical inertia. Examples include devices for cursor movement, virtual elbow angle, hand aperture, and integrating with tendon-vibration illusion (TVI).
- Auditory Sensory Substitution (Sonification): Encodes afferent information by modulating pitch, timbre, or volume of sounds. It is light, fast, and low-power, leveraging the strong association between auditory and motor areas. However, it interferes with normal hearing and requires significant attentional resources. Results in proprioceptive substitution have been modest.
- Augmented Reality (AR) Feedback: Increases visual information via head-mounted displays. It has been successfully integrated into sensorimotor control, allowing users to decouple information and execute tasks more consistently. Similar to auditory feedback, it can interfere with normal vision and requires additional devices.

Kinesthetic vs. Kinetic Feedback:
- Kinesthetic Feedback (Position, Movement, Configuration): Muscle spindles are key physiological receptors for position and movement. In prosthetics, rotary encoders or biomimetic approaches can emulate muscle length changes. Cutaneous mechanoreceptors also contribute to kinesthetic senses. Vibrotactile and skin-stretch stimulations are common strategies for this.
- Kinetic Feedback (Force, Tension, Effort): Golgi tendon organs (GTOs) are responsible for sensing tendon tension and muscle load. Artificial sensing can use tension sensors or measure current fed to prosthetic motors. While vibrotactile devices can create kinesthetic sensations homomodally, a force/heaviness sensation cannot be elicited in this way with current technology. Electrotactile stimulation and AR feedback have shown promise in conveying grasping force information. EMG activity itself, as an anticipatory input, has also been shown to improve force control.

Impact and Future Directions:
While no single "best approach" for non-invasive proprioceptive feedback has emerged, vibrotactile, electrotactile, and skin-stretch stimulation have shown better and more consistent results, considering both kinesthetic and grip force information.
- Motor Control: Proprioceptive feedback can reduce performance variability. While vision is dominant in simple tasks, proprioceptive inputs are crucial for coordinating multi-joint movements in more complex prostheses. Longer training periods are often needed for users to integrate new artificial feedback into their sensorimotor dynamics.
- Prosthesis Embodiment: Proprioceptive feedback has the potential to enhance the sense of owning and controlling artificial limbs by promoting inter-sensory congruency. This is less extensively studied for proprioception than for touch feedback, but promising results have been found with peripheral nerve stimulation and TVI in nerve-transfer patients.
- Cognitive Load: Additional feedback can reduce the cognitive load required to control a prosthesis, freeing attentional resources that would otherwise be dedicated to constant visual monitoring. Physiological parameters like fNIRS, EEG, ECG, and EDA can be used to objectively measure this reduction.
- Brain Plasticity and Phantom Limb Pain (PLP): Proprioceptive inputs continuously update internal models for motor planning. Measuring neurophysiological parameters (e.g., SEPs, MEPs) can provide objective evidence of artificial input's influence on cognitive processes. Given that plastic changes in sensorimotor circuits are believed to underlie PLP, and proprioception plays a key role, investigating the effects of artificial proprioception on cortical plasticity and phantom limb phenomena is crucial for amputees. Providing congruent proprioceptive information from an artificial limb could address the "defective opening" in the prosthetic motor control loop.

Further technological development is needed for smaller, lower-power stimulators and better encoding algorithms. Evaluation should move beyond single-modality assessments to integrated, ecological tasks that better reflect real-life use and allow for longer training periods.
