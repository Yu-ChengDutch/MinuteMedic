Introduction to Antibiotics: History and Fundamental Concepts

The discovery of antibiotics represents one of the most significant medical advancements in human history, fundamentally transforming our ability to treat bacterial infections. Before the antibiotic era, minor infections could prove fatal, and surgical procedures carried tremendous risk of postoperative infection. The term antibiotic originates from the Greek words "anti" (against) and "bios" (life), referring to substances produced by microorganisms that inhibit or kill other microorganisms.

Historical Milestones

The modern antibiotic era began in 1928 with Alexander Fleming's serendipitous discovery of penicillin from the mold Penicillium notatum. However, it wasn't until the 1940s that Howard Florey and Ernst Chain developed methods for mass production, enabling widespread clinical use during World War II. The period from 1940-1960 is often called the "golden age of antibiotics," witnessing the discovery of most major antibiotic classes we use today.

Fundamental Concepts

Antibiotics can be classified based on their mechanism of action:

  1. Cell wall synthesis inhibitors (e.g., β-lactams, glycopeptides)
  2. Protein synthesis inhibitors (e.g., aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, macrolides)
  3. Nucleic acid synthesis inhibitors (e.g., quinolones, rifamycins)
  4. Metabolic pathway inhibitors (e.g., sulfonamides, trimethoprim)
  5. Membrane disruptors (e.g., polymyxins, daptomycin)

Understanding these mechanisms is crucial for selecting appropriate therapy, predicting side effects, and combating resistance. Antibiotics may be bactericidal (killing bacteria) or bacteriostatic (inhibiting growth), though this distinction can depend on concentration and bacterial species.

Antibiotic Resistance and Stewardship

The emergence of antibiotic resistance poses one of the most serious threats to global health. Resistance occurs when bacteria evolve mechanisms to withstand antibiotics that would normally kill them or inhibit their growth. This natural evolutionary process has been dramatically accelerated by misuse and overuse of antibiotics in human medicine and agriculture.

Mechanisms of Resistance

Bacteria employ several strategies to resist antibiotics:

  1. Enzymatic inactivation: Production of enzymes that modify or destroy antibiotics (e.g., β-lactamases that hydrolyze β-lactam antibiotics)
  2. Target modification: Alteration of antibiotic binding sites (e.g., penicillin-binding protein changes in MRSA)
  3. Efflux pumps: Active transport of antibiotics out of the bacterial cell
  4. Reduced permeability: Decreased antibiotic uptake through cell wall/membrane changes
  5. Bypass pathways: Development of alternative metabolic pathways

Antibiotic Stewardship

Antibiotic stewardship programs aim to optimize antibiotic use to improve patient outcomes while minimizing unintended consequences. Key principles include:

  1. Right drug: Selecting the most targeted, narrow-spectrum antibiotic effective against the likely pathogen
  2. Right dose: Using pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic principles to determine optimal dosing
  3. Right duration: Treating for the shortest effective duration
  4. De-escalation: Transitioning from broad-spectrum to narrow-spectrum therapy when possible
  5. Prevention: Emphasizing infection prevention measures to reduce antibiotic need

Beta-Lactam Antibiotics

β-lactam antibiotics are the most commonly prescribed antibiotic class, characterized by their β-lactam ring structure. This four-membered ring binds to and inhibits penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), enzymes critical for bacterial cell wall synthesis. Inhibition leads to defective cell wall formation and bacterial lysis.

Major Subclasses

  1. Penicillins: Natural (penicillin G, V), anti-staphylococcal (nafcillin, oxacillin), aminopenicillins (ampicillin, amoxicillin), antipseudomonal (piperacillin)
  2. Cephalosporins: Divided into generations based on spectrum (1st: cefazolin; 3rd: ceftriaxone; 5th: ceftaroline)
  3. Carbapenems: Broadest spectrum β-lactams (imipenem, meropenem, ertapenem)
  4. Monobactams (aztreonam): Primarily active against Gram-negatives
  5. β-lactamase inhibitor combinations: Clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam paired with various β-lactams

Clinical Considerations

Spectrum of activity varies significantly among subclasses. Natural penicillins primarily target Gram-positives and some anaerobes, while later-generation cephalosporins and carbapenems cover many Gram-negatives. Resistance commonly occurs via β-lactamase production or PBP modification (e.g., MRSA).

Adverse effects include hypersensitivity reactions (ranging from rash to anaphylaxis), neurotoxicity (especially with high doses or renal impairment), and Clostridioides difficile infection. Cross-reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins occurs in about 1-3% of patients with penicillin allergy.

Aminoglycosides

Aminoglycosides are potent bactericidal antibiotics that bind to the 30S ribosomal subunit, causing misreading of mRNA and inhibition of protein synthesis. Their concentration-dependent killing makes them particularly effective when high peak concentrations are achieved.

Key Agents

Common aminoglycosides include gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, and streptomycin. Each has slightly different spectra:

  1. Gentamicin: Most commonly used, active against many Gram-negatives and some Gram-positives (e.g., synergistic with β-lactams for enterococcal endocarditis)
  2. Tobramycin: Slightly more active against Pseudomonas than gentamicin
  3. Amikacin: Most resistant to aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes, often used for resistant Gram-negatives

Clinical Use

Aminoglycosides are primarily used for:

  1. Severe Gram-negative infections (often combined with β-lactams for synergy)
  2. Empiric treatment of sepsis
  3. Combination therapy for endocarditis (with cell-wall active agents)
  4. Topical formulations for ocular and skin infections

Toxicity

The major limitations are nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity (both vestibular and cochlear). Risk factors include prolonged use, high trough levels, concomitant nephrotoxins, and pre-existing renal impairment. Monitoring includes:

  1. Serum drug levels (peak and trough)
  2. Renal function tests
  3. Baseline and serial audiometry for prolonged courses

Tetracyclines

Tetracyclines are broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotics that reversibly bind to the 30S ribosomal subunit, blocking tRNA attachment and inhibiting protein synthesis. The class includes natural compounds (tetracycline, chlortetracycline) and semi-synthetic derivatives (doxycycline, minocycline, tigecycline).

Spectrum and Uses

Tetracyclines have activity against:

  1. Atypical bacteria (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Rickettsia)
  2. Some Gram-positives and Gram-negatives
  3. Spirochetes (Borrelia burgdorferi causing Lyme disease)
  4. Certain parasites (e.g., malaria)

Key clinical uses include:

  1. Community-acquired pneumonia (atypical coverage)
  2. Lyme disease
  3. Acne vulgaris (long-term low-dose doxycycline)
  4. Rickettsial infections (e.g., Rocky Mountain spotted fever)
  5. Brucellosis (with rifampin or streptomycin)

Pharmacokinetics

Doxycycline has excellent oral bioavailability and is preferred for most indications. Tigecycline (a glycylcycline) has expanded Gram-positive coverage but carries a black box warning for increased mortality in serious infections. Tetracyclines chelate divalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺), requiring separation from antacids, dairy, and supplements by 2-3 hours.

Adverse Effects

  1. Photosensitivity (especially doxycycline)
  2. Tooth discoloration and enamel hypoplasia (contraindicated in pregnancy and children <8 years)
  3. Esophageal irritation (take with ample water, avoid bedtime dosing)
  4. Benign intracranial hypertension (rare)
  5. Vestibular toxicity (minocycline)

Macrolides

Macrolides are bacteriostatic antibiotics that bind to the 50S ribosomal subunit, blocking peptide chain elongation. The class includes erythromycin (original agent), clarithromycin, and azithromycin (azalide subclass).

Spectrum and Clinical Use

Macrolides are particularly valuable for:

  1. Atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella)
  2. Community-acquired pneumonia (Streptococcus pneumoniae, though resistance is increasing)
  3. Pertussis (erythromycin)
  4. Mycobacterial infections (clarithromycin for MAC)
  5. STIs (azithromycin for chlamydia)
  6. Gastric motility (erythromycin as a motilin agonist)

Pharmacokinetics

Key differences exist among agents:

  1. Azithromycin: Long tissue half-life allowing short-course therapy (e.g., 5-day or even single-dose regimens)
  2. Clarithromycin: Metabolized by CYP3A4, with an active metabolite
  3. Erythromycin: Short half-life requiring QID dosing, potent CYP3A4 inhibitor

Adverse Effects

  1. GI upset (especially erythromycin - stimulates motilin receptors)
  2. QT prolongation (risk of torsades de pointes, especially with azithromycin in high-risk patients)
  3. Hepatotoxicity (cholestatic hepatitis with erythromycin estolate)
  4. Drug interactions (erythromycin and clarithromycin are strong CYP3A4 inhibitors)
  5. Ototoxicity (high doses, especially in renal impairment)

Fluoroquinolones

Fluoroquinolones are broad-spectrum bactericidal antibiotics that inhibit bacterial DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II) and topoisomerase IV, preventing DNA replication and transcription. The fluorine atom at position 6 distinguishes fluoroquinolones from earlier quinolones (e.g., nalidixic acid).

Generations and Spectrum

Fluoroquinolones are classified by generation:

  1. 1st: Narrow Gram-negative (norfloxacin, ciprofloxacin)
  2. 2nd: Expanded Gram-positive (levofloxacin, ofloxacin)
  3. 3rd: Respiratory fluoroquinolones with enhanced Gram-positive and atypical coverage (moxifloxacin, gemifloxacin)
  4. 4th: Broadest spectrum including anaerobes (delafloxacin)

Clinical Uses

Fluoroquinolones are used for:

  1. UTIs (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin)
  2. Intra-abdominal infections (combined with metronidazole)
  3. Respiratory infections (community-acquired pneumonia, exacerbations of COPD)
  4. Bone/joint infections (especially Gram-negative)
  5. Anthrax (ciprofloxacin)

Adverse Effects and Black Box Warnings

FDA warnings limit fluoroquinolone use due to:

  1. Tendon rupture (risk increases with age >60, corticosteroids, renal disease)
  2. Peripheral neuropathy (may be irreversible)
  3. CNS effects (insomnia, dizziness, rarely seizures)
  4. QT prolongation (especially moxifloxacin)
  5. Hypoglycemia (enhanced insulin secretion, particularly with gatifloxacin - now withdrawn)
  6. Aortic dissection (recent association with cumulative use)

Rifamycins (Rifampin/Rifabutin)

Rifamycins are bactericidal antibiotics that inhibit DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, blocking transcription. Rifampin is the prototypical agent, with rifabutin having similar activity but fewer drug interactions.

Spectrum and Uses

Rifamycins have unparalleled activity against:

  1. Mycobacterium tuberculosis (first-line TB therapy)
  2. Mycobacterium leprae (leprosy)
  3. Staphylococcus aureus (used for MSSA/MRSA in combination therapy)
  4. Neisseria meningitidis (prophylaxis)

Unique uses include:

  1. Latent TB treatment (short-course rifampin or rifapentine)
  2. Prosthetic joint infection (biofilm-active in combination)
  3. Brucellosis (with doxycycline)
  4. MRSA bacteremia (adjunct to vancomycin/daptomycin)

Pharmacokinetics

Rifampin is a potent inducer of CYP450 enzymes (particularly 3A4), accelerating metabolism of many drugs (e.g., warfarin, oral contraceptives, antiretrovirals). It turns body fluids orange-red (harmless but alarming). Rifabutin causes less enzyme induction and is preferred in HIV patients on antiretrovirals.

Adverse Effects

  1. Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs, especially with pre-existing liver disease)
  2. Drug interactions (must review all concomitant medications)
  3. Flu-like syndrome (with intermittent dosing)
  4. Thrombocytopenia (rare)
  5. Orange discoloration of urine, tears, sweat

Sulfonamides and Trimethoprim

Sulfonamides and trimethoprim are synthetic antibiotics that inhibit sequential steps in folate synthesis - sulfonamides block dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS), while trimethoprim inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR). Used alone, each is bacteriostatic; combined (as co-trimoxazole), they become bactericidal.

Spectrum and Uses

Co-trimoxazole (TMP-SMX) has broad activity against:

  1. Gram-positives (including some MRSA)
  2. Gram-negatives (E. coli, Klebsiella, Salmonella, Shigella)
  3. Pneumocystis jirovecii (treatment and prophylaxis)
  4. Toxoplasma gondii (prophylaxis in AIDS)
  5. Nocardia

Key indications include:

  1. UTIs (though resistance is increasing)
  2. PCP pneumonia (high-dose IV for moderate-severe)
  3. MRSA skin infections
  4. Traveler's diarrhea (in areas with susceptible organisms)
  5. Prophylaxis in immunocompromised hosts

Adverse Effects

  1. Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome - higher risk in HIV)
  2. Hematologic toxicity (megaloblastic anemia, leukopenia - especially in folate deficiency)
  3. Hyperkalemia (trimethoprim blocks renal epithelial sodium channels, similar to amiloride)
  4. Renal toxicity (crystalluria with sulfonamides, especially in acidic urine)
  5. Photosensitivity
  6. Drug interactions (sulfonamides potentiate warfarin, sulfonylureas)
