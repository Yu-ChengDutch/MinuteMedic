Introduction to Articular Inflammations

Joint Anatomy Fundamentals

To understand articular inflammations, we must first appreciate the complex anatomy of synovial joints, which are the most common sites of inflammatory pathology. A typical synovial joint consists of:

  1. Articular cartilage: Hyaline cartilage covering bone ends, providing smooth articulation and load distribution
  2. Synovium: Vascularized connective tissue lining the joint capsule (except cartilage surfaces), producing synovial fluid
  3. Joint capsule: Fibrous tissue surrounding the joint, providing stability
  4. Synovial fluid: Viscous ultrafiltrate of plasma containing hyaluronic acid and lubricin
  5. Subchondral bone: Bone immediately beneath cartilage, crucial for nutrient exchange

Histological Features

The synovium normally has a thin (1-3 cell) lining layer (intima) containing both macrophage-like synoviocytes (type A) and fibroblast-like synoviocytes (type B). The subintima contains loose connective tissue with blood vessels, nerves, and occasional immune cells. In inflammatory states, this architecture undergoes dramatic changes:

  1. Synovial hyperplasia (lining layer thickening to 5-10 cells)
  2. Neovascularization
  3. Inflammatory cell infiltration (lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages)
  4. Pannus formation (destructive granulation tissue)

Physiology of Joint Inflammation

Joint inflammation represents a complex interplay between innate and adaptive immunity. The inflammatory cascade involves:

  1. Initiation: Pattern recognition receptors (e.g., TLRs) detect DAMPs/PAMPs
  2. Amplification: Cytokine networks (TNF-α (the "master regulator"), IL-1, IL-6) activate endothelium and recruit leukocytes
  3. Chronicity: In autoimmune conditions, T-cell/B-cell interactions maintain inflammation via autoantibodies and memory responses
  4. Tissue damage: Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), reactive oxygen species, and osteoclasts degrade tissues

Diagnostic Approach to Articular Inflammations

Key Differentiating Features

Articular inflammations can be categorized based on several clinical and laboratory parameters:

  1. Pattern of joint involvement:
    1. Monoarthritis (1 joint) - consider septic, crystal, trauma
    2. Oligoarthritis (2-4 joints) - consider reactive, psoriatic
    3. Polyarthritis (>4 joints) - consider RA, SLE, viral
  2. Duration of symptoms:
    1. Acute (<6 weeks) - infection, crystal, trauma
    2. Chronic (>6 weeks) - RA, OA, seronegative spondyloarthropathies
  3. Inflammatory vs. mechanical:
    1. Inflammatory: Morning stiffness >1h, improves with activity
    2. Mechanical: Pain worsens with activity, minimal stiffness

Diagnostic Algorithm

A systematic approach should include:

  1. History: Onset, pattern, aggravating/relieving factors, extra-articular symptoms
  2. Examination: Joint count, swelling characteristics, distribution
  3. Laboratory:
    1. Acute phase reactants (CRP, ESR)
    2. Autoantibodies (RF, ACPA, ANA)
    3. Synovial fluid analysis (cell count, crystals, culture)
  4. Imaging:
    1. X-ray (erosions, osteophytes, joint space)
    2. Ultrasound (synovitis, effusions)
    3. MRI (early bone edema, soft tissue assessment)
Mnemonic: "CRYSTAL" for synovial fluid analysis
Color (straw-colored normal), Rheumatoid factor, Yield (volume), Strain (Gram, ZN), Turbidity, Autoantibodies, Leukocyte count

Gout and Crystal Arthropathies

Pathophysiology

Gout results from deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in joints and tissues due to chronic hyperuricemia (serum urate >6.8 mg/dL). The sequence involves:

  1. Urate crystal formation: Solubility threshold exceeded in extracellular fluid
  2. NLRP3 inflammasome activation: Crystals are phagocytosed, triggering IL-1β release
  3. Neutrophil recruitment: Intense inflammatory response with pain, swelling
  4. Chronic tophaceous deposits: With prolonged hyperuricemia (>5 years)

Clinical Features

Classic presentation includes:

  1. Acute gouty arthritis:
    1. Sudden severe pain (often nocturnal)
    2. First MTP joint most common (podagra)
    3. Erythema, swelling, exquisite tenderness
    4. Self-limited (3-10 days) if untreated
  2. Intercritical gout: Asymptomatic periods between attacks
  3. Chronic tophaceous gout: Tophi (urate deposits) in joints, bursae, ears

Diagnosis

The gold standard is synovial fluid analysis showing:

  1. Needle-shaped, negatively birefringent crystals under polarized microscopy
  2. High WBC count (10,000-70,000/μL, predominantly neutrophils)

Serum urate may be normal during acute attacks (30% cases).

Mnemonic: "GOUT" for risk factors
Genetics, Obesity, Under-excretion (renal), Thiazides/alcohol

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Pathogenesis

RA is a chronic systemic autoimmune disease characterized by symmetric inflammatory polyarthritis. The pathophysiology involves:

  1. Genetic predisposition: HLA-DRB1 shared epitope, PTPN22
  2. Autoantibody production:
    1. Rheumatoid factor (IgM anti-IgG) - 70-80%
    2. Anti-citrullinated peptide antibodies (ACPAs) - more specific
  3. Synovitis:
    1. Lymphoid infiltration (CD4+ T cells, B cells)
    2. Synovial fibroblast activation
    3. Pannus formation eroding cartilage/bone

Clinical Features

Diagnosis requires the 2010 ACR/EULAR classification criteria (score ≥6/10):

  1. Joint involvement:
    1. 1 medium-large joint (0)
    2. 2-10 large joints (1)
    3. 1-3 small joints (2)
    4. 4-10 small joints (3)
    5. >10 joints (including ≥1 small) (5)
  2. Serology: RF and ACPA (low positive 2, high positive 3)
  3. Acute phase reactants: Abnormal CRP/ESR (1)
  4. Symptom duration: ≥6 weeks (1)

Extra-articular Manifestations

RA can affect multiple systems:

  1. Pulmonary: ILD, nodules, pleuritis
  2. Cardiac: Pericarditis, accelerated atherosclerosis
  3. Ocular: Keratoconjunctivitis sicca, scleritis
  4. Hematologic: Felty's syndrome, anemia of chronic disease
Mnemonic: "RA DAMAGE" for extra-articular manifestations
Rheumatoid nodules, Anemia, Dry eyes/mouth, Atherosclerosis, Myositis, Amyloidosis, GI (sicca), Eye (scleritis)

Osteoarthritis (OA)

Pathophysiology

OA is characterized by progressive cartilage degradation with secondary bone changes. Unlike inflammatory arthropathies, OA is primarily a mechanical disorder with low-grade inflammation. Key features:

  1. Cartilage breakdown:
    1. MMP overexpression degrading collagen/proteoglycans
    2. Decreased aggrecan synthesis by chondrocytes
  2. Subchondral bone changes:
    1. Sclerosis
    2. Osteophyte formation (marginal lipping)
    3. Subchondral cysts
  3. Synovial inflammation: Mild secondary synovitis

Clinical Features

OA typically presents with:

  1. Pain:
    1. Mechanical pattern (worse with activity, better with rest)
    2. Often worse at end of day
    3. Brief morning stiffness (<30 min)
  2. Joint involvement:
    1. Weight-bearing joints (knees, hips)
    2. Distal interphalangeal joints (DIPs) - Heberden's nodes
    3. Proximal interphalangeal joints (PIPs) - Bouchard's nodes
    4. First CMC joint

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical and radiographic. X-ray findings include:

  1. Joint space narrowing (asymmetric)
  2. Osteophytes
  3. Subchondral sclerosis
  4. Subchondral cysts

Synovial fluid analysis shows non-inflammatory pattern (WBC <2000/μL).

Mnemonic: "LOSS" for OA x-ray findings
Loss of joint space, Osteophytes, Subchondral sclerosis, Subchondral cysts

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Pathophysiology

Polymyalgia rheumatica is an inflammatory disorder characterized by proximal muscle pain and stiffness, predominantly affecting individuals >50 years. While its exact etiology remains unclear, key features include:

  1. Genetic predisposition: Strong association with HLA-DR4
  2. Environmental triggers: Possible viral etiology (e.g., parvovirus B19)
  3. Inflammatory cascade:
    1. Elevated IL-6 levels correlate with disease activity
    2. Perivascular infiltration of CD4+ T cells in synovium
    3. Non-destructive synovitis of proximal joints (shoulders, hips)

Clinical Features

Diagnosis is clinical based on the 2012 EULAR/ACR classification criteria (score ≥4 without ultrasound, ≥5 with ultrasound):

  1. Morning stiffness >45 minutes (2 points)
  2. Hip pain/limited ROM (1 point)
  3. Absence of RF/ACPA (2 points)
  4. Absence of other joint involvement (1 point)
  5. Ultrasound findings: Subdeltoid bursitis, biceps tenosynovitis, glenohumeral synovitis (1 point)

Furthermore, PMR often involves a rapid increase in both ESR (sedementation rate) and CRP, which are both markers of inflammation. Conversely, ACPA and RF are usually negative, as is creatine kinase, which is a marker of muscle damage. If the latter is positive, that'd be a reason to investigate myopathies.

Key associations:

  1. Giant cell arteritis (GCA) in 15-30% of cases
  2. Fever, weight loss, fatigue (constitutional symptoms)

Mnemonic: "PMR PAIN"
Proximal muscle pain, Morning stiffness >1h, Rapid ESR elevation, Prednisone-responsive, Age >50, IL-6 elevated, No muscle weakness

Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition (CPPD) Disease

Pathophysiology

CPPD disease results from deposition of calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate crystals in joints. The spectrum includes:

  1. Asymptomatic chondrocalcinosis: Radiographic findings only
  2. Acute CPP crystal arthritis ("pseudogout"): Inflammatory arthritis
  3. Chronic CPP inflammatory arthritis: RA-like presentation

Risk factors:

  1. Advanced age
  2. Osteoarthritis
  3. Hyperparathyroidism
  4. Hemochromatosis
  5. Hypomagnesemia

Clinical Features

Pseudogout classically presents as:

  1. Acute mono/oligoarthritis (knee most common)
  2. Severe pain, swelling, erythema (mimics septic arthritis)
  3. Precipitated by illness, surgery, or trauma

Diagnosis:

  1. Synovial fluid analysis:
    1. Positively birefringent rhomboid crystals
    2. WBC 10,000-50,000/μL
  2. X-ray findings:
    1. Chondrocalcinosis (linear calcifications in fibrocartilage)
    2. Degenerative changes

Mnemonic: "3 H's of CPPD"
Hemochromatosis, Hyperparathyroidism, Hypomagnesemia

Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)

Pathogenesis

Psoriatic arthritis is a heterogeneous inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis, classified under spondyloarthropathies. Key features:

  1. Genetic factors: HLA-B27 (especially in axial disease), HLA-C*06:02
  2. Immune mechanisms:
    1. IL-23/Th17 pathway activation
    2. Entheseal inflammation (insertion sites of tendons/ligaments)
  3. Clinical patterns (Moll & Wright classification):
    1. Oligoarticular (≤4 joints, asymmetric)
    2. Polyarticular (RA-like, symmetric)
    3. Distal interphalangeal (DIP) predominant
    4. Spondylitis (axial involvement)
    5. Arthritis mutilans (severe osteolysis)

Clinical Features

Musculoskeletal manifestations:

  1. Dactylitis ("sausage digit") - pathognomonic
  2. Enthesitis (Achilles, plantar fascia)
  3. Nail changes: Pitting, onycholysis, oil spots
  4. Axial involvement: Sacroiliitis (often asymmetric)

Diagnosis (CASPAR criteria - ≥3 points):

  1. Psoriasis (current=2, history/family=1)
  2. Psoriatic nail dystrophy (1)
  3. Negative RF (1)
  4. Dactylitis (1)
  5. Juxta-articular new bone formation on x-ray (1)

Mnemonic: "PSORIASIS" for PsA features
Pitting nails, Sausage digits, Oligo/polyarthritis, RF negative, Irregular joint involvement, Asymmetric, Spondylitis, Insertional tendonitis, Skin plaques

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) Arthritis

Pathophysiology

Joint involvement in SLE differs fundamentally from RA:

  1. Non-erosive synovitis:
    1. Lymphocytic infiltration without pannus formation
    2. Minimal synovial hyperplasia
  2. Immune complex deposition:
    1. Anti-dsDNA antibodies correlate with disease activity
    2. Complement activation (low C3/C4 during flares)
  3. Jaccoud's arthropathy:
    1. Reversible deformities (ulnar deviation, swan neck) due to ligament laxity
    2. No bone erosions on imaging

Clinical Features

Musculoskeletal manifestations:

  1. Arthralgias (90% of patients) - often disproportionate to exam findings
  2. Non-erosive polyarthritis (symmetrical small joints)
  3. Tendonitis/tenosynovitis

Diagnostic clues:

  1. Positive ANA (nearly 100%)
  2. Anti-dsDNA/Sm antibodies (specific)
  3. Synovial fluid: Mildly inflammatory (WBC 3,000-10,000/μL)

Differential from RA

Key distinguishing features:

  1. Non-erosive (except rare "rhupus" overlap)
  2. Morning stiffness <1 hour
  3. Extra-articular features: Malar rash, nephritis, cytopenias

Mnemonic: "LUPUS ARTHRITIS"
Lax ligaments (Jaccoud's), Usually non-erosive, Pain > swelling, Usually reversible, Symmetric, ANA positive, RF negative (usually), Tenosynovitis common, Hands/wrists affected, Rare bone damage, Inflammatory markers may be normal, Tender points possible, Instability without erosion, Systemic symptoms
