Introduction to Depression, Psychosis, and Their Pharmacological Treatment

Depression and psychosis are debilitating psychiatric disorders with distinct yet sometimes overlapping neurobiological underpinnings. Depression is characterized by persistent sadness, anhedonia, cognitive impairment, and somatic symptoms, while psychosis involves hallucinations, delusions, and disorganized thinking. Both conditions arise from dysregulation in monoaminergic (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine) and glutamatergic pathways.

Neuroanatomy and Neurophysiology

The limbic system (amygdala, hippocampus, prefrontal cortex) modulates mood and is hypoactive in depression. The mesolimbic and mesocortical dopamine pathways are implicated in psychosis, with hyperactivity in the mesolimbic pathway contributing to positive symptoms (e.g., hallucinations) and hypoactivity in the mesocortical pathway leading to negative symptoms (e.g., blunted affect).

Historical Context

The discovery of imipramine (the first tricyclic antidepressant, TCA) in the 1950s revolutionized depression treatment, while chlorpromazine (the first antipsychotic) transformed psychosis management by blocking dopamine D2 receptors. The monoamine hypothesis of depression posits that deficits in serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine (NE), and dopamine (DA) underlie depressive symptoms, whereas the dopamine hypothesis of psychosis suggests excessive dopaminergic transmission in subcortical regions.

Depression: Treatment Schedule

Treatment of depression follows a stepwise approach:

  1. First-line: Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs, e.g., fluoxetine) or serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs, e.g., venlafaxine).
  2. Second-line: Atypical antidepressants (e.g., bupropion, mirtazapine) or augmentation with lithium/thyroid hormone.
  3. Third-line: Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs, e.g., amitriptyline) or monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs, e.g., phenelzine).
  4. Adjunctive therapies: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) for refractory cases.

Key Considerations

Onset of action: Antidepressants typically require 2–6 weeks for clinical effect due to downstream neuroplasticity changes (e.g., BDNF upregulation). SSRIs are preferred due to their favorable side-effect profile, whereas TCAs are reserved for treatment-resistant cases due to anticholinergic/cardiotoxic risks.

Mnemonic: "START LOW, GO SLOW" – Antidepressants should be initiated at low doses to minimize side effects (e.g., SSRI-induced GI distress) and titrated gradually.

Psychosis: Treatment Schedule

Antipsychotics are classified as first-generation (FGAs, typical) or second-generation (SGAs, atypical). FGAs (e.g., haloperidol) primarily block D2 receptors, while SGAs (e.g., risperidone) target both D2 and 5-HT2A receptors.

Acute Phase

SGAs (e.g., olanzapine, quetiapine) are first-line due to lower extrapyramidal side effect (EPS) risk. FGAs may be used for rapid sedation (e.g., haloperidol + lorazepam IM).

Maintenance Phase

Long-acting injectables (LAIs, e.g., paliperidone) improve adherence. Clozapine is reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (failure of ≥2 antipsychotics).

Mnemonic: "CLOZAPINE is the KING of antipsychotics" – Clozapine is the most effective but requires weekly ANC monitoring due to agranulocytosis risk.

Antidepressants: Pharmacodynamics, Pharmacokinetics, and Adverse Effects

SSRIs (e.g., Fluoxetine, Sertraline)

Mechanism: Inhibit SERT (serotonin transporter), increasing synaptic 5-HT. Side effects: GI distress, sexual dysfunction, SIADH, QT prolongation (citalopram). PK: Hepatic metabolism (CYP2D6/CYP3A4); fluoxetine has a long half-life (active metabolite norfluoxetine).

SNRIs (e.g., Venlafaxine, Duloxetine)

Mechanism: Inhibit SERT + NET (norepinephrine transporter). Side effects: Hypertension (dose-dependent NE effect), sweating, nausea. PK: Venlafaxine is a CYP2D6 substrate.

TCAs (e.g., Amitriptyline)

Mechanism: Inhibit SERT + NET + anticholinergic/antihistaminergic effects. Side effects: Orthostatic hypotension, arrhythmias (Na+ channel blockade), dry mouth. PK: Highly protein-bound, CYP2D6 metabolism.

Mnemonic: "TCAs are DIRTY drugs" – They have Diverse Interactions and Risky Toxicities (e.g., overdose lethality).

Antipsychotics: Pharmacodynamics, Pharmacokinetics, and Adverse Effects

FGAs (e.g., Haloperidol)

Mechanism: Potent D2 antagonism. Side effects: EPS (parkinsonism, dystonia, akathisia), tardive dyskinesia, hyperprolactinemia. PK: CYP3A4 metabolism.

SGAs (e.g., Risperidone, Olanzapine)

Mechanism: D2 + 5-HT2A antagonism. Side effects: Metabolic syndrome (weight gain, diabetes), sedation (H1 blockade), orthostasis (α1 blockade). PK: Olanzapine is metabolized by CYP1A2 (smoking ↓ levels).

Mnemonic: "OLANZAPINE is HEAVY" – High risk of weight gain, sedation, and metabolic effects.

Off-Label and Non-Psychiatric Uses

Antidepressants

TCAs: Neuropathic pain (amitriptyline), migraine prophylaxis. SSRIs: Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD), OCD. Bupropion: Smoking cessation.

Antipsychotics

Quetiapine: Insomnia (low-dose). Haloperidol: Delirium. Aripiprazole: Augmentation in MDD.
