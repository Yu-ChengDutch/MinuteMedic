Introduction

Sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) can be separated into categories based on aetiology:

  1. Bacteria: Chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis
  2. Virus: Genital warts (HPV), genital herpes, HIV/AIDS
  3. Parasites: Trichomoniasis

In the developed world, diagnostic tests are easily available but the situation is different in the developing world. However, even without HIV/AIDS, roughly 1.1 billion people wordlwide currently have STDs. Roughly 500 milion have syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia or trichomoniasis, and an additional 530 milion suffer from genital warts.

Bacterial STDs

Chlamydia

Caused by C. Trachomatis, a gram-negative obligate intracellular bacterium, it is the most common bacterial STD in humans and the leading cause of infectious blindness. It is transmitted during sex or direct contacted with infected tissue; or from mother to child during childbirth. It is diagnosed using PCR from a swab performed on the cervix or urethra or even on voided urine.

70% of infected women remain asymptomatic, but about 50% of asymptomatic female patients will develop pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) which may develop into scarring inside the reproductive organs, which in turn may develop into chronic pelvic pain, fertility problems, ectopic pregancy and other complications. When they do become symptomatic, women often display vaginal bleeding or discharge, abdominal pain, painful intercourse, fever, painful urination or urinary urgency. Hence, screening is recommended in those under 25 or others at risk of infection.

50% of men remain asymptomatic, and the other half shows urethritis which presents as painful urination, penile discharge, testicular pain/swelling, fever. If untreated, it may cause epididymitis which could rarely lead to sterility. Potentially, it could also cause prostatitis but this is uncertain.

Trachoma

Caused by the same pathogen, trachoma is a chronic conjunctivitis which may be transmitted from eye to eye by fingers i.e., shared towels or cloths, coughing, sneezing, eye-seeking flies etc.

It presents as mucopurulent ocular discharge, irritation, redness and lid swelling. The WHO aims to have erradicated this disease by 2030 using hygiene measures.

Other complications

Patients may develop reactive arthritis, presenting with the triad of conjunctivitis, arthritis and urethral inflammation which occurs especially in young men.

A different serovar may cause lymphogranuloma venereum which is an infection of the lymph nodes and lymphatics. It presents as genital ulceration and lymphadenopathy in the groin, but may include rectal inflammation, fever or generalised lymphadenopathy.

Treatment

Chlamydia should be treated using macrolides, most effectively so by azithromycin, single dose. After three months, the patient should be tested again to check for reinfectin. Partners should also be treated.

Gonorrhea

Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoea, a gram-negative diplococcus bacterum, it has severe complications when untreated. It may be diagnosed in the location where infection happened: urethra/vagina/cervix urine, throat, or rectum. It is recommended to test all women under 25, MSMs and those with new sexual partners.

It usually presents 2-14 days after infection, with most symptoms appearing 4-6 days after infection. It initially causes local swelling, itching, pain and pus formation. For example in throat gonorrhea, it causes a sore through (altough 90% remain asymptomatic).

When it comes to genital gonorrhea, 50% of women remain asymptomatic but the others experience vaginal discharge, lower abdominal pain, or painful intercourse. This too may cause pelvic inflammatory disease.

When men become symptomatic (around 50% of cases), it results in urethritis which causes painful urination and discharge from the penis. Similarly, this may cause epidimytis and is associated with a risk of prostate cancer and sterility.

Neonatal conjunctivitis / Opthalmia neonatorum

When children are born to women with either Chlamydia or Gonorrhea, children might develop neonatal conjunctivitis presenting as crusty, purulent eyes. When resulting from gonorrhea it will present before 5 days after birth while if resulting from Chlamydia it will present 5-14 days after birth.

Complications

When left untreated, it may spread from the original site to joints, skin and other organs. Rarely, it may settle in the heart (endocarditis) or spinal column (meningitis). It is also possible to be allergic to N. Gonorrhea.

Treatment

Gonorrhea should be treated by IV ceftriaxone or azithromycin per os, altough antibiotic resistance is starting to play a big role. Ertapenem is a good solution when gonorrhea has become resistant. Sexual partners need to be treated too.

Syphilis

Caused by Treponema Pallidum, a spiral-shaped, gram-negative, highly mobile bacterium. Diagnosis then is made using blood tests, CSF tests for neurosyphilis or direct testing of a chancre using dark field microscopy or PCR tests.

Syphilis presents itself in stages and for its varied presentation was called "the great imitator":

  1. Primary (2-6 weeks): presents with a single, firm, painless, non-ichy chancre in 40% of cases. Else, it could present as multiple laesions, which may be tender and which be located in other places than the genitals. The main location for women is the cervix and for men the penis. Around 80% of patients have lymphadenopathy around the area of infection.
  2. Secondary (4-10 weeks): presents as a symmetrical, reddish-pink, non-itchy rash on trunk and extremities including palms and soles. The rash may become maculopapular or pustular. It may also present more generalised as fever, sore throat, malaise, weight loss etc. which is not always preceded by a chancre
  3. Latent: during early latent phase (under 2 years) includes having serologic proof of infection without symptoms but with 25% risk of recurrent secondary infection, while late latent phase (i.e., after 2 years) could last many years and sees people being less infectious
  4. Tertiary (3-15 years): gummatous syphilis, neurosyphilis or cardiovascular syphilis

Gummatous syphilis

The most benign form of tertiary disease, it may occur up to 46 years after initial exposure and is characterised by the formation of chronic gummas which are soft, tumour-like balls of inflammation of varying size. They may occur everywhere but typically affect the skin, bone, and liver.

Cardiovascular syphilis

Typically occuring 10-30 years after infection, it most commonly results in syphilitic aortitis which may result in the formation of an aortic aneurysm.

neurosyphilis and meningovascular syphilis

Neurosyphilis involves and infection of the central nervous system, which may occur at any stage of the infection.

In an early stage, meningitis is the most common presentation, usually arising within one year after infection, possibly infecting the cranial nerves. Similar symptoms may be mimicked by cerebral gummas.

Meningovascular syphilis is considered an intermediate stage, where CNS vessels inflame and occlude which causes ischemia and thus may present as stroke or spinal cord injury with the Middle Cerebral Artery being the most common target.

Parenchymal syphilis would be considere late-stage as it occurs 15-25 years after initial infection. It presents as tabes dorsalis (degeneration of the dorsal roots, which causes severe pain when coughing, and sensory ataxia), as well as general paresis. Besides pain and ataxia, tabes dorsalis may present with ataxic wide-based bait, paresthesias, loss of position and vibratory sense, Charcot joints and reduced reflexes. Similarly, there may be an Argyll Robertson pupil. Due to overall damage to the brain, neuropsychiatric symptoms may slowly appear.

Any part of the eye may be involved in neurosyphilis, most commonly the uvea resulting in uveitis. Similarly, otosyphilis refers to an infection of the N. Vestibulocochlearis presenting as issues with hearing and balance.

Treatment

Syphils is highly sensitive to penicillin, and in primary or secondary stages the treatment consists of intramuscular benzylpenicillin, which renders them non-infective in 24 hours. When allergic, doxycyline and tetracycline are safe alternatives. For neurosyphilis, intravenous penicillin G is given for 10 days.

A complication of treatment is the Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction which springs from a release of pro-inflammatory cytokines as a response to lipoproteins released from rupturing syphils bacteria. It starts within a day, and presents as fever, muscle pains, headache, tachycardia and rash.
