Milestones

When talking about age in human development in-utero, we talk about two often conflated terms Gestational Age (GA), which starts counting from the first day of the Last Menstrual Period (LMP) and timing which refers to days from fertilisation (i.e., GA - 10). When talking about stages from development, these indications of timing then refer to ideals or averages, not to actual milestones which every baby should or will reach.

Until week 8 (GA 10), we talk about the embryonic period of development, which is indeed focussed on development of the major organ systems. The first month is referred to as the neonatal period.

Week 9 until 37 (GA 11-39) are then referred to as the foetal period of development. By the start of the foetal period, all major organ systems have mostly formed and this stage is thus focussed on growth.

E comes before F in the alphabet, as embryogenesis comes before the foetus

Early embryogenesis

fertilisation takes place when the gametes fuse together, which usually happens in the ampulla of the follopian tubes. This fertilisation is dependant on three factors: chemotaxis, adhesive compatibility and acrosomal reaction.

Chemotaxis is the process that guides the movement of the spermatozoon towards the ovum. The adhesive compatibility ensures the spermatozoon actually adheres to the ovum. The acrosomal reaction finally, entails the entering spermatozoon being capped by an acrosome which is filled with enzymes that break down the zona pellucida or protective barrier around the oöcyte. On entry, the so-called zona reaction takes place, which leads the oöcyte to release cortical granules to prevent polyspermy.

Cleavage

This process starts when the newly formed zygote divides in two through mitosis. Each division takes between 12 and 24 hours. This continues until eight blastomeres, i.e., eight cells formed by cleavage, have formed within the zona pellucida of the ovum. After that point, they start to compact and form gap junctions. When the cells number sixteen, the baby is then referred to as a morula.

MORE of morula rhymes with four and there are four cleavages (i.e., 16 cells) before morulation

Blastulation

While cleavage could already be called the first stage of blastulation, the actual blastocyst forms when cells differentiate in an outer layer called the trophoblast, an inner pole called the inner cell mass and a cavity (i.e., the part within the trophoblast not covered by the inner cell mass) called the blastocoele. Eventually, the blastocoele inflates with fluid from the trophoblasts and bursts through the zona pellucida which will desintegrate away. This happens on day six of the pregnancy, and makes way for implantation. It is from the inner cell mass that the pre-embryo, amnion, yolk sac and allantois form, while the trophoblasts will form the foetal part of the placenta.

Differentiation

Shortly after implantation, namely at the beginning of week 2 the bilaminar germ discs forms which contains two layers: hypoblast (lower layer) and epiblast (upper layer). On the side of the trophoblasts, which have by now attached to the endometrium, two sub-layers form too: cytotrophoblast and synctiotrophoblast. For a while, this will be covered by Heuser's membrane. The latter will eventually develop into the yolk sac.

The upper layer, epiblast, is made of columnar layer and lies adjacent to the trophoblast. The hypoblast lies adjacent to the blastocyst cavity and is made from cuboidal cells.

Gastrulation

As the both the epiblast migrates downward (forming the amniotic cavity) and the hypoblast does the same (forming Heuser's membrane and the yolk sac), the epiblast leaves behind a primitive streak which determines the symmetric head-to-tail direction of the embryo. This takes place around day 17 of fertilisation. After, a primitive node forms, which will organise neurulation. In the centre thereof, a primitive pit forms, which connects to the directly underlying notochord. As the epiblasts from the centre of the primitive streak move downword in a process called ingression, the mesoderm forms from those epiblasts. Then, the hypoblast is pushed out of the way to form the amnion. The bilaminar disc has now formed three layers and has become trilaminar, the gastrula.

These three layers, the ectoderm, mesoderm and endoderm will form all organ structures and will be treated in their respective organ systems.

The Mesoderm

The mesoderm has forms from the epiblast through gastrulation. It has four major parts:

  1. Axial mesoderm
  2. Paraxial mesoderm
  3. Intermediate mesoderm
  4. Lateral plate mesoderm

Most important to remember are the axial mesoderm which gives rise to the notochord and the paraxial mesoderm which gives rise to the somites.
