Early development

Directly after the blastula stage, around the third week a process known as gastrulation occurs in which the embryo splits into three distinct layers:

  1. Ectoderm: Nervous system, skin, sensory organs
  2. Mesoderm: Muscles, bones, circ. system, connective tissue
  3. Endoderm: GI tract, lungs, internal organs

Immediately after, the neural plate forms on the ectoderm which entails a small section differentiating influenced by the notochord and paraxial mesoderm. Then, in process called neurulation, the edges of the neural plate fold up, fuse and form the neural tube. This tube will become the entire CNS. From this same neural plate will form the neural crest cells which will come to make up the majority of the peripheral nervous system and autonomic nervous system.

Neurulation

The process described above starts in week 4 of development (~ day 22) and starts from the cervical region going towards the tail-end. In the end, both ends, called the neuropores, stay open and remain in contact with the amniotic cavity. They eventually close a few days later (~ day 25), at which point vascularisation of the nervous system starts, as certain regions start to thicken which forms a recognisable brain, spinal cord and ventricles.

Differential development

The newly formed neural tube then starts developing in two distinct ways: the brain and the spinal cord. The cutoff for this is the fourth pair of somites.

Towards the end of the preganancy, myelination starts, which continues throughout the first postnatal year. Myelinisation occurst first in the motor roots and only then in the sensory roots. In the CNS, this is done by the oligodendrocytes.

Spinal cord

The part caudal to that pair ends up forming the spinal cord. To this end the walls thicken until only a very thin central canal is left. The neuroepithelium surrounding this canal starts out as epithelium but ends up transforming into neurons and macroglia. However, they first form an intermediate stage called neuroblasts and glio-/spongioblasts respectively. As production of neurons and glia stops, the remaining cells turn into ependymal cells which will line the canal of the spinal cord as the ependyma.

During this process of differentiation, eventually a sulcus limitans i.e., a long, thin groove on each side of the spinal cord which separates the alar (i.e., dorsal) plate from the basal (i.e., ventral) plate. As these plates span the entire length of the spinal cord, they eventually transform into the dorsal gray columns/horns and ventral grey columns/horns. Eventually, as these form, so does the ventral/anterior median fissure.

Notably, the dorsal root ganglia spring not from the neural tube but from the cells of the neural crest as they form a sort of "hybrid part" between the CNS and PNS as they extend into the visceral organs where they perform a sensory role.

At 8 weeks of gestation, the spinal cord spans the exact length of the vertebral canal. This means that the spinal nerves pass through the intervetrebral formaina at the exact level where they emerge from the cord. As the embryo grows faster than the cord, the caudal nerves have to stretch to still sprout from their vertebral canals and we end up with the cauda equina. At the end of the pregnancy, the spinal cord stops around S1. As the child continues to grow outside of the uterus, eventually, the spinal cord will end around L1 instead. That then forms the aforementioned cauda equina, where the dura and arachnoid mater end at S2 while the pia mater forms a long, thread like structure called the filum terminale which starts the the conus medullaris and runs all the way to the the first coccygeal vertebra.

Brain development

Even before the neural folds fully fuse, three vesicles may be recognised cranially to the fourth somite:

  1. Prosencephalon: Forebrain
  2. Mesencephalon: Midbrain
  3. Rhombencephalon: Hindbrain
Pro = Fore, Mes = Mid

Then, in week 4 of gestation, the primitive brain bends anteriorly which forms the midbrain and cervical flexures. Then in week 5 of gestation the:

  1. Prosencephalon -> Telencephalon & Diencephalon
  2. Rhombencephalon -> Metencephalon & Myelencephalon

This lends us with a total of five brain vesicles in week five of gestation. At this point, the sulcus limitans and hence the alar/basal plates are recognisable up and until the midbrain.

This is clear, no?

Essential in recognising the various structures then, the various flexures are essential. They form because of a different tempo of growth. The cervical flexure then, marks the transition from hindbrain (i.e., rhombencephalon) to spinal cord, while the pontine flexure marks the transition from hindbrain (i.e., rhombencephalon) to mesencephalon. The latter will eventually become the pons (hence the name) and cerebellum.

On that note, talking about the various structures in the hindbrain:

  1. Metencephalon (rostral) -> pons (basal plate), cerebellum (alar plate)
  2. Myelencephalon (caudal) - > medulla oblongata
  3. Hindbrain cavity -> fourth ventricle

The midbrain then, contains the cerebral aqueduct of Sylvius which connects the CSF to flow between the third and fourth ventricles. Here, the plates split as such:

  1. Alar plate -> Tectum (colliculi)
  2. Basal plate -> Other nuclei of the midbrain

Forebrain

Remember that the neuropores close around day 25? Well, it is around that same day that the optic vesicles develop as two outgrowths lateral to the forebrain. At the same time, on the front and back part of the developing front brain, two diverticula develop into the telencephalic vesicles, they contain cavities. The vesicles themselves will become the cerebral hemispheres, while the cavities will become the lateral ventricles, connected to the third ventricle through the foramen of Monro.

The Thalamus, Hypothalamus, mammilary bodies, and epithalamus then, derive from the lateral walls of the third ventricle.

The pituitary gland is a strange case: the anterior part (the adenohypofysis) springs from oral ectoderm (i.e., the hypophysial diverticulum a.k.a. Rathke's pouch). The posterior part (i.e., neurohypophysis) forms from a downgroth of the neuroectoderm from the diencephalon which becomes the neurohypoophysial diverticulum. With the anterior part coming up, and the posterior part coming down, in week 5 they touch and slowly the hypophysial diverticulum soon disappears.

From week 6, the corpus striatum is distinguishable at the base of each cerebral hemisphere. These hemispheres then grow much faster than the striatum (i.e., the floor), which results in C-shaped hemispheres.

Meninges

The entire neural tube and neural crest is surrounded by mesenchyme which divides into two layers: the outer becomes the dura mater and the inner the leptomeninges (i.e., arachnoid mater and pia mater). At that point, CSF will start to form.

The pia mater will eventually cover the entire roof of the fourth ventricle (i.e., hindbrain), after which she keeps proliferating, eventually extending into the fourth ventricle to form the choroid plexus. The same happens to the third ventricle. In order to manage the flow of CSF produced by these plexus, three foramen form in the fourth ventricle: two lateral foramina of Luschka and a single medial foramen of Magendie. These open up into the subarachnoid space.

Late development

In the late foetal period, so towards the end of the pregnancy, as the CNS becomes fully vascularised various microglia migrate into the CNS. Originally, they derive from mesenchymal cells which develop in bone marrow.

As the cerebral hemispheres continue to grow, the grooves (sulci) and convolutions (gyri) form so that the brain is able to expand without expanding the skull.
