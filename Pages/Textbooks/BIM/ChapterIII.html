Introduction to Imaging in Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

In the emergency setting, timely diagnosis and management of traumatic brain injury (TBI) rely heavily on both clinical assessment and neuroimaging. Recent advances in computer technology have allowed for storage and manipulation of high-resolution digital images, significantly improving the precision and speed of neuroimaging interpretation.

While many imaging modalities exist, Computed Tomography (CT) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) have become the backbone of acute and chronic TBI assessment, offering different strengths in tissue characterization, availability, and speed.

Imaging Modalities in TBI

Plain radiography is largely obsolete in evaluating TBI, offering limited detail except for skull fractures or foreign bodies. In contrast, CT scans are used as the initial diagnostic tool due to their speed, accessibility, and sensitivity to hemorrhage. Modern helical CT scanners can rapidly image the entire body, a vital feature in polytrauma scenarios.

MRI, although slower and less available in acute trauma, is invaluable in identifying subtle brain injuries, such as diffuse axonal injury (DAI) or non-hemorrhagic contusions, often invisible on CT. Its strength lies in soft tissue contrast, multiplanar imaging, and sensitivity to fluid shifts and chronic blood products.

CT Principles and Techniques

The invention of CT was revolutionary, earning Hounsfield and Cormack the Nobel Prize in 1979. Modern CT uses X-ray attenuation differences, expressed in Hounsfield Units (HU), to differentiate tissues. Air measures around -1000 HU, water is 0 HU, and bone and metal appear very bright due to high HU values.

The diagnostic power of CT lies in its contrast resolution, enhanced by adjusting window settings tailored for bone, soft tissue, or brain structures. In non-contrast CT—the standard for acute TBI—acute blood is hyperdense and thus easily identifiable. Intravenous contrast is only used when vascular pathology is suspected, as it may obscure acute hemorrhage.

The only absolute contraindication to a non-contrast CT is an unstable patient, though pregnant women and children require special consideration due to radiation exposure.

Mnemonic: "ABCs of CT" — Air = -1000 HU, Blood = Bright, Contrast = Considered if vascular injury suspected.

MRI Principles and Use in TBI

MRI leverages the behavior of hydrogen protons in tissues when exposed to strong magnetic fields. Upon disruption by a radiofrequency pulse, protons enter a high-energy state. Their relaxation to equilibrium is quantified by T1 and T2 relaxation times. These properties enable detailed imaging of brain anatomy and fluid dynamics.

Water appears dark on T1 and bright on T2, while fat is generally bright on both. Gradient fields help localize the proton signals, producing high-resolution multiplanar images. Gadolinium, a non-iodinated contrast agent, is used to assess vascularity or blood-brain barrier breakdown, especially in subacute or chronic phases.

Imaging Pathology in Acute TBI

The adult skull is a fixed compartment, so even small lesions can cause dangerous mass effect. Initial imaging starts with non-contrast CT focusing on midline shift, ventricles, cisterns, herniation, and extra- or intra-axial lesions.

Pneumocephalus indicates a communication with air, usually due to fracture. Brain structures are classified as intra-axial (within the brain) or extra-axial (e.g., meninges and CSF spaces). Understanding this distinction helps localize pathology.

Intra-Axial Injuries: Contusions, DAI, Herniation, Edema

Brain contusions occur when blunt or penetrating trauma damages brain tissue. These may be hemorrhagic, appearing bright on CT, or non-hemorrhagic, appearing dark due to edema. The frontal, temporal, and occipital lobes are often involved, due to their proximity to bony ridges.

Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI) results from rotational or acceleration/deceleration forces. Damage often occurs at the gray-white junction, corpus callosum, and brainstem. CT may miss these subtle injuries, while MRI—especially with FLAIR or GRE sequences—can reveal hemorrhagic foci.

A normal CT scan in a comatose patient strongly suggests DAI, which is associated with poor prognosis.

Herniation syndromes reflect dangerous shifts in brain compartments due to increased pressure. Types include subfalcine, uncal, tonsillar, transsphenoidal, and external herniation. The most common is subfalcine, causing displacement under the falx cerebri.

Cerebral edema leads to raised intracranial pressure (ICP). Cytotoxic edema (irreversible) results from cellular injury and is seen as restricted diffusion on DWI MRI. Vasogenic edema (reversible) arises from increased extracellular fluid, often around tumors, and appears as fingerlike low-density areas on imaging.

Mnemonic: "SHUT CT" — Types of Herniation: Subfalcine, Herniation (Uncal), Uncus, Tonsillar, Central, Transsphenoidal.

Extra-Axial Hemorrhages and Hydrocephalus

Subdural Hematomas (SDH) result from tearing of bridging veins. On CT, acute SDH appears hyperdense and crescent-shaped. Over time, it becomes isodense and later hypodense. Early diagnosis is vital: mortality is reduced if treated within 4 hours.

Subdural Hygromas are CSF collections in the subdural space due to arachnoid tears, and resemble chronic SDH on CT. MRI can differentiate based on CSF signal characteristics.

Epidural Hematomas (EDH) are biconvex (lens-shaped) and occur between the dura and skull, often from middle meningeal artery rupture. Unlike SDH, EDHs don’t cross sutures but may cross midline.

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) appears as hyperdensity in sulci/cisterns. Most SAHs are aneurysmal, but trauma can also cause it. MRI (FLAIR) detects chronic SAH.

Intraventricular Hemorrhage (IVH) may arise from SAH extension or primary subependymal rupture. It risks hydrocephalus. Hydrocephalus itself can be communicating (due to poor CSF resorption) or non-communicating (obstruction). Hydrocephalus ex vacuo, in contrast, reflects atrophy without raised pressure.

Prognostic Imaging in TBI

CT provides crucial triage data: signs such as midline shift, ventricle compression, and cistern effacement predict increased ICP. The National Traumatic Coma Data Bank found that cistern effacement and shift are powerful mortality predictors.

A normal CT in a comatose patient suggests DAI, emphasizing that imaging and clinical exam must be interpreted together. The location of injury also matters: brainstem lesions imply worse functional outcomes.

MRI helps assess long-term sequelae and guides rehabilitation, while delayed complications, such as delayed hemorrhage, warrant follow-up imaging. Post-traumatic seizures (PTS) and epilepsy (PTE) are more common after contusions, DAI, or hematomas.

Chapter 12: Functional Neuroimaging of TBI

Overview and Paradigms

Functional neuroimaging assesses brain activity and physiology, not just anatomy. It complements exams and neuropsychological testing but remains largely investigational in TBI.

Resting paradigms measure brain activity in baseline conditions—used in SPECT and PET. Activation paradigms require a task or stimulus, helpful in identifying regions involved in cognition or motor control.

SPECT: Single Photon Emission CT

SPECT tracks regional cerebral blood flow (rCBF) by detecting tracer uptake. It is cheaper and more available than PET but has lower resolution and slower response to rapid changes.

In TBI, SPECT shows reduced rCBF in frontal regions, even when CT or MRI is normal. However, findings may lack specificity, and it is not approved for routine TBI assessment.

Question 10

Which finding is common in TBI on SPECT?

  • Decreased blood flow in the prefrontal cortex
  • Increased metabolism in occipital cortex
  • Normal perfusion with structural damage

PET: Positron Emission Tomography

PET provides high-resolution maps of metabolism or blood flow. It uses radioactive tracers like FDG and often requires a cyclotron.

In acute TBI, PET shows hyperglycolysis and regional hypoperfusion. Despite detailed imaging, few studies link PET findings to cognition due to logistical challenges and poor longitudinal data.

fMRI: Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging

fMRI is based on the BOLD (Blood-Oxygen-Level Dependent) effect. Neural activity increases local oxyhemoglobin, enhancing the signal. It avoids radiation and offers superior anatomic resolution compared to SPECT or PET.

In TBI, fMRI reveals atypical activation patterns—e.g., increased bilateral recruitment in working memory tasks. This suggests patients require more effort to achieve the same cognitive output.

Chapter 13: Electrophysiologic Testing in TBI

Introduction

Evoked potentials and electroencephalography (EEG) offer objective insight into brain function, independent of behavior or cooperation. They are especially useful for early diagnosis and prognosis.

Somatosensory and Brainstem Auditory Evoked Potentials (SEP & BAEP)

SEP evaluates brain responses to sensory stimuli. Bilaterally absent cortical responses within 8 days in severe TBI (GCS ≤ 8) strongly predict poor outcomes.

BAEP uses auditory clicks to assess brainstem function. Normal BAEPs in the first week predict awakening, but not necessarily cognitive recovery.

Question 12

  • High likelihood of death or vegetative state

EEG and Event-Related Potentials

Conventional EEG identifies general brain dysfunction. Patterns like non-reactive delta or burst suppression suggest poor prognosis. In contrast, quantitative EEG (QEEG) offers better sensitivity in mild TBI, using frequency analysis and topographic mapping.

P300 is an event-related potential that reflects attention and working memory. A preserved P300 in coma is highly predictive of recovery, while its absence does not guarantee poor outcomes.

P50 indicates sensory gating, often disrupted in TBI. P250, a pain-related response, can help assess responsiveness in vegetative states.

Conclusion: Combining Imaging and Electrophysiology

SEP and P300 together enhance prognostication. Functional neuroimaging and electrophysiology, when integrated, offer a comprehensive, objective view of injury severity, recovery potential, and cognitive function after TBI. Future tools must unify structural and functional data for improved patient care.
