Chapter 14: Prognosis and Outcome After Severe Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

The Ethical Duty and Clinical Challenge of Prognostication

Clinicians caring for patients with severe TBI carry a heavy responsibility: to provide accurate, compassionate, and evidence-based prognostic information to families. This information is not a luxury. It is one of the most important needs families express following severe brain injury. Delivering prognostic insight offers families a form of cognitive control, reinforcing their trust in the medical team and reducing anxiety.

In fact, many experts argue that prognostication is an ethical obligation, not merely a clinical option. Families need guidance to make informed decisions about care, rehabilitation, and expectations. Yet, physicians often avoid this task, not out of malice, but due to a combination of fears: fear of destroying hope, lack of training in delivering bad news, discomfort with probabilistic language, and an over-reliance on personal clinical experience.

From Experience to Evidence: Creating Clinical Thresholds

This chapter takes a major step forward by moving beyond anecdote and tradition. Instead, it presents an evidence-based, threshold-driven approach to TBI prognosis. Rather than vague estimations, clinicians can now use hard data to communicate likely outcomes based on specific predictor variables.

The analysis draws from studies of adults with moderate to severe TBI. Predictor variables include the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS), length of coma, post-traumatic amnesia (PTA), age, and neuroimaging findings. The most valuable outcome measures were those evaluated at least six months post-injury: the Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS), return to work, and ability to live independently.

The innovative contribution of this chapter is the introduction of "threshold values". These are cut-off points in predictor variables that dramatically alter the probability of a certain outcome. For example, when PTA exceeds 3 months, the chances of a good recovery become very unlikely. Conversely, if PTA is under 2 months, the likelihood of severe disability is minimal.

Mnemonic: "3-Month PTA = Poor Prognosis" — Think of PTA as a recovery clock. If it’s still ticking past 3 months, the prognosis likely stalls.

Predictive Power of Variables

Let us now examine the predictive utility of each variable discussed in this chapter. While no single factor is absolute, some are more powerful than others, particularly when used together.

The initial GCS score is only moderately predictive. Lower scores correlate with worse outcomes, but no clear cut-off exists that strongly predicts one outcome over another. By contrast, length of coma is more telling: when coma exceeds a month, the chance of a good recovery is slim; if it's under two weeks, the risk of severe disability drops significantly.

PTA duration is among the most reliable indicators. Less than two months means severe disability is unlikely. More than three months means good recovery is unlikely. This variable is especially robust because it captures both cognitive and consciousness recovery.

With age, older patients do worse. A good recovery is unlikely if the patient is over 65. Neuroimaging contributes valuable context: early MRI with bilateral brainstem lesions predicts poor outcomes, and CT findings like subarachnoid hemorrhage, cisternal effacement, and midline shift are associated with worse outcomes, although not determinative alone.

Chapter 15: Neuroimaging Correlates of Functional Outcome

Complexity of Predicting Functional Recovery

Understanding how neuroimaging predicts function after TBI is challenging. Unlike motor or sensory deficits—where a small targeted lesion can reliably predict outcome—complex functions like memory and language are handled by widely distributed networks. A lesion seen on imaging does not always correspond to real-world impairment due to functional reorganization and individual brain variability. Neuroimaging shows structure, but not necessarily function.

Question 8

Diffuse Brain Injury and Ventricular Enlargement

TBI often causes widespread microstructural damage that leads to macro changes: enlarged ventricles, thinning of the corpus callosum, and wider cortical sulci. The ventricle‑to‑brain ratio (VBR) quantifies generalized atrophy and correlates with poorer cognitive and behavioural outcomes. Even a quick visual check of enlarged ventricles can be informative.

Mnemonic: “BVS—Big Ventricles, Sad outcomes” to associate ventricular enlargement with worse prognosis.

Limitations of Imaging Modalities

Different MRI sequences (e.g., T1, T2, FLAIR, GRE) highlight different pathologies, but none show microstructure directly. This means the apparent damage may underestimate the real functional impact. Future progress depends on combining structural and functional imaging to better predict recovery outcomes.

Regional Vulnerability Patterns

The frontal and temporal lobes are especially vulnerable to TBI due to their anatomical relationship with cranial bones. Consequently, deficits in memory and executive functions are most prevalent. Structures like the hippocampus, fornix, and mammillary body—central to memory processing—are often damaged, contributing to lasting cognitive impairment.

Day‑of‑Injury (DOI) CT and Outcome Prediction

The initial CT scan is pivotal. The Marshall classification helps categorize abnormalities. While many CT findings alone aren’t strongly predictive, some are significant: brainstem lesions on DOI CT are associated with the worst functional prognosis. Combined imaging findings and PTA duration provide a better predictive model.

Visual Rating by Clinicians

Clinicians can use simple visual assessments: frontal and temporal atrophy predict memory/executive deficits; white matter hyperintensities (WMHs) correlate with cognitive and mood disorders; enlarged ventricles forecast poorer outcomes; coup–contre‑coup injuries often indicate diffuse damage.

Looking Ahead: Integration of Imaging Modalities

The future of prediction lies in combining structural imaging with functional measures—like PET, fMRI, or MRS—to bridge the gap between what is seen and what is actually happening in the brain. This layered approach aims to yield more accurate, personalized prognoses.

Chapter 16: Functional Assessment in TBI Rehabilitation

Defining Functional Assessment (FA)

Functional assessment measures how physical, cognitive, or emotional impairments affect daily life. It involves two core components: quantification—reporting a numerical value for function—and valuation—understanding the individual's personal perception of that function's meaning and importance.

Question 14

What does ‘valuation’ refer to in functional assessment?

  • A numeric score only
  • The patient’s subjective importance of a function
  • A clinician’s observation alone

Disablement Continuum: ICF Framework

The WHO’s ICF model guides FA. It consists of:

  1. Impairment—body structure/function issues (e.g., GCS).
  2. Activity limitation—difficulty performing tasks (e.g., FIM).
  3. Participation restriction—difficulty engaging in life roles (e.g., CIQ).

This framework clarifies that FA must target the correct level—impairment, activity, or participation—based on the purpose of assessment.

Measuring Instruments and Their Limits

Functional assessment tools use ordinal scales, which may not allow for true mathematical operations like percentage changes. While Rasch analysis can convert ordinal to interval scales, many common tools lack this transformation. It’s important to recognize that adding or comparing scores directly can be misleading.

Capacity vs. Performance & Data Collection Methods

FA distinguishes between capacity (what someone can do under ideal conditions) and performance (what they actually do in daily life). Methods include testing, clinician observation, and self- or proxy-report. Valid assessment considers context—environment and motivation significantly influence outcomes.

Question 17

  • Home behavior observation
  • Proxy questionnaire

Psychometric Properties of FA Instruments

To be useful for individuals, FA instruments need high reliability (≥0.90) and validity. Validity types include content, predictive, known-group, and construct validity, ensuring the instrument measures what it should. Instruments must also avoid floor/ceiling effects and maintain ecological validity—applicability to real-life situations.

Uses and Patient Perspective

FA supports:

  1. Individual care: guiding admission, goal-setting (e.g., COPM, GAS), tracking progress.
  2. Program use: evaluation, benchmarking, accreditation, reimbursement reporting.
  3. Research: assessing treatment impact and describing recovery trajectories.

Crucially, participation measures align best with life satisfaction. Instruments like GAS, COPM, and POPS integrate patient values, bridging the gap between clinician and patient perspectives.

Common FA Instruments Overview

The chapter reviews many tools:

  1. Impairment: GCS.
  2. Activity: Barthel Index, FIM (with FAM additions).
  3. Participation: CHART, CIQ, POPS, PM‑PAC.
  4. Cross‑cutting: LCFS/Rancho, DRS, MPAI, GOS‐E.

A useful online resource is COMBI, which catalogues TBI-specific instruments and their psychometric profiles.

Chapter 17: Life Expectancy After Traumatic Brain Injury

Understanding Life Expectancy Terminology

Life expectancy reflects the average survival time for a group, not for an individual. Important distinctions include:

  1. Survival time—how long someone lives after injury.
  2. Median survival—the time at which half the group has died.
  3. Standardized mortality ratio (SMR)—comparison of death rates to expected norms.

Clinicians need these definitions to interpret data correctly and avoid overgeneralizing from group averages to individual patients.

Causes of Death: Short‑ and Long‑Term

In the short term, death is mainly due to the brain injury itself, followed by complications like pneumonia. Long-term, mortality from epilepsy, suicide, respiratory infections, and circulatory disease is elevated. For example, post‑traumatic epilepsy carries an SMR of ~24, and pneumonia deaths are 10× higher in immobile individuals.

Impact of Disability on Life Expectancy

Life expectancy after TBI depends heavily on injury severity and disability level. For instance:

  1. Severely disabled children/adolescents can lose ~40 years of life expectancy.
  2. An average reduction of ~7 years was found among TBI Model System participants.
  3. Those with extreme disability may have life expectancy reduced by half.

Here, the degree of functional ability is the strongest determinant of lifespan.

Key Predictors of Long‑Term Survival

Strong predictors include:

  1. Mobility—nonambulatory individuals have ~4× higher mortality.
  2. Feeding ability—lack of self-feeding predicts worse survival.
  3. Age, sex, epilepsy, maladaptive behaviors also affect risk, though motor ability remains the biggest driver.

Trajectory Over Time and Care Quality

Mortality risk declines after the first two years but then plateaus—the "healthy survivor" effect. Despite this, some decline functionally faster than uninjured peers.

Quality of care matters—but cannot offset the impact of severe disability. Even with excellent care, life expectancy remains significantly reduced in profoundly disabled individuals.

Individual Prognosis vs. Group Statistics

It’s critical to distinguish between individual survival and group-based life expectancy. While group data suggest significant reductions, individual outcomes vary unpredictably. Using mortality tables adjusted for mobility, feeding status, and other factors allows more reasoned estimates than intuition alone.
