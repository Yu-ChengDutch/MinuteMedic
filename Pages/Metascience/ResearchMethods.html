Experimental design

These studies are also referred to as interventional studies, as opposed to observational studies.

In Evidence Based Medicine (EMB) the most important way to garner new information is not experience, but clinical studies. These are divided into two types of studies:

  1. Treatment studies
  2. Observational studies

Treatment studies

Randomised controlled trial (RCT)

This design uses blinding and randomisation to control factors not under direct experimental control. To control for bias, blinding is used. There exist three types of blinding:

  1. Single blinding: Participants are blinded
  2. Double blinding: Both the participants and the researchers are blinded
  3. Triple blinding: Participants, researchers and analysts are blinded

Adaptive design

In this design, the methodology of the study may be changed based on an interim study.

Nonrandomised trial

In a nonrandomised trial or a quasi-experiment, the experiment of an intervention on a population is studied without any randomisation.

Observational studies

Descriptive studies

These studies describe the situation in a certain population at a certain time, but they don't look at a certain outcome or predictive factor. Based on size, they may be divided in:

  1. Case report
  2. Case series
  3. Population study

Longitudinal studies

These studies take specific population and follow them through time instead of just describing them at a moment in time:

  1. Cohort study
  2. Case-control study
  3. Cross-sectional study

Cohort study

This form of analytical study is a longitudinal study follows a specific cohort which shares a defining characteristics and performs a cross-section at intervals. The repetition in time, however, may take place at different moments:

  1. Prospective cohort study: take a group sharing a characteristic, follow over time and look at outcome
  2. Retrospective cohort study: take a group sharing an outcome, look at a shared history and determine an effect

Case-control study

In this kind of study, two groups with a different outcome are taken and they are compared on the basis of some supposed causal attribute. They are used to produce an odds ratio.

Cross-sectional study

In this kind of study, the cross section of a population is studied in the same manner as in a case-control study.

Other studies

Meta-analysis

These studies combine the results of multiple studies all addressing a similar research question. The data are pooled, and recalculated to determine a combined effect size.

Systematic review

These studies review all studies on a certain topics by taking them at face value (i.e., not collecting the data and recalculating but rather just reading the text).

Bias and confounding

Confounding

In terms of variables, we separate two kind of variables:

  1. Independant variables: The predictor that is supposed to influence the outcome
  2. Dependant variables: The target or outcome variable that we're trying to study

Then, what are confounders? Here too, we see two kinds of variables that influence the outcome (i.e., dependant) variable that are not the independant variables. These are:

  1. Mediators: these are intermediate variables between the exposure and outcome. In other words, the independant variable only influences the dependant variable through the mediator variable
  2. Confounders: these variables influences both the dependant and independant variables without being influenced itself by either

The danger of confounders is that they may cause a spurious relation between the dependant and independant variables. This is a relation where there is another explanation for the observed relation, that is not a direct causal effect of the independant variable on the dependant variable.

As one might imagine, such a spurious relation makes finding the causes relation between the independant and dependant variables challenging. This is a threat to internal validity: namely, making it challenging to find out a causal relationship. External validity on the other hand, tells us about the generalisability of the found effect.

Outcomes and effect sizes

Odds ratio

This statistic shows the likelyhood of A occuring in the presence of B and vice versa. An Odds Ratio (OR) of 1 means that there is no relation, while an OR higher than 1 means a positive correlation.

Relative risk

The Relative Risk (RR) or risk ratio is the probability of an outcome in an exposed group vs an unexposed group.

Risk difference

The Risk Difference (RD), also known as excess risk or attributable risk is the difference of risk of an outcome in the exposed group vs the unexposed group. In practice, this is calculated as the incidence in exposed minus incidence in unexposed.

Errors

Observational error

These errors entail a difference between a measured quanitity and an unknown true value. These exist in two kinds:

  1. Random errors
  2. Systematic errors

Random errors

These errors are indeed random, and are basically what you imagine with broken equipment: the relation between the measurement and the actual value approaches zero. They are described in terms of accuracy, namely how close a give set of observations are to their true value.

Systematic

These errors introduce a relation that isn't there. Imagine, for example, if a ruler is too short: it will never measure the true value. They are described in terms of precision, which means how close the observations are to each other.

Sensitivity and specificity

Sensitivity

Sensitivity or true positive rate helps ruling out a disease if a test is negative. It is the true positives divided by all the true positives and false negatives.

Specificity

Specificity or true negative rate is the proportion of people who test negative among all those who actually do not have that disease. It helps rule a disease in when positive. It is calculated as the true negatives divided by true negatives + false positives.

Positive and negative predictive values

Positive predictive value

The Positive Predictive Value (PPV) is simply the probability of a positive test result given one truly has that disease. It is calculated by the true positive divided by all positives.

Negative predictive value

The Negative Predictive Value (NPV) is the probability of a negative test result in case one does not have a certain disease. It is calculated by the true negative values divided by all negative values.
