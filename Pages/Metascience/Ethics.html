What is ethics?

On Meta-ethics

Meta-ethics determines which statements are moral, i.e., have to do with ethics, and amoral, i.e., do not have to do with ethics. Now, confusingly, moral also has a different meaning: namely, in contrast to immoral, i.e., that which is unjust. The branch of philosophy which concerns itself with this just-unjust distinction is normative ethics.

Normative ethics then, concerns itself with norms and values. Norms in this sense, are negatively formulated, concrete principles. Values on the other hand, are positively formulated and unatainable.

On normative ethics

There are two main theories on what moral behaviour ought to be: teleological and deontological. Here, teleology determines moral behaviour based on it's consequences. Compare the root teleo- and tele-, as in television, to remember that teleo- just means some consequence that is far off. This ultimate goal may be pluralistic, as Plato did, or some monistic objective like pleasure (Epicurius), happiness (Locke) or utility (Bentham). Deontological thought, on the other hand, supposes that an act is moral if and only if it's done according to and because of a certain moral obligation ('pflicht'). The intention to act accordingly is the most important thing.
