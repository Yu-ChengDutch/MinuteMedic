Introduction to Viognier

Viognier is a white wine grape variety known for its aromatic intensity, lush texture, and complex flavor profile. Once nearly extinct, it has experienced a resurgence in popularity due to its unique characteristics and versatility in both single-varietal wines and blends.

History of Viognier

Ancient History

The origins of Viognier are shrouded in mystery, but it is believed to have been brought to the Rhône region by the Romans around 281 AD. Some theories suggest it may have originated in Dalmatia (modern-day Croatia), though genetic studies remain inconclusive.

Pre-Modern History (Medieval & Renaissance)

During the Middle Ages, Viognier was cultivated in the Condrieu and Château-Grillet appellations of the Northern Rhône. Its production was limited due to its low yields and susceptibility to disease. By the Renaissance, it was considered a noble grape, prized for its aromatic qualities.

Modern History

By the 20th century, Viognier was nearly extinct, with only about 30 acres remaining in Condrieu by the 1960s. Its revival began in the 1980s when New World winemakers (particularly in California and Australia) recognized its potential. Today, it is planted globally, from France to South Africa.

Mnemonic: "VIP Viognier" – Viognier was nearly Vanished, then Innovated by Producers in the New World.

Production of Viognier

Agriculture: Historical vs. Modern

Historically, Viognier was grown on steep, granite slopes in the Rhône, requiring manual labor due to its low vigor and susceptibility to powdery mildew. Modern viticulture employs careful canopy management and selective clonal breeding to improve yield and disease resistance.

Winemaking Process

Viognier is typically whole-cluster pressed to avoid bitterness from skins. Fermentation occurs in stainless steel (for freshness) or oak (for richness). Some producers use wild yeast for complexity. Malolactic fermentation is often avoided to preserve acidity.

Ageing

Viognier can be aged in neutral oak, concrete eggs, or stainless steel. Extended lees contact adds creaminess. Unlike Chardonnay, it rarely benefits from long oak ageing, as its delicate aromas can be overwhelmed.

Mnemonic: "Fresh or Fat Viognier" – Stainless steel keeps it fresh, while oak makes it fat (richer).

Growth and Terroir of Viognier

Key Regions

France (Rhône): Condrieu and Château-Grillet are the classic appellations, producing intensely floral wines. Languedoc makes more affordable, fruit-driven styles. New World: California (Central Coast), Australia (Yarra Valley), and South Africa (Stellenbosch) excel with riper expressions.

Terroir Influence

Granite soils (Condrieu) produce wines with mineral precision, while clay-limestone (Languedoc) yields rounder textures. Warmer climates (California) emphasize tropical fruit, whereas cooler sites (Virginia) retain acidity and floral notes.

Mnemonic: "Viognier's ABCs" – Aromatics, Balance, Complexity define great terroir.

Taste and Flavor Profile

Chemical Composition

Viognier’s signature aromas come from terpenes (also found in Muscat) and C13-norisoprenoids, which create floral (violets, orange blossom) and stone fruit (apricot, peach) notes. Low acidity (pH ~3.6) contributes to its plush mouthfeel.

Wine Descriptions

Young Viognier: Explosive aromas of jasmine, honeysuckle, and ripe pear. Aged Viognier: Develops honey, ginger, and toasted almond nuances. Overripe examples can show sherried notes.

Food Pairings

Viognier’s richness pairs well with aromatic spices (turmeric, saffron) and fatty textures. Classic matches include:

  1. Lobster with vanilla beurre blanc – The wine’s viscosity complements butter.
  2. Thai green curry
  3. Foie gras – Balances richness with acidity.

Avoid overly acidic dishes, which clash with Viognier’s low acidity.

Sales and Branding

Viognier’s market positioning hinges on its exoticism and approachability. Key strategies:

  1. Luxury branding (e.g., Condrieu bottles priced over $100).
  2. Blending (e.g., with Syrah in Côte-Rôtie for aromatic lift).
  3. New World "gateway" wines – Affordable, fruit-forward styles attract Chardonnay drinkers.

Label design often features floral motifs to emphasize aroma.
