History of Riesling

Ancient History

Riesling is one of the oldest cultivated grape varieties, with its origins tracing back to the Rhine region in modern-day Germany. While there is no definitive proof of Riesling in antiquity, some historians speculate that the Roman Empire may have cultivated early ancestors of the grape. The first written mention of "Rüssling" appears in a 1435 document from the Count of Katzenelnbogen, a key figure in medieval viticulture.

Pre-Modern History (Medieval & Renaissance)

During the Middle Ages, monasteries played a crucial role in preserving and refining Riesling cultivation. The Cistercian monks of the Rhine and Mosel regions were particularly influential, perfecting vineyard sites that are still renowned today. By the Renaissance, Riesling had gained prominence in Germany, with plantings expanding along steep riverbanks. The grape was prized for its ability to reflect terroir and its resistance to cold climates.

Modern History

The 19th century marked Riesling's golden age, particularly in Germany, where it was considered superior to even Bordeaux and Burgundy wines. The Prussian classification system of 1868 formally recognized top Riesling vineyards, many of which remain prestigious today. However, the 20th century saw a decline due to world wars and industrial winemaking trends. A resurgence began in the 1980s, as dry Riesling styles gained international acclaim.

Mnemonic: "3 R’s of Riesling History" – Rhine (origin), Renaissance (monastic refinement), Resurgence (modern revival).

Production of Riesling

Agriculture: Historical vs. Modern

Historically, Riesling was grown on steep, slate-rich slopes to maximize sun exposure in cooler climates. Vineyards were often terraced by hand, a labor-intensive process. Today, while some traditional methods persist, modern viticulture employs precision farming, including soil sensors and drone monitoring, to optimize ripeness and acidity balance.

Winemaking Techniques

Riesling is typically cold-fermented in stainless steel or neutral oak to preserve its delicate aromatics. Some producers use wild yeast for complexity. The grape’s high natural acidity allows for versatile styles: dry (Trocken), off-dry (Halbtrocken), sweet (Spätlese, Auslese), and botrytized (Beerenauslese, Trockenbeerenauslese).

Ageing Potential

High-quality Riesling can age for decades, developing petrol-like notes (from TDN compound) and honeyed complexity. Sweet Rieslings, in particular, gain depth over time due to their sugar and acid structure.

Mnemonic: "STARS" for Riesling Ageing – Slate soils, Time-resistant, Acid-driven, Residual sugar, Sweet styles last longest.

Growth and Terroir

Key Regions

Germany (Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz) remains the benchmark, with Mosel’s slate soils producing razor-sharp acidity and floral notes. Alsace, France yields fuller-bodied, dry Rieslings with higher alcohol. Austria’s Wachau region is known for mineral-driven, steely styles. New World regions like Australia’s Clare Valley (lime/zest) and New York’s Finger Lakes (high acid, lean profile) have also gained acclaim.

Terroir Influence

Slate imparts flinty minerality, limestone adds texture, and sandstone yields softer wines. Cool climates emphasize green apple/citrus, while warmer sites show peach/apricot.

Taste and Flavor

Chemical Composition

Riesling’s high tartaric acid gives it backbone, while terpenes (like linalool) create floral aromas. TDN emerges with age, adding petrol notes.

Flavor Profiles

Young Riesling: Lime, green apple, jasmine. Aged Riesling: Honey, petrol, dried apricot. Sweet Riesling: Candied citrus, tropical fruit.

Mnemonic: "Riesling’s Aromas in Stages" (LAS) – Lively (young), Advanced (aged), Sumptuous (sweet).

Food Pairings

Dry Riesling: Pair with spicy Asian cuisine (Thai, Szechuan) or pork schnitzel. Off-dry: Balances Indian curries or blue cheese. Sweet Riesling: Perfect with foie gras or fruit desserts.
