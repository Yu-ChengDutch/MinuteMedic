Welcome to the system of temperature regulation!

Mediated through the hypothalamus, the system of temperature regulation is essential for bodily functions.

Temperature regulation

One of the roles of the hypothalamus is to regulate body temperature. The area of the hypothalamus most involed therein is the preoptic area or POA as seen in the picture below. The POA might be thought of as the body's thermostat and contains the central thermoreceptors monitoring the body's core temperature and comparing it to the average of 37.3 C.

Input to the system

While the POA forms the central control unit, it needs input. For that, it uses the peripheral thermoreceptors which are located in the skin. While the thermorceptors connect to both A-delta fibers and C-fibers, it is mainly the A-delta fibers that are responsible for cold and sharp pain, while the C-fibers are responsible for warmth and dull pain or prolonged sensations. They then enter the spinal cord through the dorsal root ganglia, synapse in the dorsal horn and follow the spinothalamic tract with temperature sensation and pain mostly located in the lateral spinothalamic tract. Here, it arrives to the thalamus where it synapses and information is relayed to both the somatosensory cortex and hypothalamus.

Pyrogens

Now, besides the "normal" input of the thermoreceptors, there are so-called pyrogens which are able to hyperactivate the hypothalamus. These are classes as exogenous pyrogens and endogenous pyrogens.

Exogenous pyrogens are those pyrogens coming from outside the body, typically produced by bacteria or viruses.

Endogenous pyrogens are those pyrogens produced by the body, possibly in response to pathogens, such as IL-1, TNF-alpha and IFN-gamma.

Pyrogenic stimulation of the hypothalamus

Through the direct pathway pyrogens cross into the brain at the Organum Vasculosum of the Lamina Terminalis or OVLT, a part of the hypothalamus without blood brain-barrier.

Following the indirect pathway, pyrogens stimulate the blood-brain barrier to produce prostaglandins particularly prostaglandin E2 which then stimulates the POA.

Output of the system

Now that the set-point has been ... set, the body needs a way to regulate body temperature, for this it uses autonomic pathways, endocrine pathways and behavioural responses

Autonomic pathways

In response to cold, the body activates the sympathatic nervous system. This then causes vasoconstriction, shivering thermogenesis, and non-shivering thermogenesis such as activation of brown adipose tissue and metabolic changes.

In response to heat, the body activates the parasympathetic nervous system which causes vasodilation and sweating.

The parasympathetic system is to party / heat while the sympathetic system is the opposite

Endocrine pathways

In response to cold, the hypothalamus increases the release of TRH leading to an increased production of thyroid hormone. Similarly, an increase in CRH stimulates the adrenal glands to release more cortisol which in turn influences metabolism and energy production.

Behavioural responses

As we become aware of a change in the difference between the set-point and our current temperature, whether it be due to an environmental change or a set-point change, we seek out warmth and change our clothing.

Disorders of temperature regulation

There is two principle categories of an increased body temperature, namely fever or pyrexia and hypothermia. While there are multiple definitions of both, here we take fever to mean those increases in body temeprature caused by an increased hypothalamic set-point, while hypothermia is taken to mean any increase in body temperature not caused by a change in set-point.

Within fever, one may classify the severity:

  1. Low-grade: 37.3 C to 38.0 C
  2. Moderate-grade: 38.1 C to 39.0 C
  3. High-grade: 39.1 C to 41 C

The aetiology then, of a fever, can be categorised as the following:

  1. Infectious
  2. Neoplastic
  3. Inflammatory (incl. rheumatic, non-rheumatic, and drug-related)

Other origins of fever may include fever after brain injury where a hypothalamic laesion may cause thermic dysregulation; and endocrine fever where an increase in thyroid hormones increase the set-point.

Based on duration, fevers can be broadly categorised in acute (less than 4 days) or non-acute (more than 4 days). An acute fever in adults is highly likely to be infectious.

Consequences of fever and hypothermia

Metabolic effects

With a febrile state we see a 10% raise in metaolic rate per 1 C increase in body temperature, naturally this demands an increase oxygen demand, increased heart rate and increased respiratory rate. The type of metabolism changes too, as protein and fat metabolism is increased and glucose metabolism is decreased. At the same time immune function is enhanced and normal growth of pathogens is disrupted.

Other effects

At the same time, we see acute neurologic and cognitive dysfunction, especially of cerebellar origin in extreme fevers and heatstroke. At the same time we see hypotension, high cardiac output with an EEG showing T-wave abnormalities, QT and ST changes and conduction defects. Furthermore, in extreme fever we might see increased liver enzymes. Lastly, with increased temperature we see decreased GFR and decreased clotting ability.
