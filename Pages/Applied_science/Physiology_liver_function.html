Introduction to Liver Function Tests

Anatomy and Physiology of the Liver

The liver is a vital organ located in the right upper quadrant of the abdomen, just beneath the diaphragm. It is divided into two major lobes and is primarily composed of hepatocytes. These cells perform a variety of critical functions including metabolism, detoxification, protein synthesis, and bile production.

The liver receives dual blood supply: oxygen-rich blood from the hepatic artery and nutrient-rich blood from the portal vein. This unique vascularization supports its extensive metabolic activity. Blood from the liver drains into the inferior vena cava via the hepatic veins.

Functions of the Liver Relevant to Testing

The liver’s biochemical activities can be grouped into the following:

  1. Metabolic regulation (glucose, lipid, and protein metabolism)
  2. Excretory function (bilirubin and bile acids)
  3. Synthetic function (albumin, clotting factors)
  4. Detoxification (ammonia, drugs, toxins)

Derangements in these functions are reflected in a set of blood tests collectively known as liver function tests (LFTs). These tests do not all measure the same aspect of liver function, and some are better thought of as markers of liver injury rather than function per se.

Mnemonic: Think of the liver as a MAPS center: Metabolism, Albumin synthesis, Protein processing, and Secretion.

Alanine Aminotransferase (ALAT/ALT)

Physiology

ALT is an intracellular enzyme found predominantly in hepatocytes. Its physiological role is in amino acid metabolism, specifically catalyzing the reversible transfer of an amino group from alanine to α-ketoglutarate to form pyruvate and glutamate.

Pathophysiology

In hepatocellular injury, ALT leaks into the bloodstream. Because ALT is more liver-specific than AST, it is a sensitive marker for liver cell injury. Elevations in ALT are seen in viral hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, ischemic hepatitis, and autoimmune hepatitis. It is usually higher than AST in non-alcoholic causes of hepatocellular injury.

Clinical Use

ALT is used to detect hepatocellular injury. In chronic liver disease, ALT levels may normalize despite ongoing fibrosis, especially in cirrhosis, due to a reduction in viable hepatocyte mass.

Mnemonic: ALT = "A Liver Target" — Think of ALT as the enzyme that directly signals hepatocyte injury.

ASAT (Aspartate Aminotransferase)

Physiology and Role

ASAT, also known as AST, is an enzyme found in a variety of tissues, including the liver, cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, and kidneys. Like ALAT, ASAT catalyzes the transfer of an amino group from aspartate to alpha-ketoglutarate, producing oxaloacetate and glutamate.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Because ASAT is less liver-specific than ALAT, elevated levels must be interpreted carefully. While liver injury (e.g., hepatitis, cirrhosis) can cause ASAT elevation, so can conditions such as myocardial infarction, muscle trauma, and hemolysis. In liver disease, a particularly high ASAT/ALAT ratio (>2) is often associated with alcoholic hepatitis.

Diagnostic Use

ASAT is best interpreted in the context of other enzymes. For example:

  1. High ASAT + High ALAT: Acute hepatocellular injury
  2. High ASAT/ALAT ratio: Consider alcoholic hepatitis or cirrhosis
  3. High ASAT + normal ALAT: Suggests muscle or cardiac origin

Mnemonic: "S = Systemic"

Remember that ASAT is found in more systemic tissues than ALAT—this helps you recall that its elevation may reflect extrahepatic damage.

AF (Alkaline Phosphatase)

Physiology and Role

Alkaline Phosphatase (AF) is an enzyme found in the membranes of many tissues, particularly in the liver (bile canaliculi), bone, placenta, and intestines. It functions best at an alkaline pH and plays a key role in dephosphorylation of various molecules.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

In the liver, AF is particularly associated with the biliary epithelium. Hence, an elevation in AF is a hallmark of cholestasis, both intrahepatic and extrahepatic. Elevation can also occur with biliary obstruction (e.g., gallstones, tumors), primary sclerosing cholangitis, or primary biliary cholangitis.

Extrahepatic causes of elevated AF include bone disease (e.g., Paget’s disease, osteoblastic metastases), pregnancy, and certain malignancies. Therefore, interpretation must be done in context, often using GGT to confirm hepatic origin.

Diagnostic Use

AF is especially useful in distinguishing cholestatic from hepatocellular patterns of liver injury. In a cholestatic pattern, AF is disproportionately elevated compared to transaminases.

Mnemonic: "AF = Alkaline Flow"

Remember AF rises when flow of bile is impaired — think of “alkaline flow” being blocked in biliary obstruction or cholestasis.

GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)

Physiology and Role

Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase (GGT) is a membrane-bound enzyme involved in glutathione metabolism and amino acid transport. It is found in the liver, kidneys, pancreas, and intestines, but clinically it is most relevant as a marker of hepatobiliary injury.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

GGT is often elevated in cholestasis, biliary tract disease, and alcoholic liver disease. Because GGT is highly inducible, it is also increased by enzyme-inducing drugs (e.g., phenytoin, barbiturates) and chronic alcohol use. GGT elevation alone does not confirm liver damage, but in combination with elevated AF, it strongly suggests a hepatic source.

Diagnostic Use

GGT is primarily used to confirm the hepatic origin of elevated AF. It is also helpful in screening for alcoholic liver disease, where GGT can be disproportionately high. However, GGT is nonspecific and can be elevated in a variety of conditions without liver injury.

Mnemonic: “GGT = Good for Getting Truth”

When AF is elevated, use GGT to get the truth about whether the source is the liver — if both are up, it’s hepatic; if only AF is high, suspect bone.

Bilirubin

Physiology and Role

Bilirubin is a yellow pigment derived from the breakdown of heme, primarily from senescent red blood cells. It exists in two major forms in the bloodstream: unconjugated (indirect) and conjugated (direct). Unconjugated bilirubin is lipid-soluble and transported bound to albumin to the liver, where it is conjugated with glucuronic acid by UGT1A1 (UDP-glucuronosyltransferase), making it water-soluble for excretion into bile.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Hyperbilirubinemia results from increased production, decreased uptake/conjugation, or impaired excretion. These processes can be classified as: Pre-hepatic (e.g., hemolysis), Hepatic (e.g., hepatitis, cirrhosis, Gilbert syndrome), or Post-hepatic (e.g., biliary obstruction).

Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia indicates intrahepatic cholestasis or biliary obstruction, while unconjugated points toward hemolysis or impaired conjugation (e.g., Gilbert, Crigler-Najjar).

Diagnostic Use

Measurement of total and direct bilirubin allows calculation of the indirect fraction. This distinction is essential in differentiating causes of jaundice. Rapidly rising conjugated bilirubin in neonates is dangerous due to potential kernicterus and always pathological.

Mnemonic: "Pre-Liver, Liver, Post-Liver"

Think of bilirubin elevation in a flow: hemolysis before the liver, liver diseases in the middle, and obstruction after. Helps determine the type of jaundice quickly.

Albumin

Physiology and Role

Albumin is the most abundant plasma protein, synthesized exclusively by the liver. It plays a central role in maintaining oncotic pressure and acts as a carrier protein for hormones, fatty acids, and drugs.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Hypoalbuminemia can occur in chronic liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis), malnutrition, protein-losing enteropathy, or nephrotic syndrome. In liver disease, low albumin reflects impaired synthetic function, which is a marker of disease severity.

Diagnostic Use

Serum albumin is used to assess chronic hepatic insufficiency and nutritional status. It is also part of prognostic scoring systems like Child-Pugh and MELD. Its long half-life (~20 days) means changes reflect long-term trends.

Mnemonic: "ALB = A Long Baseline"

Albumin reflects long-term liver function — it drops slowly but surely in chronic disease, giving a baseline of hepatic capacity.

INR / PT (International Normalized Ratio / Prothrombin Time)

Physiology and Role

The prothrombin time (PT) measures the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways, particularly factors I (fibrinogen), II (prothrombin), V, VII, and X — all synthesized by the liver. The INR is a standardized form of PT used internationally.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

An elevated INR indicates deficient hepatic synthesis of clotting factors and is a sensitive marker of liver synthetic function, especially in acute liver failure. It is also elevated in vitamin K deficiency, warfarin use, and disseminated intravascular coagulation.

Diagnostic Use

PT/INR is essential in assessing acute and chronic liver disease. A worsening INR in the absence of anticoagulants may indicate progressing liver dysfunction. It is a key component of prognostic systems such as the MELD score.

Mnemonic: “INR = I Need Repair”

A rising INR tells you the liver’s synthesis machinery is failing — it’s a call that “I Need Repair.”

Ammonium (NH₄⁺)

Physiology and Role

Ammonium is a nitrogenous waste product primarily generated from protein metabolism in the gut and peripheral tissues. It is highly toxic and converted in the liver via the urea cycle into urea, which is then excreted by the kidneys.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

In liver dysfunction, especially acute liver failure or cirrhosis with portal hypertension, the urea cycle is impaired leading to hyperammonemia. Elevated ammonium crosses the blood-brain barrier, contributing to hepatic encephalopathy, characterized by confusion, asterixis, and coma in severe cases.

Diagnostic Use

Serum ammonium levels help assess the severity of hepatic encephalopathy and guide treatment (e.g., lactulose, rifaximin). However, levels must be interpreted with caution as they can be influenced by sample handling and other systemic factors.

Mnemonic: "Ammonia = Amnesia"

Elevated ammonia causes brain dysfunction and confusion, like losing your memory.

Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH)

Physiology and Role

LDH is an intracellular enzyme involved in the reversible conversion of lactate to pyruvate during anaerobic metabolism. It is present in many tissues, including the liver, heart, muscles, and red blood cells.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Elevated serum LDH indicates cellular injury or death but is non-specific. In liver disease, it may rise with acute hepatocellular damage (e.g., viral hepatitis) or liver ischemia. It also increases in hemolysis, malignancy, and other tissue injuries.

Diagnostic Use

LDH is used as an ancillary marker rather than a primary liver function test. Its isoenzyme pattern may help distinguish between liver and other tissue sources.

Mnemonic: "LDH = Leakage Due to Hurt"

LDH leaks out when cells are hurt or damaged.

Haptoglobin

Physiology and Role

Haptoglobin is a plasma glycoprotein produced by the liver that binds free hemoglobin released during intravascular hemolysis, preventing kidney damage and iron loss.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

In hemolytic anemias, free hemoglobin increases in plasma and haptoglobin binds it, leading to decreased measurable serum haptoglobin. Thus, low haptoglobin is a sensitive marker of hemolysis. In liver disease, synthesis can also be reduced, complicating interpretation.

Diagnostic Use

Measurement of haptoglobin helps differentiate hemolytic anemia from other causes of anemia. Low haptoglobin with elevated LDH and indirect bilirubin suggests hemolysis.

Mnemonic: "Haptoglobin = Hemoglobin Harvester"

Haptoglobin grabs free hemoglobin to protect kidneys and conserve iron.

Ferritin (in Liver Disease)

Physiology and Role

Ferritin is the intracellular protein that stores and releases iron in a controlled manner. Serum ferritin correlates with total body iron stores but also acts as an acute phase reactant.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Elevated ferritin is seen in iron overload disorders such as hemochromatosis, chronic liver disease, inflammation, and malignancy. In liver disease, ferritin may rise due to both iron accumulation and inflammation.

Diagnostic Use

Ferritin measurement aids in diagnosing iron overload syndromes and monitoring treatment. In liver disease, interpreting ferritin requires consideration of the inflammatory status.

Mnemonic: "Ferritin = Fire Alarm"

High ferritin can mean lots of iron or that the body is 'on fire' with inflammation.

Ceruloplasmin

Physiology and Role

Ceruloplasmin is a copper-binding plasma protein synthesized in the liver. It functions as a ferroxidase enzyme, facilitating iron metabolism by oxidizing Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ for incorporation into transferrin.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Decreased ceruloplasmin is a hallmark of Wilson’s disease, a genetic disorder of copper metabolism causing hepatic and neurologic damage. Ceruloplasmin is also an acute phase reactant, increasing in inflammation.

Diagnostic Use

Low serum ceruloplasmin supports the diagnosis of Wilson’s disease, especially combined with elevated urinary copper and clinical features.

Mnemonic: "Ceruloplasmin = Copper Carrier"

Ceruloplasmin carries copper and helps keep iron metabolism running smoothly.

Alpha-Fetoprotein (AFP)

Physiology and Role

Alpha-fetoprotein is a fetal plasma protein produced by the yolk sac and liver. After birth, its levels drop rapidly and remain low in healthy adults.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Implications

Elevated AFP is a tumor marker for hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and certain germ cell tumors. It can also rise in active hepatitis and regenerative nodules but to a lesser degree.

Diagnostic Use

AFP measurement is used for surveillance in patients at risk for HCC, especially those with cirrhosis or chronic hepatitis B/C. Rising AFP in these patients warrants imaging for tumor detection.

Mnemonic: "AFP = A Fetal Protein"

AFP normally drops after birth; a rise in adults signals liver cancer or regeneration.
