Welcome to physiology about periods

We'll talk mostly about hormones.

Introduction

Menstruation starts at menarche, which starts at a medican age of 12.4 y/o and ends at menopause, which has an average onset at 51 y/o.

In terms of timing day 1 is the first day of heavy menstrual flow, and a normal cycle ought to be regular in frequency, regularity, duration and volume of flow. Normal cycle length is defined as 24-38 days, hence why "infrequent" means more than 38 and "frequent" means less than 24. While quantification of volume is hard in practice, for research, 80 mL per cycle is the upper limit for normal flow; anything beyond that is considered heavy. Light flow (i.e., below 5 mL per cycle) is usually not associated with pathology. In terms of regularity, 7 days difference between cycle lengths is considered the upper boundary for those aged 26-41, while those younger or older may have fluctuations up to 9 days. A good average would be +/- 4 days.

The menstrual cycle comprises 2 distinct cycles: ovarian, endometrial. The ovarian cycle consists of:

  1. Follicular phase
  2. Ovulation phase
  3. Luteal phase

The endometrial cycle consists of the:

  1. Proliferative phase
  2. Secretory phase
  3. Menstrual phase

Both cycles occur in parallel, with the follicular phase, and menstrual / proliferative phases corresponding, as well as a correspondence between the luteal and secretory phase.

Hormonal regulation

Regulation starts in the hypothalamus, where Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH) is secreated in a pulsatile fashion. It gets transported to the anterior pituitary, where it stimulates the release of FSH and LSH. They then travel through the bloodstream to the ovaries, and stimulate the production of sex steroid hormones from follicular cells.

Within the follilcle, 2 cell types produce hormones:

  1. Theca cells: LH -> (cholesterol desmolase) Progesterone and andronestenedione
  2. Granulosa cells FSH -> (aromatase) Andronestenedione -> Testosterone -> 17-beta estradiol (oestrogen)

These sex steroid hormones provide negative feedback on the anterior pituitary.

Menstrual cycle

Phase 1

The first phase correspons to the overian follicular and endometrial proliferative< phases, in which the overian follicle matures and the endometrium proliferates, respectively. The overian follicular phase always starts on day 1 and, in a typical 28 day cycle, lasts 1-14 days. The endometrial proliferative phase then, logically starts after menstrual bleeding seizes, and continues until ovulation; preparing the uterus for implantation.

Follicular phase

FSH stimulates a cohort of primordial follicules to mature into Graafian follicles, and promotes the production of oestrogen in the granulosa cells, with most of it produced by the dominant follicle.

Around day 7, several 10mm antral follicles are present in the overy. Oestrogen then starts to negatively feedback FSH, which degenerates the non-dominant follicles.

Proliferative phase

Oestrogen from the growing follicles stimulates the development on the endometrial stroma and glands from the basal layer (decidua basalis). By the end of the proliferative phase, the endometrium reaches maximal development (i.e., 8-12mm thick). Oestrogen (a.k.a., estradiol) also modifies the elasticity/protein content of cervical muculs to create channels, facilitating sperm entry.

Ovulation

Exactly in the middle, on day 14, ovulation occurs. As estradiol levels have consistently risen throughout the follicular phase, once a critical level of estradiol is reached, the feedback switches from negative to positive. Note that this is not fully understood. This results in a sudden surge of LH secretion up to 10-fold, accompanied by a smaller rise in FSH concentration.

Within this hormonal milieu, the mature follicle secretes cytokines which leads to its rupture and the release of the oocyte. It usually occurs 36-44 hours are onset of the LH surge, while the cervical changes (watery mucus to facility entry) continue. After ovulation, oestrogen decreases.

Phase 2

While the first phases are relatively variable, the luteal and secretory phases are surprisingly consistent within an individual: 14 days.

The high levels of LH stimulate progesterone production, which in turn stimulates the maturation of the endometrium:

  1. Development of complex glands
  2. Increases glycogen accumulation
  3. Increases the surface area of the spinal arteries
  4. Thickens and de-elastifies the mucus (stops sperm)

If an ovum is fertilised, it releases Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG) which stimulates the corpus luteum to maintain progesterone production and thus keep up these changes. However, without a fertilised ovum, progesterone negatively feedbacks onto LH rease in the anterior pituitary, which results in a rapid decline in levels. Due to the rapid decline, GnRH is released, which sets in motion the next cycle.

Due to this rapid decline, progestorone levels are insufficient to sustain the changes, which causes shedding of the endometrium. This is referred to as menses, which normally lasts 8 days or less and usually does not contain clots.

Pathologic bleeding

While we'll treat this in another article, this is just a small introduction.

Anovulatory cycles

While normal for the first year after menarche and before menopause, anovulatory cycles (i.e., in which no ovulation follows the follicular phase) are frequently associated with pathology. As no significant progestorone increase is seen, there is no maturation of endometrium after proliferation. Since there is also no sudden decrease, there is no synchronised shedding. Since estrogen stimulates growth, the endometrium just thickens until it slowly breaks down and sloughs off in typically irregular intervals. This pathology may be associated with "PTH":

  1. PCOS
  2. Thyroid dysfunction
  3. Hyperprolactinemia

Abnormal uterine bleeding

Heavy flow, or abnormal bleeding in general, may be caused by structural and nonstructural causes classified in the "PALM-COEIN" system:

  1. Structural aetiologies
    1. Polyps
    2. Adenomyosis
    3. Leiomyomas
    4. Malignancy/hyperplasia
  2. Nonstructural aetiologies
    1. Coagulopathy
    2. Ovulatory dysfunction
    3. Endometrial dysfunction
    4. Iatrogenic factors
    5. Not otherwise specified

Ovulatory dysfunction usually refers to endocrine and metabolic disorders affecting the HPO axis: "PTH" (PCOS, Thyroid, Hyperprolactin/-androgens/-cortisol). Endometrial dysfunction then, consists of Asherman syndrome, inflammation and other endometrial issues.
