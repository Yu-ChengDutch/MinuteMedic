Introduction to Hypersensitivity: Immune Overreaction

Hypersensitivity refers to excessive, harmful immune responses to exogenous or endogenous antigens, classified by Gell and Coombs into four types (I–IV). These reactions arise from the interplay of antibodies, T cells, and inflammatory mediators, often targeting benign substances (e.g., pollen, food).

Historical Context

The term "allergy" was coined by Clemens von Pirquet in 1906 to describe altered immune reactivity. The Gell-Coombs classification (1963) organized hypersensitivity by mechanism rather than clinical presentation.

Key Immune Components

Mast cells, eosinophils, IgE, and Th2 cells dominate Type I, while IgG/IgM, complement, and cytotoxic T cells drive Types II–IV. Dysregulation of these pathways underlies diseases ranging from anaphylaxis to autoimmune vasculitis.

Mnemonic: "Allergy Starts Fast, Then Slows Down"

Type I = Fast (minutes) | Types II/III = Intermediate (hours-days) | Type IV = Slow (days).

Antibodies in Hypersensitivity: Structure and Function

Antibodies (immunoglobulins) mediate hypersensitivity via antigen recognition and effector functions:

1. IgE (Type I)

Structure: Monomeric with high-affinity FcεRI binding sites. Function: Binds mast cells/basophils; cross-linking by antigen triggers histamine release.

2. IgG/IgM (Types II/III)

Structure: IgG (monomeric), IgM (pentameric). Function:

  1. Opsonization (FcγR-mediated phagocytosis).
  2. Complement activation (classical pathway → C3a/C5a).
  3. Antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) (e.g., IgG + NK cells).

3. IgA (Rare Hypersensitivity)

Structure: Dimeric in mucosa. Function: Can cause IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura) via immune complex deposition.

Mnemonic: "IgE = Emergency"

IgE acts fastest (anaphylaxis) and is Elevated in Eczema/Eosinophilia.

Type I Hypersensitivity: Immediate (IgE-Mediated)

Pathophysiology: Antigen (allergen) cross-links IgE on mast cells → degranulation → preformed mediator release (histamine, tryptase) and new synthesis (leukotrienes, PGD2).

Key Mediators

  1. Histamine: Vasodilation (flushing, hypotension), bronchoconstriction, pruritus.
  2. Leukotriene C4: Sustained bronchospasm (asthma).
  3. TNF-α: Recruits neutrophils/eosinophils (late-phase reaction).

Clinical Examples

  1. Anaphylaxis: Systemic (peanuts, venom).
  2. Allergic rhinitis: Localized (pollen).
  3. Atopic dermatitis: Chronic Th2-driven inflammation.

Mnemonic: "HALT" for Histamine Effects

Hypotension | Angioedema | Lacrimation | Tachycardia.

Type II Hypersensitivity: Antibody-Mediated Cytotoxicity

Pathophysiology: IgG/IgM binds cell-surface antigens → destruction via:

  1. Complement activation (MAC → cell lysis).
  2. Phagocytosis (FcγR on macrophages).
  3. ADCC (NK cells).

Clinical Examples

  1. Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (anti-RBC IgG).
  2. Graves’ disease (TSHR-stimulating IgG).
  3. Myasthenia gravis (anti-AChR IgG).

Mnemonic: "CELL" for Type II Targets

Cells (RBCs, platelets) | Endocrine (TSHR) | Ligand receptors (AChR) | Lungs (Goodpasture’s).

Type III Hypersensitivity: Immune Complex-Mediated

Pathophysiology: Antigen-antibody (IgG/IgM) complexes deposit in tissues → complement activation (C5a → neutrophil recruitment) → inflammation and tissue damage. Key features:

Mechanistic Steps

  1. Complex Formation: Soluble antigen binds antibody in circulation.
  2. Deposition: Complexes lodge in vascular beds (kidneys, joints, skin) due to hemodynamic factors (e.g., high pressure in glomeruli).
  3. Inflammation: C3a/C5a attract neutrophils → release proteases/ROS → vessel damage (vasculitis).

Clinical Examples

  1. Serum sickness: Anti-foreign protein (e.g., horse antitoxin) complexes.
  2. SLE nephritis: Anti-dsDNA/deposition in glomeruli.
  3. Cryoglobulinemic vasculitis: Cold-precipitable IgM/IgG complexes.

Mnemonic: "SAD" for Type III Triggers

Serum sickness | Autoimmunity (SLE) | Drugs (penicillin).

Type IV Hypersensitivity: Delayed (T-Cell-Mediated)

Pathophysiology: Antigen-specific Th1/Th17 or cytotoxic CD8+ T cells drive inflammation 48–72 hours post-exposure. No antibody involvement.

Subtypes and Mechanisms

  1. Contact Dermatitis (e.g., poison ivy): Haptens bind skin proteins → Langerhans cells present antigen to Th1 → IFN-γ → epidermal spongiosis.
  2. Tuberculin Reaction (PPD test): Memory T cells recruit macrophages → granuloma formation.
  3. Drug-Induced Stevens-Johnson Syndrome: CD8+ T cells kill keratinocytes via Fas-FasL.

Clinical Examples

  1. Allergic contact dermatitis (nickel, urushiol).
  2. Type 1 diabetes mellitus (insulitis).
  3. Graft rejection (direct allorecognition).

Mnemonic: "T-Cells Take Time"

Type IV is Tardy (48+ hours), Tissue-specific, and Treatable with immunosuppressants.

Diagnostic Approach to Hypersensitivity Reactions

Type I (IgE-Mediated)

  1. Skin prick testing: Wheal/flare to allergens.
  2. Serum IgE: Allergen-specific IgE (e.g., ImmunoCAP).
  3. Tryptase: Elevated in anaphylaxis (mast cell degranulation).

Type II/III (Antibody-Mediated)

  1. Direct Coombs test: Detects anti-RBC IgG (autoimmune hemolysis).
  2. Anti-dsDNA/C3/C4: Low C3/C4 in SLE nephritis.
  3. Cryoglobulins: Precipitate at cold temperatures.

Type IV (T-Cell-Mediated)

  1. Patch testing: Delayed reaction to contact allergens.
  2. PPD skin test: Induration ≥10 mm = prior TB exposure.
  3. Biopsy: Lymphocytic infiltrate (e.g., spongiotic dermatitis).

Treatment of Hypersensitivity Reactions

Type I

  1. Anaphylaxis: Epinephrine (IM), antihistamines (H1/H2 blockers), glucocorticoids.
  2. Chronic atopy: Allergen avoidance, omalizumab (anti-IgE), leukotriene antagonists.

Type II/III

  1. Autoimmune cytopenias: IVIG (blocks Fc receptors), rituximab (anti-CD20).
  2. SLE nephritis: Mycophenolate/cyclophosphamide + glucocorticoids.

Type IV

  1. Contact dermatitis: Topical glucocorticoids, calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus).
  2. Graft rejection: Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine), anti-IL-2R (basiliximab).

Mnemonic: "STOP" for Anaphylaxis Management

Supplemental oxygen | Trendelenburg position | Oxygen + fluids | Pinephrine (IM thigh).
