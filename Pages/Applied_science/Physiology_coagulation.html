Introduction

Hemostasis is the process that stops bleeding. It involves plasma, platelet and vascular components. There are four main stages:

  1. Vasoconstriction
  2. Activation of platelets and platelet plug
  3. Coagulation cascade
  4. Fibrin clot formation

Vasoconstriction

The blood vessels consist of many layers, on of which is the smooth muscle layer. This layer is generally innervated by the sympathetic nervous system (fight or flight) and not at all by the parasympathetic i.e., "feed and breed" nervous system. Generally however, if there are any substances causing vasoconstriction, they have another way of causing calcium influx. In case of endothelial damage this is done by the release of the substances thromboxane and endothelin. Many other factors may contribute to increased endothelin production: products of platelet activation, thrombin, insulin and even hypoxia.


Platelet activation

Platelets contain on their surface glycoproteins amongst which is glycoprotein Ia/IIa which has an affinity for type I and IV collagen. Normally, the collagenous layer of the vessel is not exposed, but injury will expose it. This means that the platelets will stick to the site of injury. Von Willebrandfactor released by the platelets and the damaged site, serves to strengthen that bond. The connection between the wall and platelet causes the latter to activate: a cascade is put in motion to continue the process.

Activated platelets will release the content of stored gradules: ADP, serotonin, platelet-activating factor, vWFd, thromboxane A2, all of which serve to activate again more platelets. Lastly, the platelet changes shape from spherical to stellate.

Coagulation cascade

The coagulation cascade consists of three discrete parts:

  1. Intrinsic: Triggered by surface contact
  2. Extrinsic: Triggered by tissue damage
  3. Common: Uniting the former pathways

Essentially, the product of the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways is exactly the same: namely, an enzyme to convert factor X into activated factor X. The common pathway then, exists to take factor X and create fibrin. It does so by using factor Xa to convert prothrombin into thrombin, which in turn converts fibrinogen into fibrin.


Protein C then, inhibits factors VII and V, thus modulating their activation.

Vitamin K-dependance

Vitamin K is involved in blood clotting, as it essential to the addition of a carboxyl group to these factors in the liver. The factors where this is t he case are II, VII, IX and X as well as all of the named proteins Protein s, Protein C, Protein Z. Protein C, as discussed in an inhibitor of factors Va and VIIIa. Protein S serves as co-factor in this inhibition. Protein Z, then, is responsible for the degradation of Factor Xa (which, as you remember, is the product of the in- and extrinsic cascades).

The necessity of the protein C inhibition, becomes clear in the "Leiden" variant of factor V which is a mutation in the production of factor V which causes protein C to be unable to bind to factor V which in turn creates an unchecked cascade, leading to hypercoagulability. The condition is inherited autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance, and has no current cure. It occurs in up to 5% of the general caucasian population and nearly 30% of people with DVT, occuring more often in whites than any other population.

Fibrinolysis

Eventually, the now formed blood clot has to dissolve again. This accomplished through the use of an enzyme called plasmin which is produced from plasminogen in the liver. Eventually, plasmin breaks down the clot into fibrin degradation products (FDPs) which in turn compete with thrombin which causes the inhibition of clot formation, as thrombin is necessary for the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin. A specific test for fibrinolysis then, is the D-dimer which is one of the FDPs.

Alternatively, one may use thromboelastometry (TEM) which measures the time to clot, as well as the final elasticity of the clot, using a rotating needle swirling through the clot. It is don on whole blood, and is a very fast test possible in a Point of Care (PoC) setting.
