Welcome to renal anatomy!

Today we will learn about the anatomy of the kidneys!

They kidneys lie retroperitoneally on either side of the vertebral column. Roughly, they extend from T12 to L3 with the right kidney being slightly higher. Immediately superior to the kidneys sit the adrenal glands, which are enveloped in renal fascia.


The kidneys are directly envolped in the aforementioned renal capsule. This capsule is coated in a thick layer of perirenal fat. Surrounding this thick layer of fat, lies the renal fascia which is then coated posterolaterally in pararenal fat.

Internally, the renal parenchyma may be divided into two main areas: cortex and medulla. The cortex extends into the medulla in triangular shapes called the renal pyramids.


These renal pyramids end in renal papilla. These papilla attach individually to renal calices (singular: calyx) which in turn connect to a number of larger collecting vats called major calices (singular: calyx) which then collects into a singular renal pelvis which in turn connects the kidney to the ureter. External structures such as indeed the ureter, connect with the kidney at a deep fissure called the renal hilum.

Blood supply is arranged through the renal arteries, which branch directly off the aorta abdominalis, immediately distal to the origin of the a. mesenterica superior. Noteworthy is that the right renal artery is longer and crosses the vena cava.

Both renal arteries then enter their respective kidney through the renal hilum, where they immediately divide into a posterior and anterior division which supply respectively 75% and 25% of the renal blood supply. Each division then sprouts five segmental arteries. Along the lateral plane of the kidney runs the imaginary line of Brodel which is the avascular plane dividing the portions supplied by the anterior and posterior sections.


Now on a microscopic level, the segmental arteries divide into interlobar arteries which are situated on either side of every renal pyramid. These then divide into arcuate arteries, perpendicular to which the interlobular arteries sprout. It is they that pass through the cortex, where they divide into the afferent arterioles.


For the outer two-thirds of the kidney the peritubular network supplies the nephrons with oxygen and nutrients, whereas the vasa recta fulfill this role for the inner two-thirds.


Of course, both kidneys drain into renal veins, of which the left is longer, having to cross the aorta abdominalis anteriorly in order to reac the vena cava.
