Muscle tone is a complex and dynamic state that describes the tension in a relaxed muscle or the resistance felt by an examiner during passive joint movement when muscles are at rest. It is an adaptive function of the neuromotor apparatus, essential for movement and postural control. Abnormalities in muscle tone can manifest as hypertonia, which includes spasticity and rigidity, or other disorders such as dystonia and paratonia.

Spasticity

Definition

Spasticity is a motor disorder characterised by a velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes (muscle tone) with exaggerated tendon jerks, resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex, often as a component of an upper motor neuron syndrome. More recently, it has been redefined as "disordered sensory-motor control, resulting from an upper motor neuron lesion, presenting as intermittent or sustained involuntary activation of muscles". A broader definition describes it as "involuntary muscle hyperactivity in the presence of central paresis".

Classification

Spasticity can be classified as 'phasic' or 'tonic', based on the predominant involvement of dynamic or static components of muscle stretch reflexes, respectively.

Aetiology

Spasticity results from an upper motor neuron lesion. This includes cortical lesions involving corticoreticular fibres, leading to unopposed facilitatory action of medial reticulospinal tract (RST), and spinal cord lesions (myelopathy) impacting descending long tracts.

Pathophysiology

The underlying mechanisms of spasticity involve both spinal and supraspinal influences, as well as sensory feedback and non-neural factors:
- Spinal Influence: Can arise from increased excitation or decreased inhibition. This includes enhanced fusimotor drive, denervation hypersensitivity, axonal sprouting, hyperexcitability of alpha motor neurons, and excitation of interneurons. Altered spinal inhibitory circuitry plays a role, with decreased presynaptic inhibition of Ia afferents, reduced disynaptic reciprocal Ia afferent inhibition, and diminished Ib afferent mediated inhibition.
- Supraspinal Influence: Normally, human muscle tone is balanced by inhibitory corticospinal tract (CST) and dorsal RST, and facilitatory medial RST and vestibulospinal tract (VST). Lesions disrupting these tracts lead to spasticity; for instance, cortical lesions cause spasticity due to involvement of corticoreticular fibres and unopposed medial RST activity.
- Sensory Feedback: Inadequate prediction of sensory consequences of movements, leading to co-contraction of antagonist muscles as a compensatory strategy for weakness.
- Non-Neural Factors: Changes in tissue viscoelastic properties, such as elastic stiffness and viscous damping, also contribute.
- Clasp-Knife Phenomenon: A sudden release of resistance during passive stretching, attributed to length dependency of spasticity and excitation of inhibitory group III and IV muscle afferents.
- Clonus: Regular, repetitive, rhythmic muscle contractions due to sudden, maintained stretch, potentially involving a stretch reflex-inverse stretch reflex sequence or hyperactivity of muscle spindles.

Assessment

Severity of muscle hyperactivity in spasticity can be described by the Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS), Tardieu Scale, and Frequency of Spasms Score.

Treatment

A multimodal approach is recommended, with several treatments supported by GRADE A evidence:
- Stretching Exercises: Passive static/dynamic stretching can be an adjunctive therapy, with a total daily duration not exceeding 2.5 hours to avoid pain.
- Static Stretching with Positioning Orthoses: Their effectiveness in reducing wrist flexor spasticity is still uncertain, requiring more research.
- Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS): High-frequency TENS (100 Hz, >30 min/session, >2 weeks) can reduce lower extremity spasticity. Its effect on upper limb spasticity needs further investigation.
- Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (ESWT): Radial ESWT can effectively reduce spasticity, with effects lasting up to 12 weeks, and repeating sessions within four weeks can maintain the effect.
- Repetitive Peripheral Magnetic Stimulation (rPMS): May reduce spasticity in upper and lower extremities, but optimal protocol and duration are not yet established.
- Non-Invasive Brain Stimulation (NIBS): Low-frequency repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) of an unaffected hemisphere and/or anodal transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) of an affected hemisphere can reduce upper extremity spasticity.
- Botulinum Toxin A (BoNT-A) Injection: Considered essential for post-stroke spasticity management due to compelling evidence of its efficacy in reducing spasticity. It modulates cortical excitability and prevents contractures. Ultrasound (US)-guided injections improve safety and accuracy. Doses vary by product (e.g., Botox®, Dysport®, Xeomin®), with intervals of 12 weeks or more recommended. Lower doses may be recommended in hot climates or for smaller muscle mass. While effective for muscle tone and hygiene, its contribution to functional recovery and spasticity-related pain is debated.
- Dry Needling (DN): Useful as an adjuvant therapy, especially in lower extremities for short-term spasticity reduction (1 week), but not effective at 4 weeks.
- Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB): An alternative for generalised, severe post-stroke spasticity that doesn't respond to conventional medical management, showing reduction in spastic hypertonia, muscle tone, pain, and improved quality of life.
- Whole-Body Vibration Therapy (WBV): An adjunct treatment, particularly for individuals under 60 years, at frequencies below 20 Hz for 10 minutes, especially when combined with other therapies.
- Localized Muscle Vibration (LMV): A safe adjunct to conventional neurorehabilitation for reducing upper extremity spasticity (elbow and wrist), but more research is needed for lower limbs and optimal protocols. Oral medications are generally not supported by GRADE A evidence.

Dystonia

Definition

Dystonia is a movement disorder characterised by sustained or intermittent muscle contractions causing abnormal and often repetitive, movements, postures, or both. Dystonic movements are typically patterned, twisting, and may be tremulous. It is often initiated or worsened by voluntary action and associated with overflow muscle activation.

Phenomenological Spectrum

Dystonia can be characterized by its features across time, space distribution, and body state's impact:
- Time:
- Rhythmicity: Can be rhythmic (e.g., dystonic tremors) or arrhythmic (e.g., dystonic movement or posture).
- Speed: Varies from very fast (e.g., blepharospasm) to slow (e.g., dystonic postures).
- Duration of Muscular Contraction: Sustained or intermittent.
- Duration of Movement: Can be continual (repeating without sudden recurrence, e.g., dystonic movements) or continuous (without stopping, e.g., dystonic postures).
- Space Distribution:
- Body Distribution: Classified as focal (one body part), segmental (≥2 contiguous parts), multifocal (≥2 non-contiguous parts), hemidystonia (ipsilateral arm and leg), or generalised (≥3 body parts including trunk and ≥2 other sites).
- Muscular Pattern: Dystonic movements are typically patterned, involving the same muscle group repetitively.
- Amplitude: Can be small (dystonic tremor) or medium (dystonic movements).
- Body State's Impact:
- Action Rule: Often initiated or worsened by voluntary action. Overflow muscle activation is involuntary contraction in a contiguous or non-adjacent site. Mirror dystonia refers to movements triggered in the affected limb by actions of the unaffected contralateral limb.
- Suppressibility: Some dystonic movements are suppressible, often by gestes antagonistes (sensory tricks), which are voluntary movements that temporarily alleviate dystonia.
- Wakefulness: Generally present while awake, but severe dystonia can persist during sleep.



Classification (Two Axes)

The current classification system uses two main axes:
- Axis I: Clinical Characteristics:
- - > Age at Onset: Infancy (birth-2 years), Childhood (3-12 years), Adolescence (13-20 years), Early adulthood (21-40 years), Late adulthood (>40 years). Dystonia in children is more likely to generalize, while in adults it often remains focal.
- - > Body Distribution: As described above (Focal, Segmental, Multifocal, Generalised, Hemidystonia).
- - > Temporal Pattern:
- - > Disease Course: Static (no progression) or Progressive (progression over time).
- - > Variability: Persistent (same intensity throughout day), Paroxysmal (self-limited, triggered episodes), Diurnal fluctuations (vary with circadian rhythm), Action-specific (only during specific tasks).
- - > Associated Features:
- - > Isolated Dystonia: Dystonia is the sole motor feature, possibly with tremor.
- - > Combined Dystonia: Dystonia co-occurs with other movement disorders (e.g., myoclonus, parkinsonism).
- - > Complex Dystonia: Dystonia accompanied by other neurological or systemic manifestations beyond movement disorders.

- Axis II: Etiology:
- - > Nervous System Pathology: Evidence of degeneration, evidence of structural (often static) lesions, or no evidence of degeneration or structural lesions.
- - > Cause Definition:
- - > Inherited: Due to proven genetic origin (autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked recessive, X-linked dominant, mitochondrial).
- - > Acquired: Due to a known non-genetic specific cause (e.g., perinatal brain injury, infection, drugs, toxins, vascular events, brain injury, neoplastic, autoimmune, functional).
- - > Idiopathic: Without an identifiable cause, can be sporadic or familial.

Aetiology (Detailed)


- Inherited Dystonia: Over 400 genes have been linked to dystonia or dystonic symptoms. Examples include:
- Autosomal Dominant: Oppenheim dystonia (DYT-TOR1A), childhood and adult-onset familial cranial limb dystonia (DYT-THAP1), Dopa-responsive dystonia (DYT/PARK-GCH1), rapid-onset dystonia–parkinsonism (DYT/PARK-ATP1A3), myoclonus–dystonia (DYT-SGCE, DYT-KCTD17), Neuroferritinopathy (NBIA/CHOREA-FTL), Huntington’s disease, Machado–Joseph disease (SCA-ATXN3), Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease, Primary Familial Brain Calcification, DYT-KMT2B, DYT-VPS16, DYT-EIF2AK2, DYT-AOPEP, DYT-ANO3, DYT-HPCA, DYT-GNAL.
- Autosomal Recessive: Wilson disease, Neurodegeneration with brain iron accumulation (NBIA/DYT-PANK2, NBIA/DYT/PARK-PLA2G6, Aceruloplasminemia, FAHN), Aromatic-L-amino acid decarboxylase (DYT-DDC) deficiency, Niemann–Pick type C, Juvenile neuronal ceroid-lipofuscinosis, GM1/GM2 gangliosidosis, Metachromatic leukodystrophy, Homocystinuria, Glutaric acidemia type 1 (DYT/CHOR-GCDH), Methylmalonic aciduria (DYT/CHOR-MUT), Hartnup disease, Ataxia–telangiectasia, Neuroacanthocytosis, Neuronal intranuclear inclusion disease, Hereditary spastic paraplegia (HSP-SPG7), Sjögren–Larsson syndrome, Biotin-responsive basal ganglia disease (DYT-SLC19A3), DYT-HPCA, DYT-AOPEP.
- X-Linked Recessive: Dystonia-parkinsonism or Lubag syndrome (DYT/PARK-TAF1), Lesch-Nyhan syndrome (DYT/CHOR-HPRT), Mohr-Tranebjaerg syndrome (DYT-TIMM8A).
- X-Linked Dominant: Rett syndrome.
- Mitochondrial: Leigh syndrome, Leber’s hereditary ocular neuropathy (LHON) plus dystonia (DYT-mt-ND6).
- Acquired Dystonia: Caused by environmental insults leading to brain, spinal, or peripheral nervous system damage.
- Perinatal Brain Injury: Athetoid cerebral palsy, delayed onset dystonia.
- Brain Injury: Head trauma, brain surgery, electrical injury, cervical cord injury, peripheral injury.
- Vascular: Ischemia, intracranial haemorrhage, arteriovenous malformation, hypoxia.
- Neoplastic and Paraneoplastic: Brain tumours, paraneoplastic encephalitis.
- Drugs: Dopamine D2 receptor-blocking drugs (e.g., neuroleptics causing tardive dystonia, acute dystonic reaction), levodopa and dopamine agonists (in Parkinson's patients), anticonvulsants, calcium channel blockers.
- Toxic: Manganese, cobalt, carbon disulphide, cyanide, methanol, disulfiram.
- Metabolic: Hypercalcaemia, hypoparathyroidism, hypoglycaemia, hyperbilirubinemia, pontine myelinolysis.
- Encephalitis, Infections, and Post-Infections: Reye syndrome, poststreptococcal, Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease, viral encephalitis, HIV infection.
- Autoimmune: Primary antiphospholipid syndrome, multiple sclerosis, Sjögren syndrome.
- Psychogenic (Functional) Dystonia: Can mimic organic dystonia, but cues like psychiatric history, abrupt onset, inconsistent movements, and attenuation with distraction may differentiate it.

Pathophysiology

Dystonia is increasingly understood as a dysfunctional network disorder involving the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical and cortico-cerebello-thalamo-cortical loops. Key mechanisms include:
- Anatomical Correlates: While basal ganglia (putamen, globus pallidus internus) are implicated, structural abnormalities are also noted in the cerebellum, thalamus, and cortex.
- Loss of Inhibition: Manifests as lack of surround inhibition, co-contraction of antagonistic muscles, and overflow of activity. Studies show enhanced cortical excitability and shorter cortical silent periods in dystonia.
- Abnormal Sensory Function: Patients often experience pain (e.g., cervical dystonia) and exhibit sensory phenomena like photosensitivity or neck pain before dystonia. The *geste antagoniste
- is a notable feature, indicating abnormal sensory processing and sensorimotor integration. Reduced perception of tonic vibration reflex (TVR) and abnormalities in spatial and temporal discrimination thresholds (GOT, STDT) are observed.
- Abnormal Synaptic Plasticity: Excessive plasticity responses, like altered long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD), and loss of topographical specificity in sensorimotor circuits are suggested.
- Mirror Movements: Considered an expression of motor overflow, linked to impaired interhemispheric inhibition.
- Molecular Mechanisms: Diverse pathways are affected:
- Striatal Dopamine Signalling: Defects in dopamine synthesis (GCH1, SPR, PTS, TH, QDPR, DDC, DNAJC12), cycling (SLC18A2, SLC6A3), and signalling (GNAL, ADCY5, GNAO1, GNB1, DRD2) in striatal neurons are central. Alterations in D1 and D2 receptor signalling, affecting direct and indirect pathways, are implicated.
- Gene Transcription and Neurodevelopment: Pathogenic variants in THAP1 and KMT2B, YY1, and TAF1 affect gene expression and neurodevelopment, leading to aberrant transcriptional regulation and potentially affecting myelination and synaptic transmission.
- Calcium Signalling and Synaptic Transmission: Imbalance in Ca2+ homeostasis, often leading to neuronal hyperexcitability, is linked to genes like ATP1A3, KCNN2, KCNA1, TSPOAP1, CACNA1A, PNKD, PRRT2, SCN8A, HPCA, ANO3, KCTD17, TOR1A, and SGCE.
- Cellular Stress Response: Dysregulated eIF2α signalling (PRKRA, EIF2AK2, ATF4, TOR1A, THAP1, XDP) contributes to pathogenesis, potentially linking to viral infections as a risk factor.
- Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) and Nuclear Envelope (NE) Function: TorsinA (TOR1A gene) dysfunction leads to protein misfolding, trafficking issues, ER stress, and nuclear pore complex (NPC) deficits.
- Autophagy and Lysosomal Function: Dysfunction in endolysosomal and autophagic systems (VPS16, VPS11, VPS41, IRF2BPL, SQSTM1) leads to accumulation of misfolded proteins and damaged organelles, seen in various complex dystonias.
- Cytoskeleton: Abnormalities in cytoskeletal proteins (SGCE, TUBB4A, ACTB) affect synapse assembly, function, and myelination, causing aberrant neuronal firing.

Assessment

Diagnosis relies mainly on a thorough clinical evaluation and physical examination by movement disorder specialists. Genetic testing, especially next-generation sequencing (NGS) techniques (gene panels, exome sequencing, whole genome sequencing), is crucial for inherited forms, particularly in early-onset or non-isolated dystonia. Variant-specific testing is also used for known founder mutations.

Treatment

Treatment options are largely symptomatic:
- Chemo-denervation: Botulinum toxin injections are widely used for focal and segmental dystonia to inhibit muscle contraction and reduce tone.
- Pharmacological Therapies:
- Levodopa: Highly effective for dopa-responsive dystonias (DRD).
- Anticholinergics: An alternative, though high doses can cause significant side effects.
- Other options include baclofen (oral or intrathecal), tetrabenazine, benzodiazepines, and neuroleptics, with variable responses and side effects.
- Surgical Interventions: Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS) of the globus pallidus internus (GPi) is a successful alternative for severe, drug-refractory dystonia, and less commonly, the subthalamic nucleus (STN-DBS). Response to DBS varies depending on the genetic cause.
- Physical Therapies: Should always be an integral part of treatment.

Rigidity

Definition

Rigidity, in contrast to spasticity, is a resistance to passive stretching that does not depend on the velocity of movement and equally affects flexors and extensors, resulting in a uniform resistance known as the 'lead pipe' phenomenon. It can also present as 'cogwheel rigidity' if an underlying tremor is superimposed.

Aetiology

Rigidity is a cardinal sign of Parkinson's disease (PD) and is also seen in atypical parkinsonism.

Pathophysiology


- Exaggeration of Long-Latency Stretch Reflexes (LLSR): While spinal reflexes are often normal, parkinsonian rigidity is linked to exaggerated long-loop or long-latency stretch reflexes (supraspinal influence).
- Enhanced Shortening Reaction (SR) and Stretch-Induced Inhibition (SII): The combination of these phenomena contributes to the 'lead pipe' effect.
- Role of Brainstem: Non-dopaminergic systems in the brainstem, affected by alpha-synuclein deposition in PD (e.g., sublaterodorsal nucleus, nucleus reticularis gigantocellularis, locus coeruleus, caudal raphe, pedunculopontine nucleus), contribute to altered motor neuron excitability and rigidity.
- Non-Neural Factors: Viscoelastic properties of muscle fibres and surrounding connective tissues also play a role.
- Network Hypothesis: Abnormal functional connectivity in fronto-parietal networks is associated with akinetic-rigid subtypes of PD.
- Activation Manoeuvre (Froment’s Manoeuvre): Rigidity can increase with muscle contraction in the contralateral limb, possibly due to enhanced LLSR, altered spinal motoneuron excitability, or facilitation of bilateral descending reticulospinal projection.

Paratonia

Definition

Paratonia is an increased muscle tone in response to passive movement that is proportional to the strength of the stimulus applied. It is non-velocity dependent and can be elicited in any direction, differentiating it from spasticity and rigidity. It can be facilitatory (patient assists passive movement) or oppositional (resistance increases with increasing movements).

Aetiology

Primarily linked to defective response inhibition secondary to frontal lobe dysfunction.

Pathophysiology


- Defective Response Inhibition: Orbitofrontal damage and frontal-subcortical dysfunction disrupt the balance between internal and external motor control circuits, leading to disinhibited behaviour.
- Non-Neural Factors: Increased tissue stiffness due to Advanced Glycation End Products (AGEs) deposition in muscle collagen may contribute, especially in dementia patients.

Treatment

Botulinum toxin is known to relieve the involuntary resistance seen in paratonia. In conclusion, muscle tone disorders like spasticity, rigidity, dystonia, and paratonia each have distinct clinical presentations, underlying pathophysiological mechanisms, and aetiologies, requiring careful assessment and tailored, often multimodal, treatment approaches.
