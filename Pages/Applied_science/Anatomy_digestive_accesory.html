Welcome to the anatomy of the liver, biliary system and pancreas!

Together, they form the main accesory digestive organs next to the GI tract itself

(Retro)peritoneal structures

The peritoneum is a serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity. It supports the abdominal organs and serves as a conduit for their blood vesels, lymphatic vessels and nerves. Based on their position relative to this membrane, all abdominal organs are one of three things:

  1. Intraperitoneal: Stomach, jejenum, ileum, cecum, appendix, colon transversus, colon sigmoidium, spleen, ovaries, liver
  2. Retroperitoneal: Colon ascendens, colon descendens, kidneys, adrenal glands, renal vessels, aorta, vena cava inferior
  3. Infra-/subperitoneal: bladder, gonadal blood vessels, uterus, fallopian tubes

Exceptions are the pancreas which is retroperitoneal save for the tail, the duodenum which is retroperitoneal save for the first bit, the ureters which are retroperitoneal save for the most distal part which lies infraperitoneal and the rectum which lies intra-, then retro-, then infraperitoneal.

All intestines lie intraperitoneal, except for the vertical bits (ascendens, descendens and rectum)
The entire urinary tract lies outside of the peritoneum
Oocytes follow the same path as feces: intra- to infraperitoneal

The peritoneum then is divided into two main "sacs": the greater sac and the lesser sac, the latter of which is divided into the omentum minus (essentially a ligament coneecting the stomach and liver (lig. hepatogastricum), and the duodenum and liver (lig. hepatoduodenicum). Posteriorly to itself, it forms lesser sac (i.e., bursa omentalis). This lesser sac indeed is connected to the greater sac through the omental foramen (i.e., foramen of Winslow) and may commonly contain considerable amounts of fat. The omentum majus hangs down from the greater curvature of the stomach and loops down in front of the intestines before attaching to the colon transversus, essentially being draped in front like an apron, insulating the abdominal organs.

In terms of development, only the urinary, circulatory and digestive elements are primarily retroperitoneal. The duodenum, colon and pancreas are secondarily retroperitoneal meaning they start suspended in mesentary and later migrate during development.

The liver

It is located in the right upper quadrant, intra- or mesoperitoneally. It is the largest gland in the human body, and forms a part of the accesory digestion glands. It has two surfaces:

  1. Diaphragmatic surface: partially not covered by peritoneum and thus in direct contact with the diaphragm bare area of the liver
  2. Visceral surface: covered in peritoneum; follows shape of surrounding organs

A number of ligaments attach to the liver:

  1. Falciform ligament: attaches the anterior liver to the anterior wall. It contains the lig. teres (remnant of the umbilical vein)
  2. Coronary ligament: a broad ligament that attaches the liver to the diaphragm. It circumscribes the bare area of the liver
  3. Triangular ligaments: end points of the coronary ligament
  4. Omentum minus: as described above, forms a flat ligament between liver, stomach and duodenum

Clinically relevant is the space between the liver and the right kidney called Morison's pouch or recessus subhepatici which forms the deepest part of the abdomen when lying flat. In case of intra-abdominal fluid in a supine position, it may collect here. It is the equivalent of the pouch of Douglas, which is the deepest point when standing.

Hepatic circulation

Crucially, the liver is supplied through two ways:

  1. A. Hepatica (25%): Supplies blood to non-parenchymal structures
  2. V. Portalis hepatica (75%): Runs from the small intestine to the liver, carrying partially deoxygenated blood and allowing the liver to perform gut-related functions such as detoxification

Veinous drainage goes through the V. Hepatica inferior.

Lobes of the liver

The liver is covered in Glisson's capsule which separates a right and left lobe. The right lobe is significantly larger and contains two accesory lobes: caudate lobe and quadrate lobe. The latter are separate by a deep transverse fissure called the porta hepatis which is where all vessels, nerves and ducts enter and leave except the hepatic veins.

Couinaud made a classification of lobes, where the left lobe is separated in an upper and lower part (respectively Couinaud 2 and 3). The left lobe is separated from the right one by the lig. Falciforme and the first section of the right lobe is called Couinaud 4 which runs untill Cantlie's line. Posteriorly thereto lies Couinaud 1. The rest of the right lobe is then divided clockwise starting from the bottom left in Couinaud 5, 6, 7 and 8.

In this system, the quadrate lobe is comparable to the bottom of the medial segment, i.e., Couinaud 4b. The caudate lobe, Couinaud I, forms the roof of the porta hepalis.

Hepatic nervous system

The parenchym is supplied through the hepatic plexus consisting of two parts: the sympathetic coelic plexus and parasympathetic N. Vagus. Everything enters through the porta hepatis. Glisson's capsule covering the liver, is supplied by the Nn. Intercostales and distension creates a sharp, well localised pain.

The gallbladder

The cholecyst is a pear-shaped, hollow organ where bile is stored after it is produced in the liver. The largest point (the "bottom") is called the fundus, with the tip being referred to as the neck. The neck openes up into the ductus cysticus, right before which the wall of the gallbladder pouches out slightly, called Hartmann's pouch. The common hepatic duct (ductus hepaticus communis) arises from the left and right hepatic ducts which lie within the liver. Around the gallbladder the d. cysticus and d. hepaticus commuis converge to form the common bile duct (ductus choledochus). Right before exiting into the intestines, the d. choledochus converges with the ductus pancreaticus to form the ampulla hepatopancreaticus (Ampulla of Vater) which then opens up into the major duodenal papilla (Papilla of Oddi). The system of ducts described is called the biliary tree.

An anatomical variation does occur where the top of the fundus is pulled anteriorly, called the Phrygian cap, named after the similarly named historical cap. It has no medical implications.

The pancreas

The pancreas is divided into a head, neck, body and tail from right to left. This means the head lies in midline (in the curve of the duodenum) and the tail nearly touches the spleen. Furthermore, this means the pancreas lies retroperitoneally save for the tail which lies intraperitoneally with the spleen.

There are two ducts leading out of the pancreas: ductus accesorius (d. Santorini), and ductus pancreaticus (d. Wirsung). The ductus pancreaticus is controlled by the sphincter of Boyden before passing into the ductus choledochus.

Pancreatic circulation

The pancreas is supplied arterially through branches of the coeliac artery and superior mesenteric artery. Crucially, the coeliac artery branches into the splenic artery, which supplies the left side of the body and the entire tail, while the superior mesenteric branches in to the pancreaticuduodenal arteries which run along the posterior/anterior surfaces to supplied the head and body.

The coeliac artery becomes the splenic artery, supplying the tail. While the superior mesenteric artery supplies the rest.
