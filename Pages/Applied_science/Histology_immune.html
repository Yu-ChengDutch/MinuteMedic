Cells of the immune system

Just as with all lessons within the histology section, we are going to focus on the cells and not necessarily their functions within the larger system.

In general, the immune cells may be divided into two groups:

  1. Granulocytes: Mast cells, Basophils, Neutrophils and Eosinophils
  2. Agranulocytes: Monocytes and lymphocytes

Granulocytes

This distinction is made on the basis of so-called specific granules which are secretory vesicles found only in cells of these granulocytes. They store a mixture of cytotoxic molecules such as enzymes and antimicrobial peptides. The process of attacking other cells using these granules is called degranulation. Because these cells all have segmented or irregular nuclei, they are also called polymorphonuclear leukocytes. As they call come from the common myeloid progenitor, the process of granulopoiesis takes place in the bone marrow. Granulocytes have rather short life-span, namely only 2 days.

Neutrophils

Taking up about 60% of all granulocytes, the neutrophil is the most abundant of all granulocytes. They circulate in the blood until they receive a signal, after which they take around 30 minutes to reach the site of infection, do their job and turn into pus. The granules of the monocyte fulfill two functions, namely intra- and extracellular killing. As such, the monocyte has three methods of killing pathogens: 1) Phagocytosis with intracellular killing, 2) Degranulation and 3) so-called neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs). The latter refers to a network of chromatin and serine proteases which trap and kill microbes extracellularly. In terms of communication, the neutrophil may stimulate monocytes and macrophages to increase phagocytosis.

The neutrophil is the most abundant and has the most functions; it could as well be the neutral immune cell!

Eosinophils

The Eosinophil is specifically suited to kill parasites (such as enteric nematodes) through degranulation using their specific granules. While their phagocytosis capabilities are limited, they are professional antigen-presenting cells and hence regulate other immune cell functions.

The Eosinophil is built to kill (often enteric) parasites

Basophils

These cells are the least abundant of all the immune cells and mostly aid in the immune response by providing a histamine release upon injury in the site of infeciton. This causes dilation of the local capillaries.

Mast cells

Uniquely so amongst the granulocytes, the mast cell does not need to travel to the site of infection as it is present in tissue. Releasing histamine too, it is mostly involved in the regulation of host defense and allergic reactions.

Like a flagpole (a.k.a. mast) the mastcell stays in place and marks the site

Agranulocytes

These cells do not contain any granules and thus have to resort to other methods to fulfill their ultimate function: protect the body.

Monocytes

These cells, namely macrophages and myeloid dendritic cells also descend from the common myeloid progenitor. Their main function is phagocytosis, but they also present parts of the pathogen to T-cells which may use it either to build up memory or to mount an antibody response.

The macrophage does phagocytosis
The macrophage enlarges the immune response by presenting pathogens

lymphocytes

A much more complex group, the lymphocytes descend from the common lymphoid progenitor and can be split into three groups: 1) Natural Killer (NK) cells, 2) T-cells and 3) B-cells. The latter function to make antibodies which bind to pathogens, enabeling their destruction (called adaptive opsonisation). NK cells are then however unique in their capability of recognising and killing destressed cells without any form of externally applied marking. T-cells may be split into three types: Cytotoxic (CD8+), Helper (CD4+) or Memory.

The lymphocytes descend from the common lymphoid progenitor
Natural Killer cells may kill natural (i.e., unmarked) cells
B-cells make antibodies
CD4+ comes before (i.e., marks) CD8+ cells
