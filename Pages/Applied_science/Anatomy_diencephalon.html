Primer on neural development

The neural tube divides early on into:
1. Prosencephalon (Forebrain): The most anterior part, destined to become the cerebrum and diencephalon.
2. Mesencephalon (Midbrain): The middle section.
3. Rhombencephalon (Hindbrain): The most caudal section, which further develops into the pons, cerebellum, and medulla.

PM&R -> Prosencephalon, Mesencephalon and Rhombencephalon

Later in development, the Prosencephalon divides into the Telencephalon and the Diencephalon. The Telencephalon derivatives include the cerebral cortices, as well as the caudate, putamen, Globus pallidus, and ventral pallidum, which are components of the basal ganglia. The Diencephalon gives rise to the Thalamus, Subthalamus, Epithalamus, Hypothalamus, and the subthalamic nucleus; which is what we will talk about here.

A. Thalamus

Gross Anatomy: The thalamus is a large, paired mass of gray matter, approximately four centimeters long and ovoid, located superior to the midbrain near the center of the brain. It forms the dorsal part of the diencephalon and its medial surface constitutes the upper part of the lateral wall of the third ventricle. The two thalami are connected by a flattened gray band called the interthalamic adhesion. The gray matter of the thalamus is partitioned by a distinctive Y-shaped internal medullary lamina, which divides its nuclei into anterior, medial, and lateral groups. The lateral part, phylogenetically newer, is known as the neothalamus. The metathalamus is a subdivision made up of the lateral geniculate and medial geniculate nuclei.

Functional Anatomy: Often called the "relay station" of the brain, the thalamus's primary function is to relay sensory and motor signals to the cerebral cortex. It plays a crucial role in the regulation of consciousness, sleep, and alertness. Almost all thalamic neurons project to the cerebral cortex via thalamocortical radiations, facilitating extensive information exchange. The thalamus is functionally connected to the hippocampus, forming part of the extended hippocampal system at the thalamic anterior nuclei, which is crucial for human episodic event memory and differentiates recollective and familiarity memory. It is also a subcortical motor center, providing specific channels from the basal ganglia and cerebellum to cortical motor areas, and is involved in controlling eye movements like antisaccades.

Blood Supply: The thalamus receives its blood supply from branches of the posterior cerebral artery. These branches include the polar artery, paramedian thalamic-subthalamic arteries, inferolateral (thalamogeniculate) arteries, and posterior (medial and lateral) choroidal arteries. A rare anatomical variation, the artery of Percheron, supplies both parts of the thalamus through a single trunk.

B. Hypothalamus

Gross Anatomy: The hypothalamus is a small part of the vertebrate brain located below the thalamus, forming the basal part of the diencephalon. In humans, it is about the size of an almond. It is divided into four regions (preoptic, supraoptic, tuberal, mammillary) along the anterior-posterior axis and three zones (periventricular, intermediate, lateral) along the medial-lateral axis. It contains numerous nuclei, each with distinct functions.

Functional Anatomy: The hypothalamus serves as a vital link between the nervous system and the endocrine system via the pituitary gland, acting as a central neuroendocrine hub. It is a component of the limbic system, with connections to limbic structures like the amygdala and septum. Key functions and associated nuclei include:
- Hormone Release:
- -> Posterior Pituitary: Magnocellular neurosecretory cells in the paraventricular nucleus and supraoptic nucleus produce oxytocin and vasopressin.
- -> Anterior Pituitary: Parvocellular neurosecretory cells of the paraventricular nucleus release hormones (e.g., corticotropin-releasing hormone, thyrotropin-releasing hormone, somatostatin, dopamine, growth hormone-releasing hormone, gonadotropin-releasing hormone) into the hypophyseal portal system, regulating anterior pituitary secretions.
- Thermoregulation: Preoptic and anterior hypothalamic nuclei.
- Sleep and Arousal: Ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (sleep) and paraventricular/tuberomammillary nuclei (arousal/wakefulness).
- Circadian Rhythms: Suprachiasmatic nucleus.
- Appetite and Satiety: Arcuate nucleus (feeding, GHRH, prolactin inhibition) and ventromedial nucleus (satiety). The extreme lateral part of the ventromedial nucleus controls food intake; stimulation increases it, while bilateral lesion causes cessation. Medial parts of the nucleus control the lateral part, with bilateral lesion leading to hyperphagia and obesity.
- Memory: Mammillary nuclei.
- Autonomic Functions: Regulation of blood pressure, heart rate, GI stimulation (dorsomedial nucleus), pupillary dilation, shivering (posterior nucleus), and sympathetic/parasympathetic responses.

The hypothalamus responds strongly to steroids and glucocorticoids, and contains glucose-sensitive and thermosensitive neurons. It mediates oxytocin and vasopressin secretion in response to physiological stimuli like suckling and cardiovascular changes. The hypothalamus also receives inputs from brainstem nuclei (solitary tract, locus coeruleus) and circumventricular organs (SFO, OVLT) that lack an effective blood-brain barrier, allowing it to sample blood composition and react to hormones like angiotensin and interleukins.

Blood Supply: While specific arteries for the hypothalamus's direct supply are not detailed in the provided sources, its close proximity to the thalamus and pituitary suggests shared vascularization with branches of the posterior cerebral artery. The presence of circumventricular organs (like the SFO and OVLT) with fenestrated capillaries highlights specialized areas for blood sampling, critical for its endocrine functions.

C. Epithalamus

Gross Anatomy: The epithalamus is the posterior (dorsal) segment of the diencephalon. It includes several structures:
- Pineal gland: A gland that secretes melatonin.
- Habenular nuclei (Habenula): Involved in connecting the limbic system.
- Stria medullaris thalami: A white matter tract.
- Anterior and posterior paraventricular nuclei.
- Posterior commissure.

Functional Anatomy: The primary function of the epithalamus is to connect the limbic system to other parts of the brain. It is also a key component of the dorsal diencephalic conduction system, which relays information from the limbic forebrain to limbic midbrain structures. The pineal gland within the epithalamus is responsible for the secretion of melatonin, which is crucial for regulating circadian rhythms like sleep-wake cycles. This secretion is influenced by the suprachiasmatic nucleus in the hypothalamus. The epithalamus also contributes to the regulation of motor pathways and emotions, and the conservation of energy in the body.

Blood Supply: The sources do not specifically detail the blood supply to the epithalamus, but as part of the diencephalon, it would generally share arterial supply originating from the posterior cerebral artery system.

D. Subthalamus

Gross Anatomy: The subthalamus, also known as the ventral thalamus, is a part of the diencephalon located ventral (below) to the thalamus, medial to the internal capsule, and lateral to the hypothalamus. Its most prominent structure is the subthalamic nucleus (STN). Other associated structures include the zona incerta, subthalamic fasciculus, and the Fields of Forel (H1 and H2).

Functional Anatomy: The subthalamic nucleus (STN) is crucial in motor control. Its neurons primarily contain glutamate, and they exert excitatory effects on neurons of the globus pallidus and substantia nigra. The subthalamus has reciprocal connections, sending efferent (output) fibers to the striatum (caudate nucleus and putamen), the dorsal thalamus, the red nucleus, and the substantia nigra. It receives afferent (input) connections from the substantia nigra and striatum. This intricate circuitry makes the subthalamus a critical component of the basal ganglia system, particularly in the indirect pathway of motor control.

Blood Supply: The sources do not explicitly detail the blood supply to the subthalamus, but paramedian thalamic-subthalamic arteries (branches of the posterior cerebral artery) are mentioned as supplying both the thalamus and subthalamus.
