Neurons

Just like any cell, neurons contain a soma with a nucleus etc. However, what makes a neuron special are its dendrites, and the axon ending in telodendria with synaptic terminals. The neuron then, is surrounded by a plasma membrane which, being a lipid bilayer, is a powerful electrical insulator. The structure of the cell then, is supported by neurofilaments and neurotubules which are assembled into larger neurofibrils. Certain nerve cells are coloured, notably the substantia nigra, using lipofuscin and neuromelanin which is a byproduct of the synthesis of catecholamines. So-called Nissl bodies are microscopic clumps of rough endoplasmic reticulum and ribosomal RNA. They show the metabolic activity of nerves.

Nerves may be classified by their endings:

  1. Unipolar: no dendrites, one axon
  2. Bipolar: one dendrite, one axon
  3. Multipolar: multiple dendrites, one axon
  4. Pseudounipolar: one process functioning as both

The multipolar cells can then be split into two categories:

  1. Golgi I: Neurons with long-projecting, axonal processes
  2. Golgi II: Neurons whose axonal process project locally

Nerve classification

The unipolar nerves are exclusively found as sensory neurons. Then in terms of multipolar neurons, the Golgi I are located in the grey matter, and send long axons to the spinal cord (e.g., pyramidal cells). The Golgi II cells then, are connected only locally (e.g., cerebral / cerebellar cortex).

Nerve fibres

Axons or nerve fibers are usually surrounded by myelin produced by a Schwann cell that surrounds the axon. Around this myelin sheath then, the nerve forms a protective neurilemma. Between myelinated areas, unmyelinated areas called nodes of Ranvier exist.

Nerve fibers may then be classed into a few major groups. Firstly, motor neurons:

  1. Alpha motor neurons: large, myelinated, fast
  2. Gamma motor neuron: medium, myelinated, medium speed

The alpha motor neurons lead to all contracting skeletal muscles, while the gamma motor neurons are essentially focussed on muscle spindles (i.e., intrafusal muscle fibres) and thus serve to detect stretch.

The sensory receptors are more complex:

  1. Type Ia/A-alpha: large, myelinated, fast
  2. Type Ib/A-alpha: large, myelinated, fast
  3. Type II/A-beta: medium, myelinated, medium speed
  4. Type III/A-delta: small, unmyelinated, slow
  5. Type IV/C: very small, unmyelinated, very slow

Type Ia leads to the muscle spindles, type Ib to the Golgi tendon organ detecting muscle stretch through the tendon. Type II leads to muscle spindles as well as cutaneous mechnoreceptors. This makes nerve fibres type I and II proprioceptive fibres. Type III then, leads to free nerve endings of touch and pressure and nociceptors of lateral spinothalamic tract as well as the cold thermoreceptors. Type IV then leads to nociceptors of anterior spinothalamic tract and warmth receptors.

1-2, 2-3, 3-4 -> Proprioceptors, Mechanoreceptors, Noci-/thermoreceptors
The fourth type provides warmth!
All but the nociceptors are myelinated
